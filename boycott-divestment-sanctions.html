Isolation campaign / anti-normalization / institutional pressure

Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions (BDS)

The movement turns contact with Israel into complicity.

DEFINITION

BDS is short for Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions. It is a Palestinian-led campaign launched on July 9, 2005, to isolate Israel through economic, academic, cultural, and diplomatic pressure. The official call says Israel should be boycotted, divested from, and sanctioned until it complies with international law and Palestinian rights. Its demands sound procedural. Their cumulative effect is not procedural. They challenge the continued legitimacy of Jewish sovereignty.

The campaign presents itself as modeled on the South African anti-apartheid boycott. That comparison is central to its appeal: it places Israel inside one of the clearest twentieth-century stories of international pressure and liberation. The analogy also does much of the work. If Israel is South Africa, then boycott becomes not one tactic among many but a test of public decency.

The decisive demand is the refugee return demand. Applied literally, it would move several million Palestinian refugees and descendants into Israel proper. That would not reform Israel as a Jewish state; it would end it as one. BDS supporters often describe this as a rights-based position. Its practical result is a one-state outcome, not a two-state settlement.

That point is not only an inference by critics. Omar Barghouti, a co-founder and leading advocate of BDS, rejected the two-state solution in a 2004 article, writing that it was dead and needed an official death certificate. The movement’s pressure campaign therefore does not stop cleanly at ending the post-1967 occupation.

COMMON USAGE

On campuses, BDS operates through student-government resolutions, divestment campaigns, faculty petitions, union votes, speaker disruptions, encampment demands, and pressure on university investment committees. It is often promoted by Students for Justice in Palestine chapters, Faculty for Justice in Palestine chapters, Jewish Voice for Peace, and allied coalitions. Its campus language is simple: universities must not invest in, cooperate with, or normalize Israel.

Academic boycott is one of its most consequential forms. PACBI, the Palestinian Campaign for the Academic and Cultural Boycott of Israel, calls for boycotting Israeli academic institutions. The boycott is institutional in theory, but it often reaches individual scholars in practice through cancelled talks, rejected partnerships, conference exclusions, and pressure against exchange programs. The result is not debate with Israelis. It is enforced distance from them.

Corporate campaigns have targeted companies accused of supporting Israel’s military, security, energy, or technology sectors. Cultural campaigns pressure musicians, filmmakers, artists, and athletes not to appear in Israel or cooperate with Israeli institutions. Anti-normalization campaigns oppose joint Israeli-Palestinian projects unless they adopt BDS premises. In each domain, ordinary contact with Israel is treated as complicity.

After October 7, BDS language became more visible in encampment demands and divestment resolutions. UCLA is an important example. In February 2024, both its undergraduate and graduate student-government bodies passed resolutions calling for divestment from Israel. Litigation later followed the 2024 encampment environment. In July 2025, UCLA agreed to pay more than $6 million to settle claims brought by Jewish students and a faculty member; the Justice Department also found that UCLA violated Title VI and the Equal Protection Clause by acting with deliberate indifference toward a hostile environment for Jewish and Israeli students. In May 2026, DOJ filed a separate lawsuit alleging that UCLA tolerated an antisemitic educational environment.

IMPLIED MEANING

BDS implies that Israel is uniquely unfit for ordinary relations. Trade, research, culture, sport, institutional partnership, and study abroad are treated as contamination unless Israel first satisfies the movement’s demands. The campaign is not just a protest against a government. It asks institutions and individuals to sever ordinary contact with the world’s only Jewish-majority state.

The selectivity is part of the argument. States with records equal to or worse than Israel’s do not face a comparable global boycott campaign with the same university infrastructure, professional-association pressure, cultural enforcement, and test of personal purity. China, Russia, Syria, Iran, and other states are criticized, sanctioned, protested, or condemned in various ways. They are not usually turned into a standing test of whether one may belong inside progressive institutions.

Anti-normalization makes the implied meaning explicit. The BDS Movement’s own anti-normalization guidelines define normalization as participation in joint projects between Arabs, including Palestinians, and Israelis when those projects present both sides as equally situated or seek reconciliation without adopting the movement’s account of root causes. This means that dialogue, coexistence work, shared research, and many civic exchanges become suspect precisely because they treat Israelis as legitimate partners.

For many Jews in the diaspora, BDS therefore functions as a demand for disavowal. A Jewish student, scholar, artist, or professional who maintains a relationship with Israel can be asked to renounce that relationship before being accepted as a full participant in campus or cultural life. The demand is not only “oppose this Israeli policy.” It becomes “separate yourself from Jewish collective sovereignty.”

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

BDS was launched in 2005, but boycott pressure against Jewish national life in Palestine predates the state. On December 2, 1945, the Arab League formally adopted a boycott of Jewish goods and services in Mandatory Palestine. The declaration treated Jewish products as undesirable because their production might help realize Zionist aims. The object at that point was not the policy of a state. No Jewish state existed. The object was the Jewish national project itself.

After Israel’s establishment, the Arab League boycott developed primary, secondary, and tertiary layers. The primary boycott targeted trade with Israel. The secondary boycott targeted companies doing business with Israel. The tertiary boycott targeted companies doing business with those companies. Congressional Research Service reporting describes this system as a long-running Arab League mechanism whose enforcement has varied by state and period.

The modern BDS vocabulary took shape through the 2001 Durban NGO Forum. That forum described Israel as a racist, apartheid state and called for isolation and sanctions. PACBI was founded in Ramallah in 2004, and the broader BDS call followed on July 9, 2005, the first anniversary of the International Court of Justice advisory opinion on Israel’s West Bank barrier. The movement adapted the older Arab boycott and the Durban apartheid vocabulary to Western civil society, universities, unions, churches, and cultural institutions.

German domestic-intelligence reporting in 2024 treated BDS as a suspected extremist case and noted links to secular Palestinian extremism; media summaries of that report also stated that BDS was supported by organizations including Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad. That claim should be used with precision. BDS is not a single party with formal membership in the usual sense. It is a coalition campaign with loose affiliations, supporters, and aligned organizations. The stronger point is narrower: some European security authorities have concluded that parts of the BDS environment overlap with extremist anti-Israel networks.

FUNCTION

BDS functions through isolation. It seeks to make Israel costly to engage, costly to defend, and eventually costly to recognize as a normal state. Economic boycotts target commerce. Academic boycotts target research life. Cultural boycotts target ordinary human exchange. Campus divestment campaigns teach students to treat Israel as the exception among nations. The method is social pressure with a moral vocabulary.

The campaign also changes the boundary of acceptable Jewish identity. Jews who reject Israel are elevated as proof of anti-racist virtue. Jews who support Israel’s existence are placed under suspicion. This is why BDS is not experienced by many Jewish students as an abstract foreign-policy debate. It appears as a social rule: denounce Israel, or risk exclusion.

Anti-normalization gives the movement its disciplining power. If ordinary cooperation with Israelis is forbidden, then the people most committed to coexistence become suspect. A peace program, exchange seminar, documentary collaboration, or medical research partnership can be condemned because it keeps Israelis inside the circle of legitimate human contact. That logic does not prepare the ground for compromise. It punishes the relationships through which compromise would have to occur.

The legal response in the United States reflects this conflict. As of the most recent legislation trackers, 38 states had some anti-BDS law or executive order in effect. These measures vary widely. Some concern state contracts; others concern state investments. Civil-liberties groups argue that many of these laws burden protected boycotts and advocacy. Supporters argue that the laws address discriminatory commercial conduct against Israel and, in some cases, Jews. The legal picture is mixed and should not be overstated.

NOTES

1. The official BDS call is the primary source for the movement’s stated demands. It calls for boycott, divestment, and sanctions until Israel meets three demands: ending occupation and colonization of Arab lands occupied in 1967 and dismantling the wall; full equality for Arab-Palestinian citizens of Israel; and refugee return under UN Resolution 194.

2. The right-of-return demand is the central issue. BDS can describe the demand as a universal-rights claim, but applying it to millions of descendants inside Israel proper would remove the demographic basis of Jewish self-determination. That is an inference from the demand’s practical effect, not a quotation from the BDS call.

3. Barghouti's 2004 Electronic Intifada article is the cleanest source for this entry. It rejects the two-state solution and argues that it needed an official death certificate. That source is sufficient for the narrower point made here: the movement's pressure campaign does not end neatly at post-1967 withdrawal.

4. The Arab League boycott is stronger as lineage than as identity. BDS does not simply the Arab League boycott renamed. The better claim is that BDS inherits and updates an older boycott logic: Jewish national life in Palestine is treated as something to be isolated through outside pressure.

5. The UCLA material should be presented as litigation and government findings, not as a final universal verdict on every campus protest. The settlement resolved claims brought by Jewish students and a faculty member. The DOJ finding and later lawsuit are official government actions. UCLA has disputed some allegations and stated that it has taken steps to combat antisemitism.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

It turns coexistence into complicity.

BDS does not stop at criticism of Israeli policy. Through anti-normalization, it treats many forms of Israeli-Palestinian contact as suspect unless they already accept the campaign’s premises. That makes peace work, shared research, and civic cooperation harder, not easier.

It hides a one-state demand inside rights language.

The official demands sound modular: occupation, equality, refugee return. Taken together, they do not point toward two states. The refugee demand, if applied literally inside Israel proper, would end Israel as a Jewish state. BDS can call that justice. It should not be allowed to call it merely reform.

It pressures Jews to perform disavowal.

In campus and cultural settings, BDS often asks Jews to prove their acceptability by separating themselves from Israel. That demand does not fall on other groups in the same way. It turns Jewish connection to Jewish sovereignty into a social liability.

It applies a special rule to the Jewish state.

Many states commit abuses. Many states deserve criticism, sanctions, or diplomatic pressure. BDS is different because it seeks a standing boycott of Israel across economic, academic, cultural, and civic life. The result is not ordinary criticism. It is a campaign to make one state uniquely untouchable.
