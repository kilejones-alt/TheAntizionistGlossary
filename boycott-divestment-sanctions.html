Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions (BDS)

Definition

BDS is short for Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions. It is a Palestinian-led campaign launched on July 9, 2005, to isolate Israel through economic, academic, cultural, and civic pressure. The official call says Israel should be “boycotted, divested from, and sanctioned until it complies with international law and Palestinian rights.” The demands sound procedural. Their cumulative effect is not. The project presents itself as modeled on the South African anti-apartheid boycott, and that comparison does most of the work. It places Israel inside one of the clearest twentieth-century stories of outside pressure and liberation. If Israel is South Africa, then boycott stops being one tactic among many and becomes a test for whether or not you are moral or not. BDS claims to agree with a two-state solution. Omar Barghouti, a co-founder and leading advocate of BDS, rejected the two-state solution, writing that it was dead and needed an official death certificate.

Common Usage

On campuses, BDS works through student-government resolutions, divestment drives, faculty petitions, union votes, speaker disruptions, encampment demands, and pressure on university investment committees. It is carried by Students for Justice in Palestine chapters, Faculty for Justice in Palestine chapters, Jewish Voice for Peace, and allied coalitions. The campus language is simple: universities must not invest in, cooperate with, or normalize Israel. Academic boycott is one of its sharpest forms. PACBI, the Palestinian Campaign for the Academic and Cultural Boycott of Israel, calls for boycotting Israeli academic institutions. The boycott is institutional in theory, but in practice it reaches individual scholars through cancelled talks, rejected partnerships, conference exclusions, and pressure against exchange programs. The result is not debate with Israelis: it is enforced distance from them. Corporate campaigns target companies accused of supporting Israel’s military, security, energy, or technology sectors. Cultural campaigns pressure musicians, filmmakers, artists, and athletes not to appear in Israel or work with Israeli institutions. Anti-normalization campaigns oppose joint Israeli-Palestinian projects unless they accept BDS premises. In each domain, ordinary contact with Israel is treated as inherently problematic. After October 7, BDS language became more visible in encampment demands and divestment resolutions. UCLA is the cleanest example. In February 2024, both its undergraduate and graduate student-government bodies passed resolutions calling for divestment from Israel. Litigation followed. In July 2025, UCLA agreed to pay more than 6 million dollars to settle claims brought by Jewish students and a faculty member; the Justice Department also found that UCLA had violated Title VI and the Equal Protection Clause by acting with deliberate indifference toward a hostile environment for Jewish and Israeli students. In May 2026, DOJ filed a separate lawsuit alleging that UCLA tolerated an antisemitic educational environment.

Implied Meaning

BDS implies that Israel is uniquely unfit for ordinary relations. Trade, research, culture, sport, partnership, and study abroad are treated as problematic unless Israel first meets the campaign’s demands. The project is not a protest against a government: it asks institutions and individuals to sever ordinary contact with the world’s only Jewish-majority state. This selectivity is part of the argument. States with records equal to or worse than Israel’s do not face a comparable global boycott with the same campus apparatus, professional-association pressure, cultural enforcement, and test of personal purity. China, Russia, Syria, Iran, and others are criticized, sanctioned, and sometimes protested. None of them is turned into a standing test of whether it belongs inside progressive institutions. Anti-normalization makes the implication plain. The BDS Movement’s own anti-normalization guidelines define “normalization” as participation in joint projects between Arabs, including Palestinians, and Israelis when those projects present both sides as equally situated. Or if they pursue reconciliation without adopting the movement’s account of root causes. Dialogue, coexistence work, shared research, and many civic exchanges become suspicious precisely because they treat Israelis as legitimate partners. For many Jews in the diaspora, BDS reads as a demand for disavowal. A Jewish student, scholar, artist, or professional with a tie to Israel can be asked to renounce that tie before being accepted as a full participant in campus or cultural life. The demand is not “oppose this Israeli policy.” It is “separate yourself from Jewish collective history” or fear being demonized on campus.

Origin / Lineage

BDS launched in 2005, but boycott pressure against Jewish national life in Palestine predates the state. On December 2, 1945, the Arab League formally adopted a boycott of Jewish goods and services in Mandatory Palestine. The declaration treated Jewish products as undesirable because their production might help realize Zionist aims. The object at that point was not the policy of a state. No Jewish state existed. The object was the idea of the Jewish national project. After 1948, the Arab League boycott developed primary, secondary, and tertiary layers. The primary boycott targeted trade with Israel. The secondary boycott targeted companies doing business with Israel. The tertiary boycott targeted companies doing business with those companies. Congressional Research Service reporting describes the system as a “long-running Arab League mechanism whose enforcement has varied by state and period.” The modern BDS vocabulary took shape through the 2001 Durban NGO Forum, which branded Israel a racist, apartheid state and called for isolation and sanctions. PACBI was founded in Ramallah in 2004, and the broader BDS call followed on July 9, 2005, the first anniversary of the ICJ advisory opinion on Israel’s West Bank barrier. The campaign adapted the older Arab boycott and the Durban vocabulary to civil society, universities, unions, churches, and cultural sites. German domestic-intelligence reporting in 2024 treated BDS as a suspected extremist case and noted overlaps with secular Palestinian extremism. That finding should be used with precision. BDS is not a single party with formal membership in the ordinary sense. It is a coalition campaign with loose affiliations, supporters, and aligned organizations. The narrower point is what matters: parts of the BDS environment overlap with extremist anti-Israel networks, as European security authorities have now stated on the record.

Function

BDS works through isolation. It tries to make Israel costly to engage, costly to defend, and eventually costly to recognize as a normal state. Economic boycotts target commerce. Academic boycotts target research life. Cultural boycotts target ordinary human exchange. Campus divestment teaches students to treat Israel as the exception among nations. The method is social pressure with a moral and political vocabulary. The campaign also redraws the boundary of Jewish identity. Jews who reject Israel are elevated as proof of their anti-racist virtue. Jews who support Israel’s existence are placed under suspicion and ostracized or made to feel shame. That is why BDS is not experienced by many Jewish students as an abstract foreign-policy argument. It arrives as a social rule: denounce Israel, or risk being ostracized. “Anti-normalization” gives the campaign its disciplining power. If ordinary cooperation with Israelis is forbidden, the people most committed to coexistence become suspect. A peace program, an exchange seminar, a documentary collaboration, a medical research partnership can be condemned for keeping Israelis as peers or coworkers. The legal response in the United States reflects this conflict. As of the most recent legislation trackers, 38 states had some anti-BDS law or executive order in effect. Supporters of these laws argue they address discriminatory commercial conduct against Israel and, in some cases, against Jews.

Notes

  1. The official BDS call lists three demands: 1) ending occupation and colonization of Arab lands taken in 1967 and dismantling the wall, 2) full equality for Arab-Palestinian citizens of Israel; 3) refugee return under UN Resolution 194.
  2. Barghouti’s 2004 article shows how the founder himself does not believe in a two-state solution.
  3. BDS inherits and updates an older boycott logic: Jewish national life in Palestine something to be isolated through outside pressure.

Responses / Criticisms

It turns coexistence into complicity. BDS does not stop at criticism of Israeli policy. Through anti-normalization, it treats many forms of Israeli-Palestinian contact as done with mens rea. That makes peacework, shared research, and civic cooperation harder, not easier. It hides a one-state demand inside rights language. The official demands sound reasonable: stop occupation, equality, refugee return. Taken together, they do not point toward two states. The refugee demand, applied literally inside Israel, would end Israel as a Jewish state. BDS can call that justice. It’s not merely reform. It pressures Jews to perform disavowal. In campus and cultural settings, BDS often asks Jews to prove their acceptability by separating themselves from Israel. That demand does not fall on other groups the same way. It turns Jewish connection to Jewish sovereignty into a social liability. It applies a special rule to the Jewish state. Many states commit abuses. Many deserve criticism, sanctions, or pressure. BDS is different because it seeks a standing boycott of Israel across economic, academic, cultural, and civic life. The result is not ordinary criticism. It is a campaign to make one state uniquely untouchable. BDS can be used as one of the best ways to determine double standards: if you boycott the only Jewish state in the world, but do not have an issue with Saudi Arabia, Iran, China, and Russia, the problem is a glaring and open inconsistency.
