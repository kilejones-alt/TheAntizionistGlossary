Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions (BDS)

The movement turns contact with Israel into complicity.

Definition

Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions—BDS—is a Palestinian-led campaign launched on July 9, 2005. It seeks to isolate Israel through economic, academic, cultural, and diplomatic pressure until Israel meets its demands. The demands are written as procedure, but their effect is not procedural. Together they challenge Israel's right to exist as a Jewish state.

BDS models itself on the boycott of apartheid South Africa, and the comparison does much of its work. Set beside South Africa, Israel becomes a state to be pressured, isolated, and finally undone, and the boycott becomes a test of basic decency rather than one tactic among many.

Its central demand is the return of refugees. Applied literally, it would move several million Palestinian refugees and their descendants into Israel proper. That would not reform the Jewish state. It would end it. Supporters call this a matter of rights; in practice it points to one state, not two.

This is not only what critics say. Omar Barghouti, a co-founder of BDS, declared the two-state solution dead in a 2004 essay. The campaign does not stop at the 1967 borders, and it was never meant to.

Common Usage

On campus, BDS runs through the ordinary tools of university life: student-government resolutions, divestment drives, faculty petitions, union votes, disrupted talks, and pressure on investment committees. Groups such as Students for Justice in Palestine, Faculty for Justice in Palestine, and Jewish Voice for Peace carry it forward. The message to the university is simple: do not invest in Israel, do not work with it, do not treat it as a normal country.

The academic boycott is the most serious form. PACBI, the Palestinian Campaign for the Academic and Cultural Boycott of Israel, targets Israeli institutions in theory but reaches individual scholars in practice—through cancelled talks, refused partnerships, and closed conferences. Other campaigns target companies tied to Israel's economy and press artists and athletes to stay away. Anti-normalization goes further, opposing even joint Israeli-Palestinian projects unless they accept BDS terms first.

After October 7, 2023, the language spread quickly through campus encampments and divestment votes. UCLA is the clearest case. In February 2024 its undergraduate and graduate student governments both passed divestment resolutions. In July 2025 the university paid more than six million dollars to settle claims by Jewish students and a faculty member, and the Justice Department found that UCLA had violated Title VI by meeting a hostile environment for Jewish and Israeli students with deliberate indifference. In May 2026 the Department sued UCLA again on similar grounds.

Implied Meaning

BDS treats Israel, alone among nations, as unfit for normal relations. Trade, research, art, sport, and study abroad all become off-limits until Israel meets its conditions. This is more than protest against a government. It asks people and institutions to cut every ordinary tie to the world's only Jewish-majority state.

The selectivity is the argument. States with records as bad or worse face no comparable campaign—no global network of chapters, association votes, and purity tests. China, Russia, Syria, and Iran draw criticism and sanctions, but none becomes the test of whether a person belongs in respectable institutions.

Anti-normalization makes this plain. BDS guidelines treat any joint Arab-Israeli project as suspect if it presents the two sides as equals or seeks reconciliation without first accepting the BDS account of the conflict. Dialogue, coexistence work, and shared research all fall under suspicion, precisely because they treat Israelis as legitimate partners.

For many Jews abroad, then, BDS is a demand to disavow. A Jewish student, scholar, or professional with any tie to Israel may be asked to renounce it as the price of acceptance. The demand is no longer to oppose a policy. It is to break with the Jewish state itself.

Origin / Lineage

BDS launched in 2005, but boycotts of Jewish life in Palestine are older than Israel itself. On December 2, 1945, the Arab League adopted a boycott of Jewish goods in Mandatory Palestine, treating Jewish products as a threat because they might advance Zionist aims. There was no Jewish state yet. The target was the Jewish national project itself.

After 1948 the Arab League boycott grew in layers—first trade with Israel, then companies dealing with Israel, then companies dealing with those companies. The Congressional Research Service describes a long-running system whose enforcement rose and fell over time.

The modern version took shape at the 2001 Durban NGO Forum, which called Israel a racist, apartheid state and demanded its isolation. PACBI followed in Ramallah in 2004, and the full BDS call came on July 9, 2005. The campaign joined the old Arab boycott to the Durban apartheid charge and translated both into the language of Western universities, unions, and churches.

In 2024, German domestic intelligence treated BDS as a suspected extremist case and noted its overlap with secular Palestinian extremism. The point is not that every supporter is an extremist—BDS is a loose coalition, not a membership organization—but that a Western security service looked at its associations and opened a file.

Function

BDS works by wearing Israel down. It aims to make Israel costly to deal with, costly to defend, and finally hard to treat as a normal state. Economic boycotts hit commerce, academic boycotts hit research, cultural boycotts hit ordinary exchange, and campus divestment teaches students to treat one country as the world's exception. The method is social pressure in moral language.

It also sets the terms of acceptable Jewish identity. Jews who reject Israel are praised as proof of virtue; Jews who defend its existence are viewed with suspicion. This is why many Jewish students meet BDS not as distant foreign policy but as a simple rule: denounce Israel, or be excluded.

Anti-normalization enforces it. If working with Israelis is forbidden, then the people most committed to coexistence are the first to be doubted, and shared projects are condemned for keeping Israelis inside normal life. This does not build toward compromise. It destroys the relationships compromise would need.

The American response reflects the conflict. By recent counts, thirty-eight states have some anti-BDS law or order—some covering public contracts, others public investment. Civil-liberties groups say these laws burden protected speech; supporters say they answer discrimination against Israel, and at times against Jews. On the evidence of how the boycott works, the supporters have the better case.

Notes

  1. The official BDS call is the primary source for its aims. It demands boycott, divestment, and sanctions until Israel meets three conditions: end the occupation of lands taken in 1967 and remove the barrier; grant full equality to Arab citizens of Israel; and allow refugee return under UN Resolution 194.
  2. The refugee-return demand is the decisive one. Supporters may frame it as a universal right, but applied to several million descendants inside Israel proper, it would erase the Jewish majority that makes Jewish self-rule possible. This follows from the demand's effect, not from the wording of the call.
  3. Barghouti's 2004 essay in The Electronic Intifada is the cleanest source here. It rejects the two-state solution outright—enough to show that the campaign does not stop at a 1967 withdrawal.
  4. The Arab League boycott is better understood as ancestry than identity. BDS is not that boycott renamed. It inherits and updates an older logic: treat Jewish national life in Palestine as something to isolate from outside.
  5. Treat the UCLA material as litigation and government findings, not a final verdict on every protest. The settlement resolved specific claims; the Justice Department's finding and suit are official actions; UCLA has disputed some claims and described steps against antisemitism.

Responses / Criticisms

It turns coexistence into complicity.

BDS does not stop at criticizing Israeli policy. Through anti-normalization, it treats ordinary Israeli-Palestinian contact as suspect unless it first accepts BDS terms. This makes peace work, shared research, and cooperation harder, and it damages the very efforts a settlement would require.

It hides a one-state demand inside the language of rights.

The three conditions look modular: occupation, equality, return. Together they do not lead to two states. Refugee return, applied inside Israel proper, would end the Jewish state. Supporters may call that justice. They should not call it reform.

It asks Jews to disavow Israel.

On campus and in the arts, BDS often asks Jews to prove themselves acceptable by cutting ties to Israel. No other group faces this test. A people's link to its own state becomes a personal liability.

It applies one rule to the Jewish state alone.

Many states commit abuses and deserve criticism and pressure. BDS is unusual in seeking a standing boycott of one country across its whole economic, academic, and cultural life. That is not ordinary criticism. It is an effort to make a single nation untouchable.
