From the River to the Sea

The slogan names the whole land and leaves no room for the state of Israel.

DEFINITION

“From the River to the Sea” is a slogan that refers to the land between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea. In antizionist use, it usually appears as “From the river to the sea, Palestine will be free.” The words sound geographic. Their force is political. The slogan names the whole space in which Israel exists and imagines it under a different sovereign order.

Some supporters define the phrase as a call for equal rights, democratic coexistence, or freedom from Israeli rule. In antizionist usage, however, the phrase often does more than that. It names a future in which Jewish sovereignty disappears from the whole map. The slogan is therefore not only about Gaza, settlements, or the West Bank. Without a limiting explanation, it applies to Tel Aviv and Haifa as much as Hebron and Gaza City.

The problem begins with geography. Israel exists inside the territory named by the chant. If “Palestine” is to be free from the river to the sea as a single sovereign outcome, the Jewish state cannot continue in its present form. That is why many Jews hear the slogan not as a demand for rights, but as a demand for replacement.

COMMON USAGE

After the Hamas attacks of October 7, 2023, the slogan became one of the most visible phrases in anti-Israel protest culture. It appeared at street demonstrations, campus encampments, online campaigns, and public debates over antisemitism. Its use also entered government proceedings. In November 2023, the United States House of Representatives censured Representative Rashida Tlaib in part after her use and defense of the phrase. In April 2024, the House passed H. Res. 883, declaring that the slogan “from the river to the sea, Palestine will be free” is antisemitic and must be condemned.

Jewish organizations have treated the slogan as a denial of Jewish self-determination. The American Jewish Committee’s 2024 survey reported that 64 percent of American Jews considered the statement antisemitic, while 35 percent did not. The Anti-Defamation League describes the chant as a call for a Palestinian state from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea, a result that would dismantle the Jewish state.

Other institutions have taken a narrower approach. Meta’s Oversight Board ruled in September 2024 that three Facebook posts using the phrase did not violate Meta’s rules because the posts showed solidarity with Palestinians and did not call for violence, exclusion, or praise for Hamas. That decision did not settle the meaning of the slogan. It showed that platform enforcement and public meaning are not the same question.

By 2026, the phrase had moved from protest dispute into statutory law in Queensland, Australia. The Fighting Antisemitism and Keeping Guns out of the Hands of Terrorists and Criminals Amendment Act 2026 inserted a prohibited-expression offence into the Queensland Criminal Code. The statute names “from the river to the sea” and “globalise the intifada” as prohibited expressions when publicly recited, distributed, published, or displayed in a way reasonably expected to make a member of the public feel menaced, harassed, or offended, subject to listed reasonable-excuse provisions.

IMPLIED MEANING

The slogan’s central move is territorial compression. It removes the line between Israel within its internationally recognized pre-1967 armistice boundaries and the territories Israel captured in 1967. Once that line disappears, the demand no longer stops at ending military rule in the West Bank or altering Gaza policy. It reaches the whole state.

A speaker may intend a single democratic state with equal rights. But even that version eliminates Jewish national sovereignty. It asks Jews to accept that their collective self-determination is uniquely illegitimate in the one place where it was restored after centuries of exile, persecution, and genocide. The slogan’s softest reading still dissolves Israel as a Jewish state.

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

The exact origin of the English slogan is disputed. The territorial demand behind it is not. The Palestine Liberation Organization’s 1964 National Charter described Palestine, within the boundaries of the British Mandate, as an indivisible territorial unit. That language appeared before Israel controlled the West Bank or Gaza. The later 1968 Palestinian National Covenant rejected the 1947 partition and the establishment of Israel. The demand therefore cannot be explained only as a response to the post-1967 occupation.

Hamas carried the territorial claim in Islamist form. Its 1988 covenant says the movement strives to raise the banner of Allah over every inch of Palestine. Its 2017 document defines Palestine as extending from the River Jordan in the east to the Mediterranean Sea in the west, rejects any alternative to the full liberation of Palestine from the river to the sea, and states that the establishment of Israel does not create rights for the Zionist entity. The document also mentions a Palestinian state on the 1967 lines as a formula of national consensus, but it does so without recognizing Israel.

The modern English chant gives this older territorial maximalism a softer public sound. It turns a map-wide claim into a short liberation phrase. That is its power. It can carry the lineage of rejection while sounding, to many listeners, like ordinary solidarity.

FUNCTION

The slogan’s function is territorial replacement. It takes the map between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea and names it Palestine. Israel disappears from the sentence. That disappearance is not incidental. It is the work the slogan performs.

It also works as a loyalty test. In protest settings, refusing to chant it can mark a person as suspect. The phrase sorts insiders from outsiders and turns a dispute over borders and recognition into a public test of belonging.

Its most important function is replacement by implication. The speaker does not have to say “end Israel.” The map says it. If the land between river and sea is to be one Palestine, Israel is no longer there as a Jewish state. The slogan’s discipline is that it makes this conclusion feel like liberation.

NOTES

1. The phrase alone does not prove the private intent of every speaker. Some users sincerely mean equal rights or Palestinian freedom.

2. Context matters for speech-law and platform-enforcement purposes. Meta’s Oversight Board treated three standalone uses as non-violating under Meta’s rules because the posts lacked calls for violence, exclusion, or praise for Hamas.

3. The public dispute is therefore not whether every utterance is legally punishable. The stronger question is what the slogan asks the map to become.

4. The slogan’s maximalist content predates the 1967 occupation. That weakens the claim that it is only an anti-occupation phrase.

5. If a speaker means two states, equal rights, or Palestinian self-determination beside Israel, clearer language is available.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

Supporters say it means equality.

Then the slogan should say equality. “From the river to the sea” does not name equal rights. It names the full landmass in which Israel exists and then attaches freedom to “Palestine” alone.

Supporters say it is not always antisemitic.

That can be true as a legal or platform-enforcement matter. It does not make the slogan harmless. A phrase can fall short of a punishable threat and still deny Jewish collective self-determination.

Supporters say Israelis also use map-wide claims.

Some Israeli maximalist language deserves criticism for the same reason: it erases Palestinian national claims. But one maximalism does not sanitize the other. The existence of Israeli hardliners does not make an antizionist replacement slogan moderate.

Supporters say the phrase predates Hamas.

That does not solve the problem. It shows that the territorial rejection is older than Hamas and older than the 1967 occupation. Hamas inherited and sharpened a claim already present in earlier Palestinian nationalist texts.
