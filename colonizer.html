Identity slur / foreigner claim / Jewish erasure

Colonizer

Definition

“Colonizer” is used in antizionist speech to mark Jews in Israel as foreign settlers who arrived from outside and therefore possess no legitimate national claim. The word does more than criticize a policy. It recodes Jewish return as invasion and Jewish self-determination as theft.

The charge is historically selective. Jews are indigenous to the land in the ordinary civilizational sense: the people, language, calendar, law, sacred texts, collective memory, and national name were formed there. Modern Zionism did not invent that bond. It turned an older attachment into a modern political movement.

This does not mean the land was empty or that Palestinian attachment is unreal. It means the antizionist use of “colonizer” depends on deleting one side of the history. It makes Jews visible only as Europeans and Israelis visible only as invaders.

Common Usage

The word appears on protest signs, campus statements, social media, and activist literature. It is usually paired with “settler,” “white,” “European,” “colonial,” or “decolonize.” The effect is to place Israel inside a familiar moral script: native people against foreign colonizers.

That script has persuasive force because real colonialism existed and did real damage. But the Israel case does not fit cleanly. There was no Jewish imperial metropole sending settlers to enrich a mother country. There is no distant Jewish empire to which Israel belongs.

The label also erases Mizrahi and Sephardi Jews. A Jew whose family fled Baghdad, Sana’a, Cairo, Tripoli, Fez, Tehran, or Damascus is not a European colonizer in any serious historical sense. The slogan survives by flattening Jewish diversity into one usable enemy.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that Jews are not a people returning to a historic homeland but outsiders occupying someone else’s country. Once that premise is accepted, Jewish political rights become easier to dismiss. A colonizer is not owed compromise. A colonizer is expected to leave.

That is why the term often slides from criticism of settlements to rejection of Israel itself. Used precisely, one could argue about settlement policy in the West Bank. Used as a total description of Jews in Israel, the word becomes a denial of Jewish national legitimacy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem to Haifa.

Origin / Lineage

The colonial frame entered anti-Israel language through Arab nationalist opposition to Zionism, Soviet anti-Zionist propaganda after 1967, Third World diplomacy at the United Nations, and later postcolonial academic theory. Fayez Sayegh’s 1965 pamphlet “Zionist Colonialism in Palestine” became a key early text in this lineage.

The Durban NGO Forum in 2001 helped globalize the vocabulary for campus and NGO activism. It described Israel through racism, apartheid, and colonialism and linked that framing to boycott and isolation campaigns. The BDS movement later drew heavily from that moral grammar.

Function

The word functions as a legitimacy weapon. It does not ask which law, border, military practice, or government decision should change. It declares Jewish sovereignty itself foreign.

It also blocks evidence. Jewish archaeology, Hebrew language, biblical and rabbinic memory, continuous communities, pilgrimage, burial, and repeated return all become irrelevant once the speaker has assigned Jews the role of colonizer. The conclusion is built into the label.

Notes

1. The word “colonizer” can be used descriptively in some historical contexts. Its antizionist use against Israel usually functions as a totalizing accusation.

2. Palestinian attachment to the land is real. Jewish attachment is also real. A serious account must hold both facts at once.

3. The existence of West Bank settlements does not make all Israeli Jews colonizers.

Responses / Criticisms

It turns Jewish indigeneity into foreignness. The Jewish people did not borrow their name, language, calendar, scripture, or memory from Europe. The civilizational root is in the land of Israel.

It erases non-European Jews. Much of Israeli Jewish society descends from communities in the Middle East, North Africa, Ethiopia, the former Soviet Union, and other places outside the simple European-colonizer frame.

It hides the policy question. If the issue is a settlement, a border, or a military order, name it. Calling Jews colonizers avoids the policy question and attacks the legitimacy of Jewish statehood itself.
