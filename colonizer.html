Identity slur / foreigner claim / Jewish erasure

Colonizer

Definition

“Colonizer” is an antizionist label that presents Jews in Israel as foreign invaders in a land to which they have no legitimate connection. It turns Jewish national return into European conquest and treats Hebrew, Jewish memory, biblical geography, archaeology, prayer, pilgrimage, burial, and continuous Jewish presence as if they were late inventions. The word does not simply criticize settlement policy or a particular government. It recasts the Jewish people as outsiders in their own ancestral homeland.

The term is powerful because it supplies a ready-made moral script: colonizer versus indigenous, thief versus owner, oppressor versus victim. Once that script is accepted, Jewish history becomes evidence to be ignored rather than material to be weighed. Mizrahi and Sephardi Jews, whose families came from Iraq, Yemen, Morocco, Egypt, Syria, Iran, Turkey, Libya, Tunisia, and other Middle Eastern and North African communities, are pushed out of view because their existence damages the claim that Israel is a European colonial transplant. So are Ethiopian Jews, Soviet Jews, Holocaust survivors, and Jews whose families lived in the land long before the state.

Common Usage

The word appears in campus protests, social-media graphics, NGO language, and postcolonial activist writing. “Zionist colonizer,” “settler-colonial state,” and “colonizer out” are used to place Israel inside a familiar anticolonial story. In that story, Jewish sovereignty is not a national project of a persecuted people but an imported system of domination. The point is not to debate borders. The point is to make Jewish statehood morally impossible.

In many cases the word is applied not only to Israeli policy but to Israeli Jews as a class. It can be aimed at students, academics, artists, businesses, synagogues, and Jewish institutions abroad. This makes the term different from criticism of settlements, military rule, or government decisions. It assigns inherited guilt to people because they are Jewish, Israeli, Zionist, or presumed to be attached to Israel.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that Jews do not belong where Israel exists. If Jews are colonizers, their state is not a flawed state in a hard conflict; it is an illegitimate occupation by definition. Their language, army, courts, elections, immigration laws, and public culture become instruments of foreign domination. The moral end point follows naturally: decolonization means removing Jewish sovereignty.

This is why the word is so useful to antizionist politics. It collapses the Jewish refugee story into the Palestinian displacement story and permits only one people to be native. It makes the Holocaust, pogroms, expulsions from Arab and Muslim-majority countries, Soviet antisemitism, and Jewish homelessness look irrelevant to the question of sovereignty. Jewish vulnerability disappears because the Jew has been recast as the colonizer.

Origin / Lineage

The colonizer charge developed from several overlapping sources. Arab nationalist writing after 1948 described Zionism as an alien implant backed by imperial power. Soviet propaganda after 1967 helped merge Zionism with racism, imperialism, and colonial aggression. Fayez Sayegh’s Zionist Colonialism in Palestine, published by the PLO Research Center in 1965, became one of the most important early formulations of this frame. Later postcolonial scholarship gave the charge academic durability, especially after Israel’s 1967 victory and the rise of settlement debates.

The term became more common in Western activist culture after the 2001 Durban Conference and the spread of BDS after 2005. By the 2010s and especially after October 7, 2023, “colonizer” was no longer only a theoretical category. It became a street accusation, a campus chant, and a social sorting device. It told audiences who deserved sympathy and who deserved removal from sympathy.

Function

The function of “colonizer” is to deny Jewish indigeneity and to place Israel outside the family of legitimate nations. It turns a national conflict between two peoples into a one-sided morality play. It also removes Arab rejectionism, antisemitic violence, Jewish refugee history, and Israeli security concerns from the frame. Once the word is accepted, those facts appear as excuses rather than evidence.

The term also disciplines Jews in the diaspora. A Jew who defends Israel’s existence can be treated as a defender of colonialism. A Jew who rejects Zionism can be presented as acceptable. This makes the word a loyalty test as much as an argument. Its target is not only policy; it is Jewish peoplehood expressed through statehood.

Notes

1. Calling Israel a colonial state ignores the central fact that Jews understand the land as their ancestral homeland, not as a distant imperial possession.

2. European Jews were not agents of a European mother country. They were often fleeing European exclusion, violence, quotas, and extermination.

3. Most Israeli Jews are not simply European settlers. The history of Mizrahi and Sephardi Jews, and of Jews displaced from Arab and Muslim-majority countries, is essential to the record.

4. None of this erases Palestinian displacement or Palestinian national claims. It means those claims do not cancel Jewish peoplehood or Jewish self-determination.

Responses / Criticisms

It erases Jewish history in the land. The Jewish connection to Israel did not begin in 1897, 1917, or 1948. It runs through language, religion, law, memory, archaeology, pilgrimage, burial, and repeated return. A movement can modernize an ancient attachment without inventing it.

It misdescribes Jewish refugees as imperial agents. A colonizer usually represents an expanding empire. Jews fleeing pogroms, Nazi extermination, Arab-world persecution, Soviet repression, and statelessness were not agents of a mother country. They were often people without protection seeking the one place where Jewish life could be defended by Jews.

It turns criticism into delegitimation. A person can criticize settlements, military rule, annexation proposals, or discrimination without calling Jews colonizers. The colonizer label goes further. It says Jewish sovereignty itself is foreign and therefore removable.
