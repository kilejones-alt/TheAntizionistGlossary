Definition

In modern antizionism, apartheid is lifted out of South Africa and made into one of the central slurs against Israel. In South Africa, apartheid was a race-based legal order that ran from 1948 to 1994. It held white minority rule in place through segregation, pass laws, racial classification, civic exclusion, forced removals, and the denial of rights to the black majority. The Afrikaans word means “separateness.” That history is specific, documented, and defined in law. Applied to Israel, the word stops being criticism and becomes a verdict. It is a comparison to one of the most condemned systems of rule in the modern age. Checkpoints, barriers, military administration, and policing are recast as Israel’s own version of Afrikaner race law. Security measures forged under fire become evidence of racial supremacy. The conclusion arrives before the history is ever opened. The comparison survives only by flattening everything Israel has done since 1948. It passes over the Oslo II Accord (1995), which split the West Bank into Areas A, B, and C through agreements that Israeli and Palestinian leaders both signed. It strains hardest inside Israel’s pre-1967 borders, where Arab citizens vote in national elections and sit in the Knesset. They serve as judges, practice medicine, teach in universities, and live as full citizens. Israel’s own statistics put the Arab population at 2.157 million in April 2026, 21.1 percent of the country. By transplanting South Africa’s racial order onto a fight over land and political legitimacy, antizionists conflate Palestinians in the territories together with Arab citizens of Israel and wave away the conditions Israel actually contends with: contested borders, terrorism, rejected negotiations, the conduct of Palestinian leaders. The label asks the reader to reach the conclusion before weighing any of that. One of the things that separates antizionism from criticism of Israeli policy is this very move—a charge that damns an entire state while naming no policy it opposes and offering no alternative. To call Israel an apartheid state clears that bar, because it was never a criticism of policy to begin with.

Common Usage

The word has hardened into standard vocabulary across campuses, NGO reports, activist coalitions, and UN bodies. Israeli Apartheid Week began in Toronto in 2005, run by the Arab Students’ Collective at the University of Toronto, and grew into an annual fixture tied to boycott and divestment drives. Its programs lean on speakers, mock-wall installations, staged performances, and student-government resolutions. The largest human rights organizations have taken up the label in long legal reports. Human Rights Watch’s A Threshold Crossed (2021) accused Israeli authorities of the crimes of apartheid and persecution. Amnesty International’s Israel’s Apartheid Against Palestinians (2022) stretched the charge across Israel proper, East Jerusalem, the West Bank, Gaza, and the refugees—a reach that gathers wholly distinct legal questions under one theory taken as given. It sounds comprehensive. What it actually does is blur the line between citizens, residents, noncitizens, occupied territories, Hamas-run Gaza, and diaspora refugees. Inside the UN, the same label turns up as settled fact in the reports and speeches of the Human Rights Council and the Special Rapporteur on the Situation of Human Rights in the Palestinian Territories, spoken as though the case were already closed. On campuses, the word drives divestment resolutions, faculty open letters, and demands that Israeli scholars be shut out. It hands antizionists a single noun that carries a criminal charge, a South African analogy, and a call for punishment all at once. That is why it carries.

Implied Meaning

A study of suicide bombing during the Second Intifada counted 148 bombers by July 2005, behind 657 deaths and 3,682 injuries. The West Bank separation barrier went up in those same years. Its stated and documented purpose was to keep bombers out of Israeli population centers, not to enforce racial separation. The apartheid charge also forgets what came after Israel’s full withdrawal from Gaza in August 2005. Israel tore down every settlement and pulled out every soldier. Hamas and the other armed factions answered with rockets fired into Israeli towns. Human Rights Watch counted roughly 2,700 Qassam-type rockets between September 2005 and May 2007. The U.S. Congressional Record in January 2009 put the number above 6,300 rockets and mortars since the pullout. The label cannot survive that sequence. The deeper claim rides on the South Africa comparison itself. The settled judgment on South African apartheid was that the system could not be reformed, only dismantled. Carry the label to Israel and the same logic comes with it. If Israel is a racist state, the answer on offer is not a change of policy. It is the removal of whatever lets the state go on existing as a Jewish sovereign power. The analogy also feeds itself once swallowed. A barrier or a checkpoint can be read back as fresh proof of the same claim, and so can a military operation, a police measure, a citizenship dispute, or an emergency order. The charge grows almost impossible to test, because every Israeli security act is folded straight back into it. David Hirsh names this loop in Contemporary Left Antisemitism (2017) as a recurring habit of antizionist argument. The label works less like a conclusion than like an engine for producing more of them.

Origin / Lineage

The Israel-apartheid comparison carries a traceable institutional history, one that runs well behind the NGO reports of the 2020s and the BDS campaign of 2005. Its line passes through the Soviet, Arab League, and Third World campaigns of the Cold War, all aimed at cutting Israel off from the world. The vocabulary was not born on campus, even if campus activism is what later made it household language. The comparison surfaced abroad long before it became a chant. South Africa’s own apartheid government is sometimes said to have thrown the charge at Israel first, in 1961, after Israel criticized Pretoria at the United Nations. Whatever the truth of that exchange, the line that mattered came from Fayez Sayegh’s pamphlet Zionist Colonialism in Palestine (1965), issued by the PLO Research Center, which cast Zionism as a racially exclusive settler-colonial venture, likened it to the European empires, and left later activists a vocabulary ready to use. The Soviet Union made the claim a Cold War export and aimed it at the newly independent nations of Africa and the Arab world. The campaign helped push through UN General Assembly Resolution 3151G (1973), which denounced an “unholy alliance between Portuguese colonialism, South African racism, Zionism and Israeli imperialism.” Two years later came Resolution 3379, the campaign’s most famous prize, declaring that “Zionism is a form of racism and racial discrimination.” The repeal arrived in 1991. The vocabulary it had normalized stayed put. The modern legal version took its shape at the 2001 World Conference Against Racism in Durban. The NGO Forum declaration branded Israel a “racist, apartheid state” and called for measures modeled on the campaign against South Africa, handing the BDS movement much of its legal vocabulary four years later. Over the next two decades, NGOs worked the 1973 Apartheid Convention and the 1998 Rome Statute’s definition of the crime into their arguments against Israel. By the time the courts at The Hague began taking up related claims in the 2020s, six decades of groundwork were already in place.

Function

The word lifts the charge into the courtroom language of war crimes and crimes against humanity. In that register, naming the crime is meant to carry consequences: sanctions, isolation abroad, arrest warrants, business boycotts, academic exclusion, professional ruin. This is the vocabulary of disqualification, not negotiation. The legal scholar Eugene Kontorovich has traced exactly this strategic use of the courts against Israel. Once the language turns criminal, Israel is set in the dock and told to defend its right to exist. In academic life, the word doubles as a loyalty test. Jewish students, scholars, artists, and professionals who keep any tie to Israel meet it as a demand to denounce—a demand that lands on most diaspora Jews, for whom Israel is no abstraction but a thread of communal, familial, religious, and historical life. The label also yields a one-sided account of the conflict. It fixes on Israeli authority and Israeli action and says far less about Hamas rule in Gaza, Palestinian Authority administration in the West Bank, the offers turned down, or the security conditions Israel was answering. None of that lopsidedness is an accident. If Israel is a racist state, then dead Israeli civilians can be written off as the system’s byproduct instead of murders committed by armed men. After October 7, 2023, the logic stepped into the open, when antizionists in several Western cities greeted the Hamas massacres not as atrocities but as resistance against an illegitimate state. The apartheid label had already built the room in which that sentence could be said out loud.

Notes

  1. The apartheid label sits badly against ordinary life inside Israel. Arab citizens vote in national elections, sit in the Knesset, and hold posts throughout the judiciary. Justice Khaled Kabub, an Israeli Arab Muslim, joined the Supreme Court in 2022. Arab citizenship inside Israel cuts against the apartheid charge, which is why the conversation tends to hurry off toward the West Bank and Gaza.
  2. Israel has real internal inequalities: economic gaps, uneven policing, planning fights, unsettled tensions involving Arab citizens. Those problems are documented and serious. They are not the racial machinery of Afrikaner rule, and flattening the two insults both the victims of South African apartheid and the actual texture of Israeli civic life.
  3. NGO and legal arguments routinely collapse Israel proper, East Jerusalem, the West Bank, Gaza, and the refugees into one system, though each carries its own legal, military, and administrative arrangement. Palestinians in Areas A and B live under Palestinian Authority jurisdiction built by the Oslo accords; Gaza has answered to Hamas since 2007. Palestinians in Ramallah or Gaza City do not vote in Israeli elections because they are not Israeli citizens under Israeli civil law, not because Israel sorts them by race.
  4. The label keeps leaving out the question of Palestinian rule. It holds Israel answerable for the conditions of Palestinian life while treating Hamas and the Palestinian Authority as background. Since October 7, 2023, the imbalance has grown plainer: the same organizations reaching for the word to demand Israeli accountability have had far less to say about how Hamas treats Gaza’s own civilians.

Responses / Criticisms

It treats a national conflict as a racial caste system. South African apartheid was a domestic racial order built to keep a white minority above a disenfranchised black majority. The Israeli-Palestinian conflict is not that. It holds two national movements, repeated wars, terrorism, failed negotiations, partial Palestinian self-rule, Hamas rule in Gaza, and unsettled claims to territory. That does not make every Israeli policy defensible. It means the South African template cannot be dropped onto the conflict without bending it out of shape. It treats the security record like an inconvenient footnote. The label tends to open with Israeli control and skip the violence that drove Israeli policy. The shortcut matters. The Second Intifada and its suicide bombings were no minor backdrop, and neither was the rocket fire out of Gaza or the long line of collapsed negotiations. These are the facts behind why Israeli voters backed checkpoints, barriers, and military action. A serious argument is free to attack those measures. It is not free to pretend they came from nowhere. It turns criticism of Israeli policy into opposition to Israel’s existence. Criticizing governments is ordinary and legitimate. People do it to Israeli governments every day, Israelis loudest among them. Calling Jewish self-rule racist by its nature is a different act. The claim fixes on no single law, no one coalition, no lone court ruling. It tells the reader the state itself belongs beside Afrikaner rule—and once that is granted, Israel’s very existence is up for debate.
