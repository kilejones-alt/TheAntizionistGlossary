Nazi inversion / Holocaust memory / historical reversal

Modern-Day Nazis

The comparison turns Jews into heirs of their destroyers.

DEFINITION

“Modern-day Nazis” is a label used in antizionist speech to portray Israel, Zionists, or Jews identified with Israel as heirs of Hitler’s regime. It does more than say Israeli policy is wrong, harsh, or unlawful. It places the Jewish state in symbolic continuity with the movement that sought to destroy the Jewish people.

Nazism was not a generic synonym for cruelty. It was a racial-totalitarian project built on dictatorship, conquest, antisemitism, racial law, and extermination. The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 made Jewish exclusion part of German law. Nazi racism later produced the systematic murder of six million Jews and millions of other victims across Europe. That is the historical weight carried by the word Nazi.

For that reason, calling Israelis or Zionists "modern-day Nazis" is not ordinary criticism. It takes the central crime committed against Jews and turns it into a weapon against Jewish sovereignty. The International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance identifies "drawing comparisons of contemporary Israeli policy to that of the Nazis" as an example that may be antisemitic, depending on context. The reason is straightforward: the analogy does not clarify Israeli policy. It reverses the Holocaust against the people it targeted.

COMMON USAGE

The label appears in chants, placards, cartoons, graffiti, social media posts, campus slogans, speeches, and protest art. Israeli leaders are given Hitler mustaches. Swastikas are placed over Israeli flags. Gaza is compared to Auschwitz. The Warsaw Ghetto is invoked as if Israeli military power and Nazi extermination policy belonged to the same category.

The comparison has also appeared in high-level public speech. In December 2023, Reuters reported that Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan said Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu was "no different" from Hitler and likened Israel's Gaza campaign to Nazi treatment of Jews. That language did not come from an anonymous protest sign. It came from a head of state, which helped normalize the analogy for audiences already prepared to hear it.

The phrase is especially common when the speaker wants the strongest possible condemnation without doing the work required by legal or historical comparison. "War crime," "unlawful strike," "reckless command decision," or "disproportionate use of force" all require facts. "Modern-day Nazis" requires only the emotional force of the symbol.

IMPLIED MEANING

The implied meaning is that Jewish power has become the new Nazism. The claim turns the Holocaust into a mirror image: Jews were once victims of extermination, and now Jews are said to have become exterminators. That reversal is the point. It strips Jewish historical vulnerability of its force and recasts Jewish sovereignty as a danger to humanity.

The analogy also destroys useful distinctions. It blurs war and extermination, battlefield harm and death camps, emergency rule and racial dictatorship, state errors and state doctrine, civilian casualties and planned industrial murder. Once the Nazi comparison is accepted, context sounds like excuse-making. Evidence becomes secondary because the verdict has already been supplied by the symbol.

The comparison also shifts suspicion from Israel to Jews more broadly. If Zionists are Nazis, then Jews who support Israel's existence can be treated as collaborators with Nazism. This is how a claim about a state becomes a social weapon against Jews in classrooms, workplaces, arts spaces, unions, and professional life. The target is no longer only one Israeli minister or one Israeli operation. The target becomes the Jew who refuses to disown Jewish self-determination.

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

The Israel-Nazi analogy has a traceable lineage. After the Six-Day War in 1967, Soviet anti-Zionist propaganda intensified. Soviet writers and state publishers described Zionism as racist, fascist, colonial, and allied with imperial power. Yuri Ivanov's Caution: Zionism!, published in English by Progress Publishers in 1970, presented Zionism as a sinister global system and linked it to fascism, racism, and conspiracy.

UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 in 1975 did not call Zionism Nazism. Its operative line declared Zionism "a form of racism and racial discrimination." But the resolution belonged to the same diplomatic campaign that made Zionism comparable to the twentieth century's most hated ideologies. The resolution was revoked in 1991. The vocabulary survived.

Another channel ran through Palestinian and Arab public writing. Mahmoud Abbas published al-Wajh al-akhar: al-'alaqat al-sirriyah bayna al-Naziyah wa-al-Sihyuniyah in Amman in 1984. The National Library of Australia catalogue lists the title, author, publisher, date, and subject matter. The English title is usually rendered as The Other Side: The Secret Relationship Between Nazism and Zionism. The book belongs to a wider tradition that tries to link Zionism with Nazism and shift part of Holocaust guilt onto Zionists.

By the time of the 2001 Durban process and the later BDS era, Holocaust inversion had become familiar in anti-Israel activism. After October 7, 2023, the analogy moved faster because the channels were already built.

FUNCTION

The label closes the argument. If Israel is Nazi-like, negotiation sounds indecent, security claims sound fake, and Jewish fear sounds manipulative. A person who asks for evidence can be treated as defending Nazism. That is why the label is so efficient. It supplies the harshest available verdict before the facts have been sorted.

It also degrades Holocaust memory. Auschwitz becomes a metaphor. The Warsaw Ghetto becomes a protest prop. The specific machinery of Nazi rule disappears: racial law, one-party dictatorship, conquest, ghettos, Einsatzgruppen, deportations, camps, gas chambers, and extermination policy. The word Nazi is kept only for its power to condemn.

The label also places Israel outside the family of ordinary states. Israel has elected governments, opposition parties, Arab citizens with voting rights, Arab members of Knesset, Arab judges, a Supreme Court, and public dissent. None of that makes Israeli policy beyond criticism. It does place Israel far outside the category of Nazi Germany. The comparison survives because it is useful, not because it is accurate.

NOTES

1. The Nazi comparison should not be confused with historical study of the 1933 Haavara Agreement, the wartime conduct of particular actors, or documented contacts among enemies and intermediaries. Serious history can examine those subjects. Holocaust inversion does something else: it turns Zionism or Israel into Nazism by category.

2. IHRA does not say all criticism of Israel is antisemitic. It expressly states that criticism of Israel similar to that leveled against any other country cannot be regarded as antisemitic. The Nazi comparison appears in the examples because it weaponizes the Holocaust and assigns the Jewish state the identity of the regime that murdered Europe's Jews.

3. Erdogan's December 2023 Hitler comparison is a strong modern example because it is reported by Reuters and came from a head of state. It should be used as a public normalization example, not as proof that every protester using Nazi language took it from Erdogan.

4. Abbas's 1984 book can be cited through the National Library of Australia catalogue. Claims about his 1982 Moscow dissertation should be used only with stronger bibliographic support unless the dissertation itself is being cited directly.

5. The comparison cheapens the word Nazi. It does not make Israeli conduct clearer. It makes the Holocaust more available for slogans.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

It reverses the Holocaust against the people it was meant to destroy.

The comparison takes the central crime committed against Jews and turns it against Jews with power. That reversal is not incidental. It is the emotional engine of the phrase.

It destroys every useful distinction.

Nazism was a racial-totalitarian project built around dictatorship, conquest, and extermination. Israel is a contested democracy fighting enemies, governing under internal strain, making grave decisions, and arguing with itself in public. A serious critic can condemn Israeli policy without pretending the IDF is the SS.

It turns Jewish grief into a liability.

The phrase tells Jews that the memory of the Holocaust no longer protects them from eliminationist language. It can now be used against them. That is why the analogy lands as more than bad history. It is a hostile use of Jewish memory.
