Ethnostate

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Ethnostate” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Definition

“Ethnostate” is used in contemporary antizionist speech to present Israel as a country organized for Jews at the expense of everyone else. The word usually points to the Law of Return, Jewish national symbols, Hebrew, the 2018 Nation-State Basic Law, and Israel’s unresolved conflict with Palestinians in the West Bank, Gaza, and East Jerusalem. It compresses all of that into one verdict: Jewish sovereignty is racist in form.

The term has force because it sounds like a neutral description while carrying the stigma of racial rule. In ordinary English after Charlottesville, ethnostate usually evokes white nationalist dreams of a racial homeland. When the same word is applied to Israel, Jewish self-determination is pulled into that field of association. A state founded as a refuge after repeated expulsions, pogroms, and the Holocaust is made to sound like a racial project modeled on exclusion.

The problem is not that Israel has no Jewish national character. It plainly does. Israel has the Law of Return, Jewish public holidays, Jewish symbols, Hebrew civic life, and a constitutional commitment to the Jewish people. The problem is that ethnostate is too crude to describe the legal and civic facts. Israel is a Jewish-majority state with democratic institutions, formal citizenship rights for Arab citizens, real minority disadvantages, disputed territories, and a long war with enemies that have often rejected Jewish sovereignty in any part of the land. Compressing all of that into ethnostate turns a mixed record into a racial verdict.

In serious scholarship, sharper terms are usually used. Sammy Smooha wrote of Israel as an ethnic democracy. Yoav Peled examined Israeli citizenship through liberal, republican, and ethno-national strands. Oren Yiftachel used ethnocracy to analyze land, borders, and unequal access to power. These terms are debatable, but they are more exact than ethnostate. Ethnostate is not a settled class in Freedom House, V-Dem, the Economist Intelligence Unit, or Polity. Its usefulness in antizionist speech lies less in precision than in the damage it can do.

Common Usage

The word became more common in anti-Israel commentary after the Nation-State Basic Law passed in July 2018. That law declares Israel the nation-state of the Jewish people, identifies Hebrew as the state language, calls Jewish settlement a national value, and states that national self-determination in Israel is unique to the Jewish people. Critics treated the law as proof that Israel had placed Jewish nationality above civic equality. Supporters answered that the law did not cancel individual rights, voting rights, or Arab citizenship, and that other Basic Laws and ordinary statutes still protect civil rights.

After the November 2022 election, the word became easier to use because Netanyahu's governing coalition included hard-right religious-nationalist parties. During the 2023 court crisis, critics used ethnostate to describe the fear that weaker judicial review would leave minorities with fewer protections. After October 7 and the Gaza war, the word appeared beside apartheid, settler-colonialism, genocide, and Jewish supremacy in campus protests, faculty letters, NGO commentary, and online campaigns.

The term also draws power from American racial politics. In U.S. usage, ethnostate is strongly tied to white nationalism and the alt-right. Wilmot Robertson's The Ethnostate gave the word an explicitly white racial meaning in the early 1990s, and Richard Spencer later helped popularize the idea of a white ethnostate. Once that meaning entered public memory, applying the word to Israel made the Jewish state sound like a Jewish version of white separatism. That is the rhetorical effect, even when the speaker does not know the word's genealogy.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that Israel's Jewish features are not national features but ethnic domination. The Law of Return becomes racial exclusion. Hebrew and Jewish symbols become supremacy. The Nation-State Law becomes proof that non-Jews are permanent outsiders. West Bank checkpoints, Gaza border controls, settlement growth, and Arab citizen disadvantages all become evidence of one Jewish order.

That reading depends on several mergers. It merges Arab citizens of Israel with Palestinians in the West Bank. It merges Palestinians in Gaza with residents of East Jerusalem. It merges Israeli civil law, Palestinian Authority administration, Hamas rule, Israeli military control, and refugee claims into one claim about Israel's internal nature. Those distinctions are not excuses. They are necessary to know what is being judged.

Arab citizens of Israel are not foreigners inside Israel. They vote, hold Knesset seats, run parties, serve as judges, study in universities, work in hospitals, litigate in courts, and participate in Israeli civil life. In 2021, Ra'am joined the Bennett-Lapid coalition; the Israel Democracy Institute describes this as the first time an Arab party was a formal member of an Israeli governing coalition. In 2022, Khaled Kabub, an Arab Muslim judge, joined Israel's Supreme Court. These facts do not erase discrimination, land and planning disputes, budget gaps, crime and policing failures, or social exclusion. They do show why ethnostate is a poor fit if it means racial caste.

The demographic facts also complicate the label. Israel's April 2026 Independence Day statistics put the population at 10.244 million, with 2.157 million Arabs, or 21.1 percent. A state with a large Arab citizen minority, Arab parliamentary parties, and Arab judges does not match the ordinary meaning of a pure exclusionary racial state. It may still be criticized as an imperfect Jewish democracy. That is not the same claim.

The Law of Return is the strongest fact for critics because it gives Jews a pathway to immigration based on Jewish connection. Yet diaspora-return laws are not unique to Israel. Many states give preferred return or citizenship access to members of a historic people, diaspora, or national kin group. Israel's case is distinctive because Jewish history is distinctive: exile, statelessness, genocide, and the absence of another Jewish sovereign refuge. To call that history racial privilege without also naming the history that produced it is not analysis. It is erasure.

Origin / Lineage

The Oxford English Dictionary identifies the earliest known use of ethnostate in a 1985 glossary by Fred W. Riggs. In that use, the term was descriptive. It did not yet carry the common post-Charlottesville meaning.

The word took a darker turn through white nationalist writing. Wilmot Robertson's The Ethnostate, composed between 1985 and 1992 and published in 1992, used the term for a white racial homeland. Later white nationalists, including Richard Spencer, helped make ethnostate a public shorthand for racial separatism. By the time the word entered mass debate after the 2017 Charlottesville rally, it no longer sounded technical. It sounded supremacist.

Anti-Israel use of ethnostate draws from a different but related line. Scholars of Israel and Palestine had long debated Jewish statehood, ethnic democracy, ethnocracy, citizenship, land, and minority rights. Smooha, Peled, Yiftachel, Rouhana, Ghanem, Hirschl, and others supplied serious critiques of Israeli civic life and national identity. Antizionist took the harder public word, ethnostate, and attached it to Israel. The result is simpler and more damaging than the scholarship from which many of the concerns came.

A third source is older anti-Zionist polemic. Soviet and Arab nationalist writing often treated Zionism as racism, colonialism, or supremacy. UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 gave that line international status in 1975 by declaring Zionism a form of racism and racial discrimination. The resolution was revoked in 1991, but its logic survived. Ethnostate is a newer word for an older move: present Jewish sovereignty not as national self-determination, but as racism by definition.

Function

The term makes Jewish statehood sound disqualifying. Once Israel is called an ethnostate, the debate shifts away from specific law and policy. The issue becomes Israel’s basic character. Boycott, divestment, sanctions, academic exclusion, cultural boycotts, arms embargoes, and diplomatic isolation then sound like anti-racist duties rather than coercive measures aimed at the only Jewish-majority state.

The word also turns ordinary Jewish national features into evidence of wrongdoing. A flag with a Jewish symbol, a Jewish immigration law, Jewish holidays, Hebrew, and state concern for Jews abroad become suspect in a way similar features in other countries usually do not. That special treatment is one reason the term raises the double-standard concern identified by Natan Sharansky's 3D test and by several antisemitism definitions, even though those definitions differ on other points.

It is easier to say ethnostate than to ask separate questions about Arab citizens of Israel, Palestinians in Area A, Palestinians in Area B, Palestinians in Area C, East Jerusalem residents, Gaza under Hamas, Palestinian refugees, Israeli settlement policy, Israeli court review, and citizenship law. Precision weakens the slogan. That is why the slogan resists precision.

Notes

1. The Nation-State Basic Law should be quoted carefully. It states that Israel is the nation-state of the Jewish people and that national self-determination in Israel is unique to the Jewish people. It does not say Arab citizens cannot vote, hold office, litigate, or serve in public roles.

2. Israel has no single written constitution. Its Basic Laws, ordinary statutes, Supreme Court decisions, and democratic practice must be read together. The Nation-State Law is important, but it is not the whole of Israeli constitutional life.

3. Arab citizens have formal rights and documented disadvantages. Both facts should remain in the entry. Denying the disadvantages weakens the defense of Israel. Denying the rights makes the ethnostate claim easier than the record allows.

4. West Bank and Gaza questions are real but separate. Palestinians in the West Bank do not have Israeli citizenship because the area has not been annexed into Israel's ordinary civil order. Palestinians in Gaza have lived under Hamas rule since 2007. These facts do not solve the conflict. They do mean Gaza and the West Bank cannot be treated as simple proof of Israel's domestic citizenship model.

5. Freedom House's 2026 Israel report rates Israel Free with a score of 73/100. Freedom House separately assesses the West Bank and Gaza. That separation is useful because it shows the very distinction the ethnostate label often erases.

Responses / Criticisms

It substitutes stigma for definition.

Ethnostate sounds exact, but its meaning changes from user to user. Sometimes it means racial caste. Sometimes it means a nation-state with a majority people. Sometimes it means a state with a diaspora return law. Sometimes it means any state with an ethnic or religious national identity. A word that shifts this much is not doing careful work.

It mistakes Jewish self-determination for racial rule.

The Jewish state was not born from a racial fantasy of mastery. It came from Jewish peoplehood, ancestral connection, modern nationalism, persecution, and the collapse of European and Middle Eastern safety for Jews. That does not make every Israeli law just. It does mean the white-nationalist meaning of ethnostate is a bad comparison.

It merges distinct populations.

Arab citizens, West Bank Palestinians, Gaza Palestinians, East Jerusalem residents, and refugees do not have the same legal status. Treating them as one undifferentiated group makes the slogan easier and the analysis worse.

It is used with unusual intensity against the Jewish state.

Many countries have established religions, majority national identities, diaspora-return laws, ancestry-based citizenship, or high ethnic homogeneity. They are rarely described with the same word, stigma, and institutional pressure. The selective application is not incidental. It is part of the term's appeal.
