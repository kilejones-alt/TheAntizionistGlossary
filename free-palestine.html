Liberation slogan / political ambiguity / symbolic demand

Free Palestine

The phrase can mean rights, statehood, or the disappearance of the state of Israel.

DEFINITION

“Free Palestine” is not antisemitic in every possible use. It can mean Palestinian self-determination, civil rights, or a negotiated state alongside Israel. In contemporary antizionist use, however, the phrase often works differently. It is an elastic demand whose content is left unspecified, allowing moderate listeners to hear rights while radicals hear removal.

The distinction turns on what Palestine is being freed from. If the answer is military rule in the West Bank, Hamas rule in Gaza, Palestinian Authority repression, corruption, poverty, or statelessness, the slogan can point toward ordinary civic claims. If the answer is Israel's existence as a Jewish state, it becomes a denial of Jewish self-determination.

That ambiguity has a history. The Palestine Liberation Organization was founded in 1964, before Israel controlled the West Bank or Gaza. The later 1968 Palestinian National Charter described armed struggle as the way to liberate Palestine, called the 1947 partition and the establishment of Israel illegal, denied that Jews constitute a nation, and rejected substitutes for the total liberation of Palestine. The modern slogan does not mechanically repeat every article of that charter, but it inherits a vocabulary in which liberation often meant replacing Israel, not building beside it.

This is why context matters. At a peace rally, in a human-rights report, or in a two-state argument, the words may mean Palestinian independence. At a campus encampment that also chants "from the river to the sea," at a rally outside a synagogue, or during an attack on Jews or Israelis, the words carry a different force. The problem is not the word free. The problem is the unresolved object of liberation.

COMMON USAGE

Since October 7, 2023, "Free Palestine" has appeared across campus encampments, street protests, social media campaigns, graffiti, clothing, concert stages, and institutional statements. The ADL describes the phrase as widely used by people advocating Palestinian rights and notes that many users do not intend support for extremism or violence. It also records that, since October 7, the phrase has been used in numerous cases to intimidate or attack Jews and Jewish institutions.

The phrase has also appeared in lethal and violent contexts. In the May 21, 2025 attack outside the Capital Jewish Museum in Washington, D.C., Yaron Lischinsky and Sarah Milgrim, both Israeli Embassy employees, were killed after leaving an American Jewish Committee event. According to the February 2026 superseding filing summarized by the Justice Department, Elias Rodriguez allegedly fired about twenty shots and called out "Free Palestine." The Justice Department also stated that he displayed a red keffiyeh inside the museum and said, "I did it for Palestine. I did it for Gaza."

On June 1, 2025, a separate attack targeted a Run for Their Lives gathering in Boulder, Colorado, held to call attention to Israeli hostages in Gaza. The Justice Department says Mohamed Sabry Soliman approached the group with Molotov cocktails and shouted "Free Palestine" while throwing one of them. The later federal case alleged hate-crime counts and described a handwritten document referring to Zionism as an enemy until Jerusalem is liberated and Jews are expelled from the land.

Those attacks do not define every use of the slogan. They do show why Jews often hear the phrase differently from the way many activists claim to intend it. A slogan can have benign users and still develop a dangerous public life when it is repeatedly attached to Jewish targets.

IMPLIED MEANING

The slogan's force depends on a missing sentence: free Palestine from what? In much antizionist usage, the answer is not Hamas, the Palestinian Authority, failed diplomacy, Arab-state rejectionism, or Gaza's Egyptian border. The answer is Israel. More precisely, it is Jewish sovereignty.

That omission does important work. It places Jews and Israel in the role of the sole jailer of Palestinian life. It leaves aside Hamas's rule in Gaza since 2007, the Palestinian Authority's authoritarian record in the West Bank, rejected peace proposals, regional manipulation of the Palestinian cause, and Egyptian restrictions at Rafah. A vast regional history is reduced to one obstacle: the Jewish state.

The phrase also allows users to avoid the harder question of what comes next. Does "Free Palestine" mean two states? A confederation? A single Arab-majority state replacing Israel? Full refugee return into Israel? The dismantling of Zionism? Different users give different answers. Antizionist movements benefit from that looseness because it lets the slogan travel widely without forcing its speakers to say whether Jewish national survival remains part of the proposed future.

Einat Wilf and Adi Schwartz argue in The War of Return that the maximalist return demand has often functioned less as a refugee solution than as a way to undo Israel through demographics. The slogan can serve the same purpose when it promises liberation while refusing to say whether Jews remain a sovereign people in their ancestral homeland.

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

The modern phrase sits inside a longer liberation vocabulary. Arab nationalist leaders in the 1950s and 1960s framed Palestine as a cause of regional redemption. The PLO gave that cause an organizational form. Its early charter language was not a post-1967 protest against settlements. It was a pre-1967 demand to liberate Palestine from Zionism and undo the legitimacy of Israel.

After 1967, the phrase moved through Third World revolutionary networks, Soviet-aligned anti-Zionist propaganda, student movements, and Palestinian solidarity campaigns. It was attached to anti-imperial, anti-colonial, and liberation idioms that made the conflict legible to Western activists. By the time of the 2001 Durban NGO Forum and the 2005 BDS call, Palestinian liberation language had been repackaged in terms of apartheid, colonialism, occupation, return, equality, and boycott.

That lineage matters because "Free Palestine" sounds new to many Western users. It is not new. The slogan belongs to a long campaign in which liberation has often meant more than Palestinian statehood. It has meant the removal of Zionism as a legitimate national project. Modern campus usage inherits both versions: the rights-based appeal and the older eliminationist demand.

FUNCTION

In antizionist spaces, “Free Palestine” works as a membership test. People are not usually asked to define it. They are asked to say it. The phrase marks who belongs in the moral community and who must explain himself.

The phrase also compresses many disputes into one slogan. Borders, terrorism, refugee policy, Israeli security, Hamas rule, PA repression, Egypt's role, Arab-state history, and Palestinian governance all disappear. The listener receives a single command: Palestine must be freed. Because the obstacle is left unnamed, Israel becomes the implied target by default.

Finally, the slogan gives radicals a way to speak through mainstream language. A speaker who wants a Palestinian state beside Israel and a speaker who wants a Palestine in place of Israel can use the same words. That shared vocabulary is useful. It lets maximalist claims borrow the respectability of human rights language while preserving the harder edge for audiences that already understand it.

NOTES

1. The 1968 Palestinian National Charter is central to the slogan's lineage. Article 5 defined Palestinians as Arab nationals who normally resided in Palestine until 1947; Article 6 included only Jews who had normally resided there before what it called the Zionist invasion; Article 9 described armed struggle as the way to liberate Palestine; Article 19 rejected partition and Israel's establishment as illegal; Article 20 denied Jewish nationality; Article 21 rejected substitutes for total liberation.

2. The slogan is context-dependent. It should not be treated as inherently violent in every use. It should also not be treated as harmless in every use. Its meaning changes when it is paired with Hamas symbols, eliminationist slogans, synagogue protests, Jewish institutional targeting, or acts of violence.

3. The IHRA working definition identifies the denial of Jewish self-determination, including claims that Israel's existence is a racist endeavor, as an example that may be antisemitic depending on context. That standard is directly relevant when "Free Palestine" is used to mean a Palestine replacing Israel, not a Palestine beside Israel.

4. Egypt has controlled the Rafah crossing on Gaza's southern border and has imposed its own restrictions. Palestinian liberation campaigns rarely treat Egypt as a central object of protest. That selectivity does not erase Israeli responsibility where Israel exercises control, but it shows that the slogan's main pressure is aimed at Jewish sovereignty.

5. The May 2025 Washington attack and the June 2025 Boulder attack do not prove that every use of "Free Palestine" is violent. They do prove that the phrase has been cited in attacks on Jews and Israelis, which makes Jewish concern about its public use grounded in evidence, not paranoia.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

It hides the real argument.

A serious slogan should be able to say what it wants. If it means a Palestinian state beside Israel, it should say that. If it means replacing Israel, it should say that too. The phrase avoids the choice. That evasion is part of its appeal.

It frees Palestine from everyone except its rulers.

The slogan almost never confronts Hamas, the Palestinian Authority, or Arab states that have used Palestinians as instruments. It turns Palestinian suffering into a one-cause story. That makes Israel uniquely guilty and leaves Palestinians trapped under leaders and systems the slogan does not challenge.

It lets moderates and extremists share the same words.

This is the phrase's central danger. It can be defended as a humane call for rights, then used as a cry against Jewish institutions. It allows the softest and hardest versions of the movement to stand under one banner.
