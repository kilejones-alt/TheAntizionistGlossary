Pinkwashing

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Pinkwashing” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Pinkwashing

S

Pinkwashing

Definition

Pinkwashing, in anti-Israel LGBT activism, means the claim that Israel uses its record on gay rights to distract from its conduct toward Palestinians. The word did not begin with Israel. Breast Cancer Action says it coined pinkwashing through its Think Before You Pink campaign to describe companies or organizations that claim to care about breast cancer while profiting from products linked to the disease. Anti-Israel activists later adapted the term to Israel. The move matters: a real civil-rights difference becomes evidence of deceit.

When applied to Israel, the term changes shape. It does not usually deny that Israel has meaningful LGBT protections. Israel decriminalized consensual same-sex conduct in 1988. Sexual-orientation employment protection followed in 1992. The Israeli Supreme Court recognized same-sex partner benefits in El Al v. Danilowitz in 1994. Israel later recognized some same-sex family rights, recognized same-sex marriages performed abroad for registration purposes, and opened surrogacy access to same-sex couples and single men after a 2021 Supreme Court ruling. The dispute is not only over the facts. It is over whether mentioning those facts is itself suspect.

That move is the core of the term. If Israel mistreated gay people, antizionists would cite the mistreatment as proof of cruelty. When Israel protects gay people, the protection is described as public relations. The facts are admitted only long enough to be discounted. The result is a claim Israel cannot answer by improving the lives of LGBT Israelis, because the improvement itself becomes evidence against it.

Common Usage

The Israel use of pinkwashing became widely known after Sarah Schulman's 2011 New York Times opinion essay, "Israel and 'Pinkwashing.'" Schulman argued that Israeli officials promoted gay rights to distract from Palestinian suffering and from Israel's rule over Palestinians.

The word is now used against Israeli embassies, Israeli LGBT groups, Pride events in Israel, Jewish LGBT organizations, and pro-Israel queer speakers. It also appears when Israel points to Hamas or Palestinian Authority treatment of LGBT people. The answer is often that Israel is exploiting those harms to make Palestinians look backward. That reply can be partly fair when Israeli officials use LGBT rights too crudely as a branding tool. It becomes dishonest when it turns Palestinian LGBT vulnerability into something too inconvenient to discuss.

The post-October 7 period made the pressure more visible. ILGA World suspended The Aguda, Israel's main LGBT rights organization, in October 2024, then announced in May 2025 that the suspension would be lifted effective October 27, 2025 after review. In May 2026, Reuters reported that Rome Pride had barred Keshet Italia, Italy's only Jewish LGBT group, from taking part with a float because the group had not explicitly condemned Israel's actions in Gaza as genocide. Rome Pride said the event remained open to all who shared its values; Keshet Italia said the decision imposed a litmus test on a Jewish LGBT group.

Those examples matter because the target is no longer only the Israeli government. Pinkwashing language can move from criticism of state messaging to exclusion of Jews and Israelis from LGBT life unless they say what the coalition demands.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that Israeli LGBT progress is not allowed to count in the ordinary way. It is treated as cover, bait, branding, or distraction. That makes the term hard to test. The better Israel's LGBT record becomes, the easier it is to say Israel has more material to exploit.

The term also changes the burden placed on LGBT Jews and Israelis. They are asked to enter queer spaces not simply as gay, lesbian, bisexual, trans, or queer people, but as people who must first answer for Israel. A gay Israeli activist can work for equality inside Israel for decades and still be treated as a state prop. A Jewish Pride group in Europe can be told that its place in a parade depends on whether it condemns Israel in the required words.

The term further discourages serious attention to Palestinian LGBT life under Palestinian rulers. In 2019, Palestinian Authority police announced that alQaws, a Palestinian LGBT organization, was barred from holding activities in the West Bank, saying such events went against Palestinian social values. After backlash, the ban was reportedly withdrawn, but Palestinian human-rights groups said the statement had triggered hate messages and threats that posed danger to the LGBT community. UN Watch's 2022 submission to the UN Committee Against Torture also said the Palestinian Authority and Hamas torture LGBT persons, among other targeted groups.

None of this absolves Israel of its own duties toward Palestinians. It does mean queer solidarity that refuses to look at Hamas, the Palestinian Authority, family violence, social persecution, or legal exposure is not protecting LGBT Palestinians. It is protecting a slogan.

Origin / Lineage

The first line of descent comes from Breast Cancer Action and corporate pink-ribbon criticism. That original use targeted companies that wrapped themselves in breast-cancer concern while profiting from products linked to cancer risk.

The Israel-specific use emerged around 2010 in queer anti-Israel activism. Scholars and activists trace the term to Queers Undermining Israeli Terrorism and to Ali Abunimah's public use of pinkwashing language. Schulman's 2011 New York Times essay then gave the word a much wider audience. Her later book Israel/Palestine and the Queer International built the claim into a larger activist account.

A second line came through Jasbir Puar's work on homonationalism. In Terrorist Assemblages, Puar argued that some states incorporate certain gay subjects into national self-presentation while casting Muslim or racialized others as backward or dangerous. Antizionists used that claim to argue that Israel's gay-rights record served Israeli state power.

That academic route gave the term prestige. BDS campaigns gave it a method. Pride conflicts gave it visibility. After October 7, the word became a tool for policing queer Jewish and Israeli participation in public life.

Function

Pinkwashing lets activists treat Israeli LGBT rights as unusable evidence. It does not say, “Israel has no gay rights.” It says those rights should be read as concealment. That is stronger than denial. Denial can be corrected by facts. This term swallows the facts and turns them into proof of deceit.

It also prevents comparison. Same-sex conduct, LGBT organizing, Pride events, asylum, family recognition, and public safety vary sharply across the region. Israel's record is imperfect, and religious marriage law still prevents same-sex couples from marrying inside Israel. But Israel remains the one regional state with open Pride events, major LGBT organizations, legal protections, and courts that have repeatedly expanded LGBT family rights. Pinkwashing language often treats that comparison as offensive because the comparison weakens the preferred verdict.

The term also disciplines LGBT institutions. Pride groups, arts spaces, universities, unions, and NGOs can be pressured to exclude Israeli partners or Jewish groups unless they denounce Israel. This changes inclusion into a test. A movement built around LGBT belonging can end up telling LGBT Jews that they belong only if they cut themselves off from the Jewish state.

Notes

1. Pinkwashing is not always an empty claim. States use branding. Israel has promoted Tel Aviv, tourism, and LGBT rights abroad. Those campaigns can be criticized for selectivity, timing, or tone.

2. The key problem is the closed loop. If Israel lacks LGBT rights, the lack is used against it. If Israel has LGBT rights, the rights are used against it. That method does not evaluate facts. It neutralizes them.

3. Israel's LGBT record should be stated accurately. Same-sex marriage is not performed inside Israel because Israel lacks civil marriage and marriage is controlled by recognized religious bodies. Civil same-sex marriages performed abroad are registered. That is an important limit and should not be hidden.

4. The alQaws example should be kept precise. PA police announced a ban in 2019; after backlash, the ban was reportedly withdrawn. The initial police statement and the wave of threats that followed are the relevant facts.

5. The ILGA World case should also be kept precise. The Aguda was suspended in October 2024. ILGA World later announced that the suspension would be lifted effective October 27, 2025 after review.

6. The Rome Pride / Keshet Italia case is useful because it shows the demand moving onto a Jewish LGBT group outside Israel.

Responses / Criticisms

It turns real rights into evidence of deception.

A fair critic can say Israel promotes LGBT rights selectively. A fair critic can also say Israel has unresolved problems, especially around religious marriage and social hostility in some communities. Pinkwashing often goes further. It treats the existence of LGBT rights as a public-relations trap. That makes the claim impossible to satisfy.

It abandons LGBT Palestinians when their suffering complicates the slogan.

Palestinian LGBT people face danger from families, armed groups, police, social hostility, and rulers who do not protect them. A movement serious about queer safety would discuss those facts directly. Pinkwashing language often makes those facts unsayable because they help Israel by comparison.

It makes queer Jewish belonging conditional.

A Pride movement that bars Jewish LGBT groups unless they pass an Israel test has left inclusion behind. The issue is not whether Jews or Israelis may be criticized. They may be. The issue is whether LGBT Jews are treated as full participants or as suspects who must first confess on behalf of a state.
