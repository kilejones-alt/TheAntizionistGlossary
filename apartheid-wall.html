Definition

“Apartheid Wall” is the antizionist name for Israel’s West Bank security barrier, first approved by the Israeli cabinet in June 2002 during the Second Intifada. The barrier is not one continuous slab of concrete. It is a system of fences, concrete sections, patrol roads, ditches, razor wire, electronic monitoring, buffer zones, gates, and checkpoints. The slogan picks the most dramatic image and makes it stand for the whole thing. Calling it a wall does two jobs at once. It selects the most visually severe stretches, even though most of the line is fencing, and then welds on the word apartheid, turning a security barrier into a monument to racial supremacy. Into those two words the slogan folds the reason for construction, the legal fight over the route, the hardship imposed on Palestinians, and the separate question of Israel’s legitimacy—all collapsed into a single verdict. The barrier’s stated purpose was to stop Palestinian attackers from crossing out of the West Bank into Israeli population centers. That context is not an afterthought. In a 2004 Washington Institute brief, IDF Col. Zohar Palti wrote that before the northern West Bank section was finished, 35 successful suicide attacks could be traced to Nablus and Jenin, killing 156 Israeli civilians. After that section went up, attacks from the same area fell sharply. The barrier was not the only cause—intelligence work, arrests, and raids all mattered—but the claim that it had no serious security rationale does not survive the record. None of that settles every question about where the barrier runs. It does not erase Palestinian hardship, the loss of land access, the permit system, or the legal criticism aimed at sections built inside the West Bank. It means only that “Apartheid Wall” is not a neutral description. It is a conclusion handed over before the argument starts.

Common Usage

The phrase travels through campus demonstrations, solidarity campaigns, NGO materials, and institutional resolutions as a fixed compound noun: apartheid wall. Mock-wall installations have gone up at universities across North America, Europe, and Australia, often timed to Israeli Apartheid Week. These displays present the barrier as a South African echo, with little attention to the wave of suicide bombings that preceded it. On July 9, 2004, the International Court of Justice issued an advisory opinion in Legal Consequences of the Construction of a Wall in the Occupied Palestinian Territory. The Court found that the wall’s construction in occupied territory, including East Jerusalem, was contrary to international law, and it called on Israel to halt building, dismantle the sections already standing there, and make reparations for the damage. The vote was 14 to 1, with Judge Thomas Buergenthal dissenting. That opinion has become a standard citation in antizionist and BDS materials, invoked as though it ended the discussion. But it was an advisory opinion, not a contested-case judgment reached after full adversarial argument between two states. Israel rejected the proceeding, arguing that the Court lacked the factual record needed to weigh security necessity. Buergenthal’s dissent did not endorse the route; his objection was that the Court lacked the evidence to rule on the barrier’s legality as presented. In UN and NGO usage, the phrase often appears without quotation marks, treated as plain description rather than advocacy. That is the whole point. Once the phrase becomes ordinary, the argument buried inside it becomes ordinary too.

Implied Meaning

The phrase says Israel built the barrier not to stop attackers but to cage Palestinians on racial grounds. It asks the reader to see the structure as kin to South African apartheid infrastructure, which means pushing the Second Intifada out of the frame—even though the barrier was approved in the middle of a wave of suicide bombings and mass-casualty attacks. It also inherits the logic of the South Africa comparison. Afrikaner apartheid was not adjusted at the margins; it was dismantled. If Israel’s barrier is an apartheid wall, then the demanded remedy is not a revised route, compensation, a reformed gate system, or court-supervised proportionality. The phrase does not stop at where the barrier runs. It presents the barrier as a lesson in Israel’s nature, so that a security measure becomes proof that Jewish sovereignty is criminal in form, and a dispute over a map becomes an indictment of a state. The selectivity shows when you look next door. Egypt moved to fortify its own Gaza border after Hamas’s 2007 takeover and the January 2008 breach, when hundreds of thousands of Palestinians poured into Sinai. David Schenker reported in 2008 that Egypt, with 23 million dollars in U.S. assistance and expected help from the Army Corps of Engineers, planned its own Gaza-border barrier. Egyptian foreign minister Ahmed Aboul Gheit, after earlier Egyptian criticism of Israel’s barrier, said that whoever wishes to build a security fence on his own land is free to do so. Egypt’s barrier never became an “apartheid wall.” The contrast gives the game away: the phrase is not really about walls. It is about Israel.

Origin / Lineage

The phrase took hold after the barrier’s first construction phases in 2002 and 2003, but the language was already waiting. The same Cold War vocabulary traced in the previous entry—Soviet, Arab League, and Third World campaigns that fused Zionism with South African racism, hardened at Durban in 2001 into the charge of a “racist, apartheid state”—was sitting ready when the barrier rose. So when the West Bank line became visible in 2002 and 2003, antizionists already had a concrete image to bolt onto it. The barrier did not create the apartheid comparison. It gave the comparison a photograph. The 2004 ICJ advisory opinion then supplied legal prestige. Though the opinion addressed the legal consequences of the barrier in occupied territory, activist usage swept it into the broader apartheid claim. The result is a layered slogan: Cold War vocabulary, Durban-era NGO activism, ICJ legal language, and campus stagecraft fused into two words.

Function

The phrase works by compression. It takes a tangled dispute—terrorism, military necessity, land access, Israeli Supreme Court review, ICJ criticism, Palestinian hardship, the route itself—and crushes it down to two words that carry the verdict before any evidence is heard. In antizionist spaces it also works as a password. To say “Apartheid Wall” signals that you accept the South Africa analogy and the criminal charge riding on it. To say “security barrier,” “separation barrier,” or “fence” can be treated as denial or apologetics. The vocabulary sorts people before they have said anything else. And it shifts the argument from policy to criminality. If the barrier is an apartheid wall, then anyone who builds, defends, funds, or normalizes it is not merely wrong about security policy—they are complicit in a crime. That is why the phrase turns up so naturally beside calls for sanctions, boycotts, ICC action, divestment, and academic exclusion. It was never meant to improve a route map. It was meant to indict.

Notes

  1. The barrier is mostly a fenced security system, not a single concrete wall. OCHA describes it as concrete walls, fences, ditches, razor wire, sand paths, electronic monitoring, patrol roads, buffer zones, and checkpoints. The concrete sections are the most photographed because they are the most dramatic. The word “wall” makes the whole system sound uniform when it is not.
  2. The ICJ opinion is important but routinely overstated. It was advisory, not a judgment in a contested case decided after ordinary evidentiary proceedings. That does not make it meaningless. It does mean it cannot be cited as if it alone resolved every factual and military question.
  3. Israel’s Supreme Court has not treated the barrier as untouchable. In Beit Sourik Village Council v. Government of Israel (2004), the Court held that the military commander could not build the barrier for political annexation, accepted that security needs could justify construction, and found parts of the route disproportionate because of the harm to Palestinian residents—ordering them reconsidered. That record does not fit a phrase that treats the barrier as a uniform expression of racism.
  4. The barrier caused real Palestinian hardship. It restricted movement, cut into land access, imposed gate and permit burdens, and separated some communities from their farmland and services. A serious defense of Israel does not need to deny any of that. It needs to explain why the barrier was built, how its route should be judged, and why a security measure in a violent national conflict is not the same thing as Afrikaner racial rule.

Responses / Criticisms

It hides the reason the barrier was built. The phrase opens with the physical object and skips the attacks that produced it. That omission matters. The barrier was approved during the Second Intifada, after repeated suicide bombings inside Israeli cities. A serious argument can attack the route, the permit system, and the hardship imposed on Palestinians. It cannot treat the barrier as if it rose out of nothing.

It turns a route dispute into an existential verdict. The strongest criticism of the barrier concerns where it runs, what land it isolates, and how it cuts into Palestinian movement and property. “Apartheid Wall” converts those questions into a judgment about Israel’s character. It does not ask whether a given section should be moved. It tells the reader what Israel is. It applies a standard reserved for Israel. Egypt fortified its Gaza border for security reasons and never became a global symbol of apartheid. Other states build barriers, fences, and border defenses without having the legitimacy of the state itself put on trial. Israel’s barrier draws a different kind of language, and that difference is the slogan’s whole purpose.
