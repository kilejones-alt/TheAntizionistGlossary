Apartheid

Definition

In South Africa, apartheid was a race-based legal order that governed the country from 1948 to 1994. White minority rule rested on formal segregation and pass laws. Racial classification decided where a person could live and work. Black South Africans were denied the vote and removed from their land by force. The Afrikaans word means "separateness." That history is specific. It is documented and legally defined. Modern antizionism lifts the word out of its South African setting and aims it at Israel. This is no ordinary criticism. It equates the Jewish state with one of the most condemned governments in modern history. Checkpoints and barriers become Israel's pass laws. Military administration became Afrikaner race law and relocated to the Middle East. Security measures born of armed conflict become proof of racial supremacy. The judgment precedes the trial.

The comparison survives only by flattening nearly eight decades of Israeli history. It ignores the Oslo II Accord of 1995, which Israeli and Palestinian leaders signed and which divided the West Bank into Areas A, B, and C. It grows especially strained inside Israel's pre-1967 borders. Arab citizens vote in national elections and serve in the Knesset. They sit as judges and teach in universities. They practice medicine and build businesses. Israel's own government statistics counted 2.157 million Arab citizens in April 2026 — 21.1 percent of the country. Grafting Afrikaner rule onto a dispute over land and sovereignty also blurs a basic distinction: it conflates Palestinians in the territories with Israel's Arab citizens. The charge pushes borders and terrorism out of view, along with rejected negotiations and the record of Palestinian leadership. One test separates antizionism from criticism of Israeli policy. Criticism names a policy and proposes an alternative; demonization condemns the state itself. Calling Israel an apartheid state fails that test. It indicates no specific law and offers no workable remedy.

Common Usage

The word is now standard fare on campuses and in NGO reports. It circulates through activist coalitions and UN bodies. Israeli Apartheid Week began in Toronto in 2005, organized by the Arab Students' Collective at the University of Toronto. It has since grown into an international event tied to boycott and divestment campaigns. Its programming runs from speakers and panels to mock walls and student-government resolutions. Major human rights organizations have adopted the charge in formal legal reports. Human Rights Watch's A Threshold Crossed (2021) accused Israeli authorities of the crimes of apartheid and persecution. Amnesty International's Israel's Apartheid Against Palestinians (2022) went further. It applied the accusation to everything at once — Israel proper and East Jerusalem, the West Bank and Gaza, even Palestinian refugees abroad. That sweep assumes its theory a priori. It sounds comprehensive. In practice it blurs every relevant distinction: citizen and resident, occupied territory and Hamas-run enclave, Israeli law and refugee status abroad.

Within the United Nations the word appears in official reports and speeches as settled fact. The Human Rights Council uses it. So does the office of the Special Rapporteur on the Situation of Human Rights in the Palestinian Territories. The case is treated as already proven. On campus the word fuels divestment resolutions and faculty open letters. It powers campaigns to exclude Israeli scholars. It hands antizionists a single noun that does triple duty: criminal accusation, historical analogy, demand for punishment. That is why it travels so well.

Implied Meaning

One study of the Second Intifada counted 148 suicide bombers by July 2005. Their attacks killed 657 people and injured 3,682. The West Bank separation barrier rose during those years. Its stated and documented purpose was to stop bombers from reaching Israeli population centers. Racial separation had nothing to do with it. The charge also forgets what followed Israel's withdrawal from Gaza in August 2005. Israel dismantled every settlement and pulled out every soldier. Hamas and other armed groups answered with rockets. Human Rights Watch counted roughly 2,700 Qassam-type rockets between September 2005 and May 2007. By January 2009 the U.S. Congressional Record put the figure above 6,300 rockets and mortars aimed at Israeli population centers since the withdrawal. An apartheid regime does not evacuate territory; a state under fire defends itself. The accusation cannot account for this sequence of events.

The deeper implication comes from the analogy itself. The international consensus on South African apartheid held that the system could not be reformed — only dismantled. Transfer that judgment to Israel and the same logic follows. If Israel is a racist state by nature, no policy adjustment will do. The only acceptable remedy becomes the end of Jewish sovereignty. Once accepted, the analogy confirms itself. A new barrier becomes fresh proof. So does a checkpoint or an emergency regulation. Every security measure gets folded back into the original charge, which makes the claim impossible to test. David Hirsh identifies this circularity in Contemporary Left Antisemitism as a recurring habit of antizionist argument. The word becomes less a conclusion than an engine for generating new ones.

Origin / Lineage

The Israel-apartheid comparison has a paper trail. It predates the NGO reports of the 2020s and the BDS campaign of 2005. Its roots lie in Cold War diplomacy conducted by the Soviet bloc, the Arab League, and their Third World allies. The goal was Israel's international isolation. Campus activism popularized the language later; it did not invent it. The first recorded use came with a twist of irony. In 1961, South African prime minister Hendrik Verwoerd — an architect of apartheid itself — reportedly accused Israel of practicing it. Israel had just voted at the United Nations to condemn South Africa's racial policies. Antizionists later inverted that moment and turned the accusation into a standing weapon. In 1965, Fayez Sayegh's pamphlet Zionist Colonialism in Palestine, published by the PLO Research Center, described Zionism as a racially exclusive settler-colonial project. It compared the movement to European imperial ventures and handed later activists a ready-made script.

The Soviet Union turned the charge into a Cold War export aimed at newly independent African and Arab states. The campaign bore fruit at the UN. General Assembly Resolution 3151G (1973) condemned what it called the "unholy alliance between Portuguese colonialism, South African racism, Zionism and Israeli imperialism." Two years later came Resolution 3379, the most notorious product of the effort. It declared that "Zionism is a form of racism and racial discrimination." The General Assembly revoked it in 1991. The language it normalized never left. The modern legal version took shape at the 2001 World Conference Against Racism in Durban, South Africa. The NGO Forum declaration there called Israel a "racist, apartheid state" and demanded measures modeled on the campaign against South Africa. The BDS movement, launched in 2005, drew much of its legal arsenal from that document. Over the next two decades, activists and NGOs worked the 1973 Apartheid Convention and the 's 1998 definition of the crime into briefs against Israel. By the time international courts took up related claims in the 2020s, the groundwork was six decades deep.

Function

In academic life the word doubles as a loyalty test. Jewish students and scholars who maintain any tie to Israel encounter it as a demand for denunciation. That demand reaches the majority of diaspora Jews, for whom Israel is no abstraction but a part of family, faith, and communal memory. The accusation also yields a curated account of the conflict. It dwells on Israeli power while saying little about Hamas rule in Gaza or Palestinian Authority administration in the West Bank. Rejected peace offers and the security conditions Israel faced barely appear. This selectivity has a purpose. If Israel is a racist state, dead Israeli civilians become casualties of the system instead of victims of murder. After October 7, 2023, that logic surfaced in the open. Antizionists in several Western cities described the Hamas massacres not as atrocities but as resistance against an illegitimate state. The apartheid charge had prepared the ground for those words to be spoken aloud.

Notes

The charge sits uneasily beside lived reality inside Israel. Arab citizens made up 21.1 percent of the population according to Israel's April 2026 Independence Day statistics. They vote in national elections and serve in the Knesset. They hold posts throughout the judiciary; Justice Khaled Kabub, an Israeli Arab Muslim judge, joined the Supreme Court in 2022. Arab citizenship is a standing counterexample to the accusation — which is why the argument usually hurries on to the West Bank and Gaza. Israel has genuine internal inequalities. Economic gaps and policing disparities are real and documented. So are planning disputes and unresolved social tensions involving Arab citizens. None of this resembles the racial machinery of Afrikaner rule. Conflating the two does a disservice both to the victims of South African apartheid and to the complexity of Israeli civic life.

Responses / Criticisms

NGO and legal briefs often collapse distinct situations into a single system: Israel proper and East Jerusalem, the West Bank and Gaza, refugees abroad. Each carries its own legal and diplomatic arrangements. Palestinians in Areas A and B live under Palestinian Authority jurisdiction created by the Oslo accords. Gaza has been governed by Hamas since 2007. A resident of Ramallah does not vote in Israeli elections because he is not an Israeli citizen under Israeli civil law — not because Israel sorts him by race. The accusation consistently omits Palestinian governance. Israel is held responsible for every condition of Palestinian life while Hamas and the Palestinian Authority recede into the background. Since October 7, 2023, the asymmetry has grown harder to miss. Organizations demanding Israeli accountability under this heading have had far less to say about Hamas's treatment of Gaza's own civilian population.
