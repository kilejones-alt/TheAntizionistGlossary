Apartheid

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Apartheid” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Definition

In modern antizionism, apartheid is lifted out of its South African setting and turned into one of the central slurs against Israel. In South Africa, apartheid was a race-based legal order that lasted from 1948 to 1994. It preserved white minority rule through formal segregation, pass laws, racial classification, civic exclusion, forced removals, and the denial of rights to the black majority. The Afrikaans word means “separateness.” That history is specific, documented, and legally defined.

Applied to Israel, apartheid is not ordinary criticism. It is a comparison to one of the most condemned systems of rule in modern history. Checkpoints, barriers, military administration, and policing become Israel’s version of Afrikaner race law. Security measures developed during armed conflict become proof of racial supremacy. The conclusion arrives before the history is examined.

The comparison works only by flattening Israel’s legal, historical, and security record since 1948. It ignores the Oslo II Accord of 1995, which divided the West Bank into Areas A, B, and C through agreements signed by Israeli and Palestinian leadership. It becomes especially strained when applied to Israel inside its pre-1967 borders. Arab citizens vote in national elections and serve in the Knesset. They sit as judges, practice medicine, teach in universities, and participate in civil society as citizens. Israel’s own government statistics put the Arab population at 2.157 million, or 21.1 percent of the total population, in April 2026.

By grafting South Africa’s racist government onto a conflict involving land disputes and political legitimacy, antizionists conflate those who live in Palestine with Israeli citizens. They also ignore the complexities Israel faces. The label pushes aside borders, terrorism, rejected negotiations, and Palestinian leaders. It invites the reader to accept the conclusion before weighing everything before. One of the criteria that distinguishes antizionism from criticism of Israeli policy, is accepting conclusions that demonize an entire state while not pointing to specific policies it is against or provide other viable options. To describe Israel as an apartheid state meets this criteria because it is not a criticism of policy.

Common Usage

The word has become standard vocabulary across campuses, NGO reports, coalitions, and UN bodies. Israeli Apartheid Week began in Toronto in 2005, organized by the Arab Students’ Collective at the University of Toronto. It later became an international event tied to boycott and divestment campaigns. Its events often feature speakers, mock wall installations, symbolic performances, and student-government resolutions.

Major human rights organizations have adopted the label in detailed legal reports. Human Rights Watch’s 2021 report, A Threshold Crossed, accused Israeli authorities of the crimes of apartheid and persecution. Amnesty International’s 2022 report, Israel’s Apartheid Against Palestinians, applied the charge across Israel proper, East Jerusalem, the West Bank, Gaza, and Palestinian refugees. That move sweeps distinct legal and factual concerns under a theory that is assumed apriori. It sounds comprehensive, but it blurs major differences between citizens, residents, noncitizens, occupied territories, Hamas-controlled Gaza, and diaspora refugees.

Within the UN, including the Human Rights Council and the office of the Special Rapporteur on the Situation of Human Rights in the Palestinian Territories, the label appears in official reports and speeches as a settled fact. It is often treated as though the case has already been proven. On campuses, the word fuels divestment resolutions, faculty open letters, and calls for academic targeting of Israeli scholars. It gives antizionists a noun that can carry a criminal claim, a South African analogy, and a demand for punishment. That is why it travels so well.

Implied Meaning

A study of suicide bombing in the Second Intifada counted 148 suicide bombers by July 2005, responsible for 657 deaths and 3,682 injuries. The West Bank separation barrier was built during this period. Its stated and documented purpose was to reduce the movement of suicide bombers into Israeli population centers, not to enforce racial separation.

The apartheid pejorative forgets what happened after Israel’s full withdrawal from Gaza in August 2005. Israel dismantled every settlement and removed every soldier. Hamas and other armed groups continued firing rockets into Israeli towns. Human Rights Watch reported about 2,700 Qassam-type rockets from September 2005 through May 2007. The U.S. Congressional Record in January 2009 referred to more than 6,300 rockets and mortars fired at Israeli population centers since Israel’s withdrawal. The apartheid label runs contrary to this sequence of events.

The deeper implication follows from the South Africa comparison itself. The international consensus on South African apartheid was that the system could not be reformed. It had to be dismantled. Transferring that label to Israel carries the same logic. If Israel is a racist state, the demanded answer is not policy adjustment. The answer becomes removal of the conditions that allow the state to exist as Jewish sovereign power.

The analogy also tends to confirm itself once accepted. A barrier or checkpoint can be redescribed as further proof of the same claim. So can a military operation, police measure, citizenship dispute, or emergency regulation. The claim becomes difficult to test because each Israeli security action is folded back into the same claim. David Hirsh identifies this pattern in Contemporary Left Antisemitism as a recurring feature of contemporary antizionist argument. The label becomes less a conclusion than a machine for producing more conclusions.

Origin / Lineage

The Israel-apartheid comparison has a traceable institutional history. It predates the NGO reports of the 2020s and the BDS campaign of 2005. Its lineage runs through Soviet, Arab League, and Third World diplomatic campaigns during the Cold War. The main aim was Israel’s international isolation. The vocabulary did not begin on campus, even though campus activism later made it familiar to a wider audience.

The comparison appeared in international diplomacy before it became a campus slogan. In 1961, South African prime minister Hendrik Verwoerd reportedly accused Israel of practicing apartheid after Israel voted to criticize South Africa’s racial policies at the United Nations. The irony was difficult to miss. Antizionists later inverted that moment and turned the charge into a recurring weapon against Israel. In 1965, Fayez Sayegh’s pamphlet Zionist Colonialism in Palestine, published by the PLO Research Center, described Zionism as a racially exclusive settler-colonial project. It compared Zionism to European imperial enterprises and gave later activists a ready vocabulary.

The Soviet Union helped turn the claim into a Cold War export. The campaign targeted newly independent African and Arab nations. It helped produce UN General Assembly Resolution 3151G in 1973, which condemned what it called the “unholy alliance between Portuguese colonialism, South African racism, Zionism and Israeli imperialism.” Two years later came Resolution 3379, the most famous result of this campaign. That resolution declared that “Zionism is a form of racism and racial discrimination.” It was revoked in 1991, but the vocabulary it normalized did not disappear.

The modern legal version took shape at the 2001 World Conference Against Racism in Durban, South Africa. The NGO Forum declaration called Israel a “racist, apartheid state” and demanded measures modeled on the campaign against South Africa. That gave the BDS movement, launched in 2005, much of its legal vocabulary. Over the following two decades, and NGOs worked the 1973 Apartheid Convention and the 1998 Rome Statute’s definition of the crime of apartheid into arguments against Israel. By the time international courts began addressing related claims in the 2020s, the groundwork had been built over six decades.

Function

The word moves antizionist campaigns into the language of international criminal law. In that register, classification brings consequences: sanctions, diplomatic isolation, arrest warrants, business boycotts, academic exclusion, and professional punishment. This is the vocabulary of disqualification, not negotiation. Legal scholar Eugene Kontorovich has analyzed the strategic use of international law against Israel in this way. Once the language becomes criminal, Israel is put in the dock and told to defend its right to exist.

In academic spaces, the word also works like a loyalty test. Jewish students, scholars, artists, and professionals who maintain any connection to Israel encounter it as a demand for denunciation. That demand applies to the majority of diaspora Jews, for whom Israel is not an abstraction but part of communal, familial, religious, or historical life. The label also produces a selective account of the conflict. It focuses on Israeli authority and Israeli action while saying much less about Hamas rule in Gaza, Palestinian Authority administration in the West Bank, rejected diplomatic settlements, or the security conditions to which Israel was responding.

This selectivity is not incidental. If Israel is a racist state, then Israeli civilian deaths can be treated as consequences of that system not as murders committed by armed attackers. After October 7, 2023, this logic appeared in explicit form. Antizionists in several Western cities described the Hamas massacres not as atrocities, but as resistance against an illegitimate state. The apartheid label helped create the background in which that response could be spoken out loud.

Notes

1. The apartheid label sits uneasily with the lived reality inside Israel. Arab citizens constitute 21.1 percent of the population according to Israel’s April 2026 Independence Day statistics. They vote in national elections, serve in the Knesset, and hold positions across the judiciary. Justice Khaled Kabub, an Israeli Arab Muslim judge, joined Israel’s Supreme Court in 2022. Arab citizenship inside Israel is a counterindicating fact for the apartheid charge, which is why the discussion often moves quickly to the West Bank and Gaza.

2. Israel has genuine internal inequalities. These include economic gaps, policing disparities, planning disputes, and unresolved social tensions involving Arab citizens. Those issues are real and documented. They are not equivalent to the racial machinery of Afrikaner rule. Conflating them does a disservice to both the victims of South African apartheid and the complexity of Israeli civic life.

3. NGO and legal arguments often collapse Israel proper, East Jerusalem, the West Bank, Gaza, and Palestinian refugees into a single system. These areas have distinct legal, military, and diplomatic arrangements. Palestinians in Areas A and B live under Palestinian Authority jurisdiction created by the Oslo accords. Gaza has been governed by Hamas since 2007. Palestinians in Ramallah or Gaza City do not vote in Israeli elections because they are not Israeli citizens living under Israeli civil law, not because Israel classifies them by race.

4. The label consistently omits the question of Palestinian rule and legal authority. It holds Israel responsible for conditions in Palestinian life while treating Hamas and the Palestinian Authority as secondary or irrelevant. Since October 7, 2023, this asymmetry has become more visible. The same organizations invoking this word to call for Israeli accountability have had far less to say about Hamas’s conduct toward Gaza’s own civilian population.

Responses / Criticisms

It treats a national conflict as a racial caste system.

South African apartheid was a domestic racial order built to preserve white minority rule over a disenfranchised black majority. The Israeli-Palestinian conflict is not that. It involves two national movements, repeated wars, terrorism, failed negotiations, partial Palestinian self-rule, Hamas rule in Gaza, and unresolved territorial claims. That does not make every Israeli policy defensible. It means the South African model cannot simply be dropped onto the conflict without distorting it.

It treats the security record like an inconvenient footnote.

The apartheid label usually begins with Israeli control and skips the violence that shaped Israeli policy. That shortcut matters. The Second Intifada and suicide bombings are not minor background details. Rocket fire from Gaza is not a minor background detail either. Failed negotiations are not minor background details. These facts help explain why Israeli voters supported checkpoints, barriers, and military action. A serious argument can criticize those measures. It cannot pretend they emerged from nothing.

It turns criticism of Israeli policy into opposition to Israel’s existence.

Criticizing governments is normal and legitimate. People criticize Israeli governments every day, including inside Israel. Saying that Jewish self-rule is racist by nature is different. That claim does not target one policy or one coalition. It does not target one law or one court decision. It tells the reader that the state itself belongs beside Afrikaner rule. Once that premise is accepted, Israel’s existence becomes debatable.
