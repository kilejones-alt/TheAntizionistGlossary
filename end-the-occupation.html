End the Occupation

Definition

“End the occupation” is an antizionist demand to terminate Israeli control beyond the 1949 armistice lines, especially in the West Bank and East Jerusalem. In its narrower use, the phrase refers to Israeli military administration, settlements, checkpoints, barriers, and security control after the Six-Day War of June 1967. In its broader use, it often expands until Israel itself becomes the occupation.

In international humanitarian law, occupation is usually defined through effective control. Article 42 of the 1907 Hague Regulations states that territory is occupied when it is “actually placed under the authority of the hostile army.” The Fourth Geneva Convention also addresses cases of partial or total occupation. The International Committee of the Red Cross, the United Nations, and the International Court of Justice apply this law to the West Bank, including East Jerusalem, and the ICJ’s July 19, 2024 advisory opinion declared Israel’s continued presence in the occupied Palestinian territory unlawful. That is the dominant international position.

Israel disputes the de jure application of that label while generally accepting that humanitarian rules apply in practice. The Israeli position rests on the prior status of the territory: Jordan controlled and annexed the West Bank after the 1948 war, but that annexation was recognized by only a small number of states. Israel captured the West Bank from Jordan in June 1967 after Jordan entered the war. On that account, the territory was not taken from a recognized sovereign and is better described as disputed territory whose final status must be decided by agreement. The Oslo II Accord of 1995, signed by Israel and the PLO, divided the West Bank into Areas A, B, and C and left permanent-status issues for negotiation. The slogan usually skips that treaty record.

Common Usage

The phrase appears in BDS materials, campus divestment resolutions, faculty letters, NGO reports, and protest chants. The 2005 Palestinian civil-society BDS call demands that Israel end “its occupation and colonization of all Arab lands and dismantle the Wall.” That wording is often presented as a post-1967 demand. But the BDS call also demands full equality for Arab-Palestinian citizens of Israel and a right of return for Palestinian refugees. Those additional demands show that the campaign is not limited to the West Bank. It treats Israel’s whole national settlement as the target of pressure.

The phrase also appears in arguments denying Israel the right to defend itself. At a November 2023 American Muslims for Palestine convention in Chicago, CAIR Executive Director Nihad Awad said, “Israel, as an occupying power, does not have that right to self-defense.” Awad later said his remarks were about international law and that targeting civilians is never acceptable. Even so, the line shows how the occupation label can be used: once Israel is classified as an occupier, its military response to Hamas is described as suppression of resistance, not defense of civilians.

The slogan also travels with the memory it omits. At the Arab League summit in Khartoum on September 1, 1967, shortly after Israel captured the West Bank, Gaza, Sinai, and the Golan Heights, Arab leaders declared “no peace with Israel, no recognition of Israel, no negotiations with it.” That statement came before the settlement enterprise took its later form. Israel’s June 1967 cabinet discussions included willingness to return Sinai and the Golan in exchange for peace with Egypt and Syria, while the West Bank question remained unresolved. The slogan turns this diplomatic history into a simpler story: Israel occupies; therefore Israel must leave.

Implied Meaning

In its narrow form, “end the occupation” implies that Israeli withdrawal from the West Bank would resolve the conflict or at least remove its central cause. That argument has to answer the Gaza precedent. In 2005, Israel dismantled all 21 settlements in Gaza and removed its soldiers from the Strip. The IDF completed its evacuation of bases and personnel on September 12, 2005, and Israel formally cancelled its military government there. International bodies still dispute whether Gaza remained occupied because of Israeli control over airspace, sea access, border crossings, and population registry. That dispute matters. But the security result also matters: after withdrawal came Hamas’s 2006 election win, its 2007 takeover of Gaza, increased rocket fire, and the build-up that preceded October 7.

The lesson is not that withdrawal is impossible. It is that withdrawal without demilitarization, enforceable security guarantees, and a durable agreement can produce a hostile armed enclave. Gaza is the warning case. A slogan that demands exit without answering who governs, who disarms Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad, who controls borders, and who prevents rearmament is not a peace plan. It is an instruction with the hard parts removed.

In its broader form, the phrase implies that Israel’s existence is the occupation. This is why it so often appears beside “from the river to the sea.” The issue then is not where the 1967 lines should fall, but whether Jewish sovereignty is legitimate in any part of the land. Ernest Bevin put the older dispute plainly in the House of Commons on February 18, 1947: for the Jews, the essential point was a sovereign Jewish state; for the Arabs, the essential point was to resist Jewish sovereignty “in any part of Palestine.” That was before Israel, before settlements, before the West Bank came under Israeli rule, and before Gaza came under Israeli rule. The later occupation vocabulary gives an older rejection a newer legal sound.

Origin / Lineage

The legal language of occupation comes from the law of armed conflict, not from antizionism. The slogan’s antizionist use developed after 1967, when Arab states, Soviet propaganda networks, UN forums, and Western activist circles made the territories captured in the Six-Day War the center of anti-Israel campaigning. UN Security Council Resolution 242 created the familiar land-for-peace formula: Israeli withdrawal “from territories occupied in the recent conflict” together with termination of belligerency and respect for every state’s right to live in peace within secure and recognized boundaries. Antizionist usage keeps the withdrawal language and often drops the reciprocal peace requirement.

The Soviet and Arab diplomatic campaign helped connect occupation to colonialism, racism, and later apartheid. UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 in 1975 declared Zionism a form of racism; the 2001 Durban NGO Forum later gave boycott and apartheid rhetoric a wider activist platform. The occupation slogan did not originate in Durban, but Durban helped fold it into the same vocabulary as settler-colonialism, apartheid, and BDS.

The legal dispute over the Fourth Geneva Convention has also shaped the phrase. The ICJ, ICRC, UN bodies, and many governments treat the West Bank, including East Jerusalem, as occupied territory and treat the Fourth Geneva Convention as applicable. Israel has long argued that the territory is sui generis because no recognized sovereign was displaced in 1967, while saying that it observes humanitarian provisions in practice. This disagreement does not make every Israeli policy wise or defensible. It means the slogan hides a serious legal dispute behind a command.

Eugene Kontorovich and other critics have also argued that Israel receives a unique form of occupation-related treatment compared with other disputed or occupied territories, including Western Sahara, northern Cyprus, Nagorno-Karabakh, Abkhazia, and Crimea. That comparative argument does not settle the West Bank question. It does show why the word has become so important in antizionist language: once attached to Israel, it triggers a set of demands rarely applied with the same intensity elsewhere.

Function

The phrase makes Israeli presence sound illegal before security, treaty language, or historical evidence is considered. It moves the argument from borders and terms to guilt and exit. Once occupation is accepted in its most expansive sense, Israeli soldiers are not defending Israelis; they are enforcing domination. Israeli civilians are not living in a contested national conflict; they are settlers by definition.

In campus and professional settings, the slogan works as a gateway demand. It justifies divestment, academic boycott, disruption, exclusion, and institutional loyalty tests. People are not asked merely whether they support a Palestinian state. They are asked whether they accept the premise that Israel’s presence is criminal and that normal relations with Israelis should end until the demand is satisfied.

It also erases rejected offers and failed negotiations. The Clinton Parameters in December 2000 proposed a Palestinian state in Gaza and roughly 94-96% of the West Bank, with land swaps. The Taba talks in January 2001 proceeded from those ideas but ended without agreement. Later offers and talks likewise failed over borders, security, refugees, Jerusalem, recognition, and the end-of-claims issue. The slogan compresses that record into a single demand and leaves the reader with one responsible party.

Notes

1. The dominant international legal position is that the West Bank, including East Jerusalem, is occupied territory and that the Fourth Geneva Convention applies. The ICJ said so in its 2004 wall advisory opinion and again in its 2024 advisory opinion. This entry does not deny that position. It argues that antizionist use of the phrase often exceeds that legal point and turns a disputed territorial question into a demand against Jewish sovereignty itself.

2. Israel’s position is not that no law applies. Israel’s long-standing view is that the Fourth Geneva Convention does not apply de jure because the territory was not taken from a recognized sovereign, while humanitarian provisions are applied de facto. That is why “disputed territory” appears in Israeli legal argument.

3. The Gaza precedent is often mishandled by both sides. Anti-Israel advocates say Gaza remained occupied after 2005 because of Israeli control around the territory. Israel’s defenders say Israel withdrew every soldier and settlement. Both points can be true in their own categories. The first concerns legal control; the second concerns physical withdrawal. The security result remains relevant to any proposed West Bank withdrawal.

4. UN Security Council Resolution 242 is frequently shortened into a demand for Israeli withdrawal. Its text also requires termination of belligerency and acknowledgment of every state’s right to live in peace within secure and recognized boundaries. The land-for-peace formula contains two sides. The slogan usually remembers only one.

Responses / Criticisms

It turns a territorial dispute into a verdict against Israel itself.

A serious discussion can ask what Israel should do in the West Bank, where borders should be drawn, how settlements should be handled, and what security arrangements are required. The slogan often skips those questions. It treats Israeli presence as self-evidently criminal and then presents withdrawal as the only possible answer.

It ignores the security record after withdrawal.

Gaza is not a footnote. Israel removed every settlement and soldier from the Strip in 2005. Hamas then consolidated power, built an armed enclave, fired rockets, and eventually carried out the October 7 massacres. Any demand for West Bank withdrawal has to answer how that result would be prevented beside Israel’s major population centers.

It often hides the maximalist version inside the moderate one.

Many listeners hear “end the occupation” as a demand to leave the West Bank. The ambiguity is useful. It lets the phrase sound like support for a two-state outcome while functioning, in many settings, as a demand for the end of the Jewish state.
