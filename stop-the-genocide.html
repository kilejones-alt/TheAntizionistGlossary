Stop the Genocide

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Stop the Genocide” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Stop The Genocide

WITH

Zionism (I, II, III)

INTRODUCTION

This glossary is written almost three years after the October 7, 2023 massacres in southern Israel, and the international response that followed. In this period, a recognizable set of terms, phrases, and slogans has spread rapidly across media platforms, colleges, and public spaces. Much of the language appears contemporary, when in fact, it can be followed to specific origins. Most of it has traceable historical precedents with identifiable authors, various channels of transmission, and other recognizable characteristics. Some, so incredibly obvious as to remove deniability–take no more than a few seconds to understand the conditions under which they were created. This history matters because the vocabulary did not arrive as a single dictionary might. It accumulated in layers, each one translating older slanders and tropes into the preferred language of the time.

A large portion of modern-day terminology directed at Israel reflects earlier social and intellectual interests. These include Soviet state publications, Arab nationalist organizations, Islamist ideas, Iranian messaging, the Western left, the Global South, and academic endeavors adopted by Universities. Distribution has occurred through media, conferences, publications, student organizations, and regional broadcasters. Some of them are even traceable to state-sponsored programs like Qatar's Al-Jazeera network, Iran’s Islamic Radio and Television Union, and Hamas-owned Al-Aqsa TV. The vocabulary itself largely predates the current war–in fact, depending on what lines you trace, it can go as far back as 1881.

Interestingly enough, one of the first non-Jewish anti-zionist writings was composed in good faith. In 1899, Yousef al-Khalidi, former mayor of Jerusalem, who—out of worry that violence might develop upon the arrival of the Zionists—wrote to Zadok Kahn, the chief rabbi of France. He stated that because of the “brutal force of circumstances” under the Ottoman Empire, Zionism would have “no hope of realisation” in Palestine. Al-Khalidi refers to Zionism as “natural, beautiful, and just” because, asked ironically, “who can dispute the rights of the Jews to Palestine?” Anti-Zionist in a very limited sense, but not in any other. Modern antizionism developed from sources across overlapping European and Middle Eastern state campaigns, political organizations, and publishing houses.

The first major modern layer was Soviet. If we were to begin anywhere, in terms of sheer number and influence, we would start with the Soviets. In the Russian Empire, The Protocols of the Elders of Zion (1903) circulated claims of coordinated Jewish global control and conspiracy; later messaging replaced religious wording with secular terminology, while preserving the content. After World War II, Joseph Stalin initially supported the UN Partition Plan and recognized Israel in May 1948. Partly to limit British influence and partly hoping the state–who’s foundation was influenced by leftists like Russian Marxist Ber Borochov and his Labor Zionism, Lenin-admirer David Ben-Gurion and his socialist Mapai party, and Ze’ev Jabotinsky–would side with Eastern Bloc communism. By the early 1950s, Soviet policy reversed.

The Slánský Trial in Czechoslovakia, and the Doctor’s Plot in Moscow merged state anti-Zionist with conspiracies of Zionism being a satellite for the Western world. It brought about accusations of dual loyalty, and in its paranoia, saw Jews as potential saboteurs, traitors, and spies for the enemy–the proverbial "fifth column." Stalin’s Jew-hatred, it seemed, could not be detoured.

Following Israel's victory in the Six-Days War (1967), the Soviet Union severed diplomatic relations and intensified messaging depicting Zionism as racist, expansionist, and aligned with American power. Trofim Kichko's Judaism Without Embellishment (1963), a modernized reworking of The Protocols, indicted Jews in a worldwide banking conspiracy using Nazi-imagery, grounding their supposed “subversive behavior” in Judaic religious beliefs, had already laid the groundwork for this. Yuri Ivanov’s Beware! Zionism (1969), circulated widely in Eastern Bloc distribution networks, similarly presenting Zionism as an all-encompassing sinister force.

Disinformation programs, including the KGB's “Operation SIG” in the 1970s, promoted similar claims internationally. Soviet lobbying contributed to the passage of UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 (1975), which declared Zionism "a form of racism and racial discrimination," before its repeal in 1991. The explosion of antizionism since October 7th has led the British Green Party recently to voting on whether to adopt “Zionism is Racism” as an official position. The past seems to repeat, and there just might be nothing new under the sun.

A second layer developed in the Arab world. During the British Mandate period, organized opposition to Jewish immigration and the UN Partition Plan consolidated into regional movements. Haj Amin al-Husseini–the grand Mufti of Jerusalem–emerged as a central political and religious figure linking Palestinian leadership to broader Arab nationalism.

Arab anti-Zionism was also shaped by a canon of translations, pamphlets, speeches, and political tracts that supplied much of its later language. Among the earliest and most influential was the prior mentioned Protocols, translated into Arabic in the early twentieth century and widely circulated by the 1920s. Imported from Tsarist Russia, the forgery claimed Jews secretly manipulated governments, finance, revolutions, and wars. Its themes proved influential enough that Gamal Abdel Nasser later recommended it as a useful guide to the “Jewish mind,” while later Arab rulers from Anwar Sadat, King Faisal, and Muammar Qaddafi treated it with seriousness. A Russian secret-police fabrication had entered the Arab world’s political minds, and was treated as genuine.

During the interwar years, these conspiratorial motifs fused with older Islamic and Christian anti-Jewish traditions, producing a distinctly Middle Eastern theory of Zionism described as both a local threat and a global force. The Palestinian educator Khalil Sakakini wrote in the 1930s that Britain had fallen “under Jewish sway” and depicted Zionism as a philosophy of betrayal and domination. Rashid Hajj Ibrahim warned in the late 1940s that Jewish ambitions extended far beyond Palestine into Egypt, Syria, Iraq, and the Hijaz, anticipating later claims of perpetual Zionist expansion. After the Arab defeat in 1948, Muhammad Nimr al-Khatib argued that the Arabs had not lost to local Jews, but to “world Zionism,” which supposedly controlled Truman, Churchill, London, New York, and Washington.

Fayez Sayegh’s Zionist Colonialism in Palestine (1965), issued through the Palestine Liberation Organization’s research program, described Zionism as a European settler-colonial implant. These ideas moved from pamphlets into ministries, classrooms, newspapers, and forums. After Israel's establishment, and particularly after the Six-Day War, multiple Arab governments formally defined Zionism as “colonial aggression.” The Khartoum Resolution of 1967 declared the "three no's": no peace with Israel, no recognition, and no negotiations. The Palestine Liberation Organization's original charter denied Jewish national legitimacy and classified Zionism as colonial and racist.

In Lebanon, the Cairo Agreement authorized Palestinian armed operations from Lebanese areas, contributing to long-term regional armed-struggles. The Lebanon War coincided with the emergence of Hezbollah, whose Open Letter explicitly called for the destruction of Israel. In Syria, President Hafez al-Assad incorporated anti-Zionism into Baʿathist state and military philosophy, including participation in the Yom Kippur War.

A third layer came through revolutionary Iran. Iran's Islamic Revolution, under Ayatollah Khomeini, transformed opposition to Israel into a state principle. Its annual "Quds Day" events are proof of this. Former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei repeatedly described Israel as illegitimate and called for its destruction in official speeches. The Middle East during the 1960s, up to the Iranian Revolution, had almost entirely turned against the nation-state of Israel. Khamenei was recently killed by a joint US-Israeli airstrike that began what we are currently calling the Iran War.

A fourth layer moved through the Western left and the academy. From the late 1960s onward, the Western New Left movement used post-colonial Arab literature as sources for their anti-imperialism and revolutionary culture. Works such as Frantz Fanon’s The Wretched of the Earth (1961), and Edward Said's Orientalism (1978), alongside innumerable others, used categories critical of European empires and applied them to Zionism and Israel. The Durban Conference (2001) marked a major point for boycott language and legal accusations against Israel. The launch of the Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions (BDS) campaign in 2005 utilized terms like “apartheid” to condemn Israel.

After October 7, 2023, these streams converged with unusual speed. University encampments and demonstrations across North America and Europe accelerated the public use of slogans like "settler-colonial state," "apartheid," and "globalize the intifada," showing the convergence of Soviet propaganda, Arab nationalism, and post-colonialism, into a widely repeated vocabulary. In this cauldron, a multicultural and global antizionist language had emerged.

Natan Sharansky’s 3D test distinguishes criticism of Israel from antisemitism by asking whether the criticism turns into demonization, applies double standards, or denies Israel’s legitimacy as a Jewish state. Regarding antizionism, this test matters because the antizionists often shift from what Israel does to whether Jewish sovereignty should exist at all. This work uses his analysis to understand antizionism.

How This Glossary Is Organized

This Glossary utilizes a specific layout as a loose guide; the Definition section establishes the literal scope of the slur, anchoring it in historical dates, events, and texts; Common Usage records the spread across specific universities and faculty unions, using hard percentages to prove the scale of the hostility; Implied Meaning reveals the claim behind the language, demonstrating how the words target the existence of the Jewish state; Origin/Lineage traces the transmission of the libel from Soviet and Arab sources, connecting modern campus slogans to 20th-century anti-Jewish campaigns; Function explains the practical application of these terms and their intentions, aims, and consequences, and Notes provide the grounding required for a comprehensive indictment of the usage of the words defined in this glossary.

I would be remiss if I did not admit myself to have particular interests, motivations, assumptions, and perspectives that influence the shaping, organization, and explanations of the parts of antizionist vocabulary described within. With that said, and having an understanding that this writer believes neutrality a myth, effort is undertaken to assist others in discovering the meanings of these revived morphologies, which meant leaving behind a certain amount (not all) of iconoclastic vigor in the heart of the author.

This volume does not answer foreign policy disputes: it catalogs language. Its task is to identify what specific terms mean, who promoted them, and how they are used. Words do not merely describe conflicts as a passive instrument might; they assign roles, form their own troops, and have their own wars.

For this reason, when referring to its current manifestation, the book uses the form “antizionism” (or Antizionism) without a hyphen. The hyphenated anti-zionism (or Anti-Zionism) suggests a disagreement with a political theory. The unhyphenated form identifies a historical grouping expressed through recurring verbiage, social acceptance, cultural symbols, and coordinated public mobilization. The model follows the scholarly standardization of antisemitism, which likewise names a distinct phenomenon, rather than a dispute over “Semitism.” Antizionism and Zionism will each have three parts in their entries: 1) before the state of Israel, 2) the state of Israel until the day before October 7, 2023, and 3) its development from the massacres to the present day.

To understand a movement, its vocabulary must be mapped. To map the vocabulary, a glossary is required.

Stop The Genocide

Definition

“Stop the Genocide” is a protest slogan that treats Israel’s war in Gaza as an ongoing genocide and demands urgent action against Israel and those who support it. The phrase is not just a call to reduce civilian suffering. It declares the legal conclusion in advance.

The legal term is narrow. Article II of the 1948 Genocide Convention defines genocide as listed acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial, or religious group as such. Civilian death, mass displacement, urban ruin, hunger, siege conditions, or even serious violations of the laws of war do not alone prove genocide. The required element is intent to destroy the protected group as such.

That is why the slogan matters. It does not say Israel has used excessive force. It does not say Israel has violated proportionality in named strikes. It does not say humanitarian access has been wrongly restricted. It says Israel is trying to destroy Palestinians as Palestinians. That is a far heavier statement, and it requires a far higher evidentiary showing.

The slogan often skips that burden. It turns the gravest crime in international law into a chant. Once repeated at protests, campus encampments, churches, city councils, NGO campaigns, and cultural boycotts, the phrase places Israel beside Nazi Germany, Rwanda, and Srebrenica before a court has reached that result.

Common Usage

After the Hamas-led attacks of October 7, 2023, the slogan spread quickly through anti-Israel protest. It appeared in marches, divestment campaigns, campus encampments, ceasefire resolutions, church statements, professional letters, and online campaigns. It was often paired with calls for arms embargoes, sanctions, academic boycotts, and cultural exclusion.

Major NGOs and UN-linked bodies later adopted the same language. Amnesty International's December 2024 report concluded that Israel had committed and was continuing to commit genocide against Palestinians in Gaza. Doctors Without Borders / Medecins Sans Frontieres also used genocide language in its Gaza materials. In September 2025, the UN Independent International Commission of Inquiry on the Occupied Palestinian Territory, including East Jerusalem, and Israel said Israel had committed genocide in Gaza.

Those statements gave the slogan institutional support. They did not create a final court judgment. The International Court of Justice case brought by South Africa remains pending. The ICJ's January 26, 2024 order indicated provisional measures; it did not rule that Israel had committed genocide. Former ICJ President Joan Donoghue later clarified in a BBC interview that the Court had decided that Palestinians had a plausible right to protection from genocide and that South Africa could present the case. That is different from a finding that genocide occurred.

The International Criminal Court has also proceeded on a different track. In November 2024, the ICC issued arrest warrants for Benjamin Netanyahu and Yoav Gallant for alleged war crimes and crimes against humanity, including starvation as a method of warfare, murder, persecution, and other inhumane acts. The warrants did not include genocide.

Implied Meaning

The slogan asks the listener to accept three propositions at once: that genocide is occurring, that Israel is the perpetrator, and that those who support Israel are aiding the crime. This is why the phrase moves so easily into demands against universities, artists, governments, businesses, and Jewish communal groups. If genocide is the premise, ordinary contact with Israel can be treated as complicity.

That leap changes the whole discussion. Hamas's October 7 massacre, hostage-taking, tunnel system, use of civilian areas, rocket fire, command sites, and refusal to surrender hostages can all be pushed aside as distractions. Israeli warnings, evacuation routes, aid entry, combatant deaths, and the legal duties of a state fighting an armed group inside a dense urban area can be dismissed as excuses.

This does not mean Israeli conduct is beyond scrutiny. It means the word genocide cannot be used as a substitute for scrutiny. A lawful-war analysis asks what was targeted, what was known, what precautions were feasible, what civilian harm was expected, what military advantage was sought, and whether unlawful intent can be proved. The slogan gives none of that. It supplies the verdict first.

The phrase also changes how Jews are heard. A Jew who asks for proof can be described as defending genocide. A Jewish institution that supports Israel's existence can be treated as aligned with genocide. A state born in the aftermath of the Holocaust is cast as the author of the very crime that shaped the postwar legal order. That inversion is central to the slogan's force.

Origin / Lineage

The word genocide was coined by Raphael Lemkin in Axis Rule in Occupied Europe in 1944 and entered treaty law in the 1948 Genocide Convention. Lemkin's word was born from the destruction of European Jewry and from the need to punish attempts to destroy protected human groups.

The same word was later aimed repeatedly at Israel. During the Lebanon War in 1982, UN General Assembly Resolution 37/123 declared the Sabra and Shatila massacre "an act of genocide." The massacre itself was carried out by Lebanese Christian Phalangist militia. Israel's Kahan Commission found the militia directly responsible and found Israeli officials indirectly responsible for failing to prevent the danger. The General Assembly wording therefore showed how quickly genocide language could be directed toward Israel even where the underlying facts were more complex than the slogan allowed.

Cold War anti-Zionist language also helped prepare the ground. Soviet and aligned rhetoric portrayed Zionism as racist, Nazi-like, colonial, and exterminatory. UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 in 1975, which declared Zionism a form of racism, did not use the genocide label, but it helped make Israel uniquely available for the vocabulary of supreme criminality. The Durban NGO Forum in 2001 later widened that style of advocacy in human-rights settings.

After October 7, the older vocabulary moved fast. Court filings, NGO reports, UN reports, campus campaigns, clergy statements, artists' petitions, and social-media graphics made genocide a default word for Israel's campaign in Gaza. The result was not a new legal finding. It was a rapid social verdict.

Function

The slogan functions as an emergency command. If genocide is happening now, delay sounds like complicity and evidence demands sound like evasion. That makes the phrase effective for mobilizing boycotts, severed partnerships, arms restrictions, sanctions, criminal referrals, and public denunciations.

It also narrows what may be said. A speaker who asks about hostages, Hamas combatants, tunnels under civilian areas, aid theft, rocket fire, or the distinction between war crimes and genocide can be treated as someone trying to help Israel escape judgment. In this way, the slogan polices debate. It does not only accuse Israel. It punishes hesitation.

The phrase also produces a burden shift. Israel and its defenders are asked to prove a negative: that the war is not genocidal. The legal burden should run the other way. A crime requiring special intent must be proved by those asserting it. War can be brutal, unlawful in parts, and catastrophic for civilians without meeting the genocide test.

Notes

1. The Genocide Convention requires intent to destroy a protected group as such. This is the key distinction between genocide and other wartime crimes.

2. The ICJ's January 2024 provisional-measures order did not find that Israel had committed genocide. It found that some rights asserted by South Africa were plausible and ordered preventive measures while the case continued.

3. South Africa filed its memorial in October 2024. Israel filed its response in March 2026. As of June 2, 2026, the ICJ case has not reached a final merits judgment.

4. The ICC arrest warrants for Netanyahu and Gallant did not include genocide. They concerned other alleged crimes under the Rome Statute.

5. Amnesty International, MSF, and the UN Commission of Inquiry have used genocide language. These are advocacy, NGO, or commission findings unless and until a competent court reaches a final judgment.

6. The strongest criticism of the slogan is not that civilian suffering is unreal. It is that the slogan announces the most serious legal result before the proof has been tested.

Responses / Criticisms

It turns a legal result into a chant.

Genocide is not a synonym for civilian death. It is a specific crime requiring proof of intent to destroy a protected group as such. The slogan skips that proof and presents the result as already settled.

It treats evidence as obstruction.

A serious legal case should welcome hard questions. The slogan often does the opposite. Questions about Hamas, hostages, tunnels, warnings, aid, combatants, proportionality, and intent are treated as efforts to excuse Israel. That is not legal discipline. It is pressure.

It turns Israel into a permanent criminal object.

When genocide becomes the lead word for Israel, every relationship with Israel becomes suspect. Universities, artists, businesses, donors, governments, and Jews abroad are pushed into the same circle of blame. The slogan does not stay in The Hague. It follows Jews into public life.

It inverts Jewish history.

The postwar genocide convention was created in the shadow of the destruction of European Jewry. Using the word loosely against the Jewish state carries special force. It does not prove that every user hates Jews. It does mean the word demands care.
