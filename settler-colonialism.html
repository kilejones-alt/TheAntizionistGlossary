Settler-Colonialism

Definition

Settler-colonialism is an academic term used for societies in which incoming settlers seek land and durable control, not only labor or trade. Patrick Wolfe’s 2006 essay, “Settler Colonialism and the Elimination of the Native,” gave the field one of its central formulas: settlers “come to stay,” and land is the central object. Lorenzo Veracini later helped formalize the field. In antizionist use, the term is applied to Zionism and Israel as if Jewish return to the ancestral homeland were simply another European settler project.

Applied to Israel, the term does more than describe. It tells the reader what to see before the evidence is weighed: Zionism as foreign intrusion, Palestinians as the only native people. That use turns a conflict between two peoples with deep attachments to the same land into a familiar script: European invader versus indigenous victim. The result is not better history. It is a shortcut.

The shortcut has costs. It treats Jewish return, refuge, and self-rule as if they were the same kind of project as Britain in Australia or France in Algeria. It leaves out the absence of a Jewish mother country, the history of Jewish statelessness, the Holocaust, Arab rejection of partition, the expulsion and flight of Jews from Arab and Muslim lands, and the fact that Arab citizens of Israel vote, serve in parliament, sit on courts, and live as citizens inside the state. None of these facts ends the Palestinian case. They do show why the imported template does not fit cleanly.

A serious use of the term can illuminate settlement, land, power, and Palestinian dispossession. The antizionist use usually goes further. It treats Jewish sovereignty itself as colonial trespass and treats Israel’s continued existence as the wrong to be undone.

Common Usage

The term appears throughout university courses, Middle East studies writing, student campaigns, NGO commentary, and boycott materials. It is often paired with apartheid, genocide, decolonization, indigenous rights, and land back rhetoric. Its effect is to move Israel out of the ordinary language of disputed borders, war, and diplomacy and into the language of colonial removal.

Rashid Khalidi’s The Hundred Years’ War on Palestine gives the term a polished historical form by describing the modern history of Palestine as a colonial war against an indigenous people. Fayez Sayegh’s Zionist Colonialism in Palestine gave the same idea an earlier PLO research form in 1965. Maxime Rodinson’s Israel: A Colonial-Settler State? moved the argument into Marxist and postcolonial debate after 1967. Later settler-colonial studies supplied a broader academic vocabulary that students now often repeat without knowing its older institutional path.

In campus use, the term rarely stays academic. It becomes a sentence of expulsion: settlers leave. Once Israelis are named as settlers and only settlers, Jewish national life becomes an occupation to be reversed, not a people’s claim to be weighed. This is why the term so often sits near “from the river to the sea.” Both phrases make the same space whole and ask what place remains there for Jewish sovereignty.

If Israelis are settlers in the same sense as imperial settlers elsewhere, then attacks on them can be presented as the expected cost of liberation. This is not an accidental side effect. It follows from the way the word sorts the actors before the facts are examined.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that Jews are foreign to the land in the only sense that counts. Jewish history may be acknowledged as religious memory, archaeology, liturgy, or ancient presence, but the modern Jewish national claim is recast as European intrusion. Exile becomes disqualification. Return becomes invasion.

This is the key move. Palestinians remain a people with collective rights, while Jews become migrants with individual rights at most. Jewish peoplehood is reduced to religion. Jewish refuge is reduced to settlement. Jewish sovereignty is reduced to domination. Once those reductions are accepted, Israel can be described not as the national home of a persecuted people, but as a colonial artifact.

That account cannot absorb Mizrahi and Sephardi Jews without strain. Pew’s 2016 study of Israeli society found Israeli Jews roughly divided between Ashkenazi and Sephardi/Mizrahi identities. The older white-European story is therefore a poor fit for Israeli society as it exists. Large numbers of Israeli Jews descend from communities in Iraq, Yemen, Morocco, Egypt, Iran, Syria, Libya, Tunisia, and other Middle Eastern or North African lands. A theory that must make those Jews into Europeans in order to work is already failing.

The term also softens Palestinian and Arab agency. The 1947 UN Partition Plan recommended Jewish and Arab states. Zionist leadership accepted partition as the basis for statehood; Arab leaders rejected it and war followed. The 1939 British White Paper, issued under Arab pressure, restricted Jewish immigration on the eve of the Holocaust and made further immigration after five years dependent on Arab consent. Those facts complicate the idea of an unstoppable European settler power imposing itself on passive natives. The record contains Jewish weakness as well as Jewish agency, Arab agency as well as Arab loss.

Origin / Lineage

The academic field associated with settler-colonialism developed mainly through studies of places such as Australia, North America, and other societies created through overseas settlement and durable control. Wolfe’s 2006 article became foundational because it treated settler-colonialism as a distinct formation centered on land and replacement. Veracini’s work helped systematize the distinction between settler rule and other colonial forms.

The anti-Israel use is older than the current academic fashion. In 1965, Fayez Sayegh’s Zionist Colonialism in Palestine, published by the PLO Research Center in Beirut, described Zionism as a colonial project. After 1967, Soviet and Arab diplomatic campaigns helped spread the same association. UN General Assembly Resolution 3151 G in 1973 grouped Zionism with South African racism and Portuguese colonialism. Resolution 3379 in 1975 declared Zionism a form of racism and racial discrimination.

Rodinson’s 1967 essay, later published in English as Israel: A Colonial-Settler State?, added Marxist credibility to the comparison. Edward Said’s The Question of Palestine then helped carry the anti-colonial reading into Western universities. In the 2000s and 2010s, settler-colonial studies made the term more portable. What had once moved through PLO research, Soviet-aligned diplomacy, and Third World forums became classroom language.

Derek Penslar’s work is useful because it refuses an easy answer. He takes seriously the ways Zionism had colonial features, including settlement, land purchase, and European backing at key moments. He also stresses that Zionism was not a normal imperial project. It was a movement of a stateless people seeking refuge and renewal, often against the interests of the very empires it had to bargain with. That tension is where serious history begins. Antizionist shorthand usually removes the tension.

Function

The term makes Jewish sovereignty sound removable. If Israel is a settler-colonial state, the answer is not border revision, minority rights, security guarantees, federation, partition, or negotiated compromise. The answer becomes decolonization. In practical use, that often means a Palestine replacing Israel from the Jordan river to the Mediterranean sea.

The term also changes how Israeli civilian death is heard. A murdered child in a kibbutz can be redescribed as a settler. A city inside Israel’s recognized borders can be treated as occupied land. A Jewish refugee from Baghdad or Fez can be made into a European colonizer. This is how the word does its work. It assigns verdicts before the facts arrive.

It also protects weak claims from scrutiny. Once the label is accepted, contrary facts become distractions: Arab citizens of Israel, Jewish refugees from Arab lands, Jewish historical attachment, the absence of a mother country, the 1947 partition vote, the 1939 White Paper, and Israel’s absorption of Holocaust survivors can all be treated as secondary. The conclusion has already been chosen.

A better account would admit two truths at once. Palestinians experienced dispossession, defeat, military rule, and statelessness. Jews were not a foreign empire. The settler-colonial label usually permits only the first sentence. A serious history needs both.

Notes

1. The term should not be dismissed simply because it comes from academia. It can raise real questions about land, settlement, sovereignty, and Palestinian loss. The problem is the overreach: using the term to erase Jewish peoplehood and turn Israel’s existence into a colonial crime.

2. Zionism had interactions with the empire. The Balfour Declaration, the British Mandate, land purchase under Ottoman and British rule, and European Jewish migration all belong in the record. But a movement that lacked a mother country, sought refuge from persecution, and later fought Britain does not match the classic imperial pattern.

3. The 1939 White Paper is an essential complication. Britain restricted Jewish immigration to Palestine as European Jews faced Nazi persecution and made later immigration dependent on Arab consent. A simple story of British-backed Jewish settler power cannot explain that document.

4. Mizrahi and Sephardi Jews are not a side issue. Their history breaks the image of Israel as a white European transplant. Many came from societies where Jewish life had become unsafe after 1948. Treating them as European colonizers is an evasion.

5. The West Bank is the strongest terrain for the term’s critics. Settlement expansion, land disputes, and annexation efforts should be argued directly. Inflating those disputes into a total claim against Jewish sovereignty weakens the case by adding more than the evidence proves.

Responses / Criticisms

It forces Jewish history into someone else’s template.

The term works best when Jews are treated as Europeans arriving behind an empire. That is not the Jewish story. Jews were a dispersed people with a continuous attachment to the land, no mother country, and a modern national movement shaped by persecution. Some Zionists came from Europe. Exile does not cancel origin.

It cannot handle Mizrahi Jews.

A large share of Israeli Jews descend from Middle Eastern and North African communities. They did not come as agents of Europe. Many came as refugees from Arab and Muslim-majority societies. The settler-colonial label needs Israel to look white, European, and foreign. Mizrahi history makes that image collapse.

It turns decolonization into permission for Jewish removal.

If Israelis are only settlers, then removing Jewish sovereignty can be sold as liberation. That is the danger. The term does not only criticize settlements or borders. In its antizionist use, it marks Jewish national life as a colonial offense to be undone.

It makes peace harder.

A settlement can be removed. A border can be negotiated. A state can reform laws. But if Israel itself is the colonial object, then compromise looks like betrayal. The term therefore pushes the conflict away from coexistence and toward replacement.
