Zionism

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Zionism” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Zionism (1880–1947)

Definition

Zionism is the Jewish national movement for self-determination in the ancestral land of Israel. In its pre-state period, from the late nineteenth century to 1947, it argued that Jews were not only a religious community but a people with a history, language, memory, and homeland. Its answer to Jewish danger was not better pleading before foreign rulers. Its answer was sovereignty.

The modern movement arose in Europe because emancipation had failed to make Jews safe. Legal equality did not end exclusion. Assimilation did not end antisemitism. Pogroms in the Russian Empire, restrictions across Europe, and the Dreyfus Affair in France showed that Jews could be citizens on paper and targets in practice. Theodor Herzl gave this crisis its most famous modern answer in The Jewish State in 1896. The First Zionist Congress in Basel in 1897 turned that answer into an organized international effort.

The pre-state Zionist claim was blunt: a stateless people survives by permission. Permission can be withdrawn. Jewish life needed a place where Jews could become responsible for their own rescue.

Common Usage

Before 1948, Zionism meant practical national construction. It meant Hebrew revival, immigration, land purchase, farming settlements, towns, schools, labor federations, health funds, newspapers, defense groups, and diplomacy. The movement built organs of Jewish public life under Ottoman rule and then under the British Mandate.

The Basel Program said Zionism sought a publicly and legally secured home in Palestine for the Jewish people. That wording matters. The early movement wanted international recognition, not only private settlement. Herzl and the World Zionist Organization pursued diplomacy with empires and governments because they understood that refuge required public standing.

The Balfour Declaration of November 2, 1917 gave Britain’s support for a national home for the Jewish people in Palestine while also saying that nothing should prejudice the civil and religious rights of existing non-Jewish communities. The League of Nations Mandate later incorporated that commitment. Zionists treated this as international recognition of Jewish national return. Arab leaders increasingly treated it as an unjust foreign-backed project that threatened Arab majority rule in the country.

By the 1930s and 1940s, Zionism had become more than a claim in speeches. The Yishuv, the Jewish community in Mandate Palestine, had representative bodies, Hebrew schools, labor institutions, agricultural settlements, urban centers, welfare networks, and armed defense groups. By the time the United Nations voted for partition in November 1947, the state did not need to be imagined from nothing. Much of its civic life already existed.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning of pre-state Zionism was that Jewish safety could not depend on the kindness of host societies. The movement did not say every Jew had to leave the diaspora. It said the Jewish people required a sovereign center, a place of refuge, and the capacity to defend Jewish life when other states failed or refused.

That claim became harder to dismiss as the century advanced. The 1939 British White Paper limited Jewish immigration to 75,000 over five years and required Arab consent for further Jewish immigration afterward. It appeared just as European Jews faced Nazi persecution and, soon after, extermination. For Zionists, that policy confirmed the core lesson: when Jews most needed entry, others could close the gate.

Zionism also implied that Jews had not become foreigners to their own past. Jewish attachment to the land was carried through prayer, law, memory, pilgrimage, burial, settlement, language, and repeated return. Modern Zionism gave that attachment organized form. It did not invent the bond. It turned the bond into a plan.

This does not mean the land was empty. It was not. Arab communities lived there, and Arab opposition grew as Jewish immigration and land purchase increased. A serious account of Zionism should not erase that collision. The Zionist answer was that Jewish self-rule was still necessary because Jewish vulnerability was not theoretical. Arab rejection was real. Jewish danger was real too.

Origin / Lineage

Several streams converged. One was ancient Jewish attachment to the land of Israel. Another was European nationalism, which taught many peoples to seek self-rule. A third was modern antisemitism, which showed Jews that integration alone could fail. A fourth was practical settlement, beginning before Herzl through Hovevei Zion and other early groups that supported Jewish farming communities in Ottoman Palestine.

Leon Pinsker’s Auto-Emancipation in 1882 argued that Jews could not wait for others to emancipate them. Moses Hess had earlier linked Jewish renewal with return. Ahad Ha’am emphasized Hebrew culture and national renewal. Herzl supplied diplomatic urgency and a mass organization. Labor Zionists later made settlement, Hebrew labor, and collective institutions central to the project. Religious Zionists read return through Jewish tradition. Revisionists, especially after Ze’ev Jabotinsky, placed greater stress on defense, sovereignty, and Jewish power.

The movement was therefore plural from the start. Its branches disagreed over religion, socialism, language, diplomacy, force, borders, and the kind of Jewish society to build. They shared one premise: Jews were a people entitled to national life in their historic homeland.

The international path moved through the Balfour Declaration, the League of Nations Mandate, British limits and reversals, wartime catastrophe, postwar displaced-persons camps, and finally the UN partition vote. Resolution 181 recommended separate Jewish and Arab states with an economic union and a special international status for Jerusalem. The Jewish Agency decided to cooperate with the UN plan despite dissatisfaction over borders. Arab leaders rejected partition. Civil war followed.

Function

Pre-state Zionism functioned as rescue before rescue was fully possible. It organized immigration, built towns and farms, revived Hebrew as a daily language, trained defense groups, created institutions, raised money, and persuaded Jews that they were not condemned to permanent dependence.

It also turned Jewish grief into public capacity. The movement did not only mourn persecution. It built tools against helplessness. The Haganah, founded in 1920, became the main defense force of the Yishuv. The Palmach was created in 1941. The Irgun and Lehi broke away to the right and used more aggressive methods against both Arabs and the British. Those divisions were serious and sometimes bitter. Still, together they showed that Jewish dependence on outside protection was ending.

By 1947, Zionism had already done the work that made statehood possible: population, language, land, institutions, defense, diplomacy, and a public ready to govern itself. The UN vote recognized a fact already taking shape.

Notes

1. Herzl’s The Jewish State did not create Jewish longing for Zion. It gave modern statecraft to an older attachment.

2. The Basel Program of 1897 is central. It defined Zionism as seeking a publicly and legally secured home in Palestine for the Jewish people.

3. The Balfour Declaration supported a national home for the Jewish people and also protected the civil and religious rights of non-Jewish communities. Both parts matter.

4. The 1939 White Paper limited Jewish immigration during the period when European Jews most needed refuge. It remains one of the clearest examples of why Zionists distrusted foreign guarantees.

5. UN Resolution 181 did not give Jews all they wanted. It recommended partition. The Jewish Agency cooperated with the plan. Arab leaders rejected it.

Responses / Criticisms

It was not an adventure by a secure people.

Zionism arose because Jewish life was unsafe in places where Jews had already tried loyalty, learning, commerce, citizenship, prayer, reform, socialism, and assimilation. The movement’s strongest case was not romance. It was exposure. Jews had no final court of appeal when states turned against them.

It was not one crude plan.

Herzl, Ahad Ha’am, Jabotinsky, labor Zionists, religious Zionists, liberals, and socialists did not all want the same future. They argued over means, borders, labor, faith, defense, and culture. That internal conflict does not weaken the movement’s case. It shows that Zionism was a national revival with many schools, not a single command.

It cannot be judged honestly without the gates that closed.

The British White Paper, refugee quotas, and postwar displacement camps are not side details. They are central to the question. A people facing extermination and then homelessness needed more than sympathy. It needed entry, power, and a state able to say yes when others said no.

It did not erase Arab opposition, but Arab opposition did not erase Jewish need.

Two national claims collided in the same land. That collision was tragic and real. But the existence of Arab opposition does not make Jewish self-determination illegitimate. It makes the conflict hard. The Zionist case rests on the claim that Jewish survival could not be postponed until every opponent agreed.

Zionism (1948–October 6, 2023)

Definition

After May 1948, Zionism changed from a movement seeking Jewish statehood into the work of keeping that state alive. The question was no longer whether Jews could build a sovereign home in their ancestral land. The question was whether that home could survive war, migration, terrorism, diplomatic isolation, internal division, and repeated attempts to make Jewish sovereignty temporary.

This period began with independence and invasion. Israel declared independence on May 14, 1948. Armies from neighboring Arab states entered the war. Israel survived, but the war also produced Palestinian displacement, dispossession, and exile. That double fact shaped the next seventy-five years. Zionism became the name for Jewish survival through sovereignty; Palestinians came to remember the same war as the Nakba.

Zionism during this period was not one party, one leader, or one fixed program. Labor Zionists built many early state institutions. Revisionist Zionists gained power through Menachem Begin and Likud in 1977. Religious Zionists became more central after 1967, especially around settlement and land. Liberal Zionists stressed democracy, courts, civil rights, and compromise. Mizrahi, Ethiopian, Russian-speaking, secular, traditional, Orthodox, and ultra-Orthodox Jews all changed what the state became. The common core was not agreement over borders or policy. It was the conviction that Jews needed sovereign power because Jewish safety could not depend on permission from others.

Common Usage

Inside Israel, Zionism became practical statecraft. It meant immigration, Hebrew public life, military service, memorial days, elections, schools, courts, diplomacy, and the absorption of Jews from radically different worlds. The Law of Return, enacted in 1950, made Jewish immigration a legal right. Holocaust survivors arrived. Jews fled or were driven from Arab and Muslim-majority countries. Ethiopian Jews came through dramatic rescue operations and later migration. More than one million immigrants from the former Soviet Union entered Israel from the late 1980s onward.

Those waves changed Israel. They made Zionism less European, less uniform, and more Middle Eastern than many of its critics admit. A state often described abroad as a white colonial implant became home to Jews from Iraq, Yemen, Morocco, Egypt, Iran, Ethiopia, Russia, Ukraine, Poland, France, Argentina, the United States, and elsewhere. That history does not erase Palestinian suffering. It does damage the simple story that Israel is only a European settler project imposed on the Middle East from outside.

The wars of this period shaped the word as well. The 1956 Suez Crisis, the 1967 Six-Day War, and the 1973 Yom Kippur War reinforced the Israeli belief that defeat could be existential. The 1967 war also put Israel in control of the West Bank, East Jerusalem, Gaza, Sinai, and the Golan Heights, turning territory, settlement, and Palestinian rule into the central disputes of the next half century.

Diplomacy altered Zionism too. Israel returned Sinai to Egypt under the 1979 peace treaty. It signed a peace treaty with Jordan in 1994. It entered the Oslo process with the PLO in 1993, recognizing the PLO as the representative of the Palestinian people while the PLO recognized Israel’s right to exist in peace and security. These agreements did not end the conflict. They did show that Zionism after 1948 was not only land-holding or force. It also included treaty, withdrawal, recognition, and compromise.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning of Zionism in this period was simple: Jewish survival requires the capacity to defend Jewish life. That capacity now had an army, borders, courts, elections, embassies, intelligence services, rescue missions, and a refuge for Jews in danger. This was the central change from pre-state Jewish life. Jews no longer had only petitions, allies, and luck. They had a state.

The cost was also real. Sovereignty meant power, and power meant hard choices. Israel had to decide how to fight wars, how to control territory captured in war, how to absorb immigrants, how to treat Arab citizens, how to answer terrorism, how to pursue peace, and how to preserve a Jewish majority without abandoning democratic life. These were not abstract questions. They determined borders, budgets, families, prisons, checkpoints, settlements, evacuations, and funerals.

Antizionist usage during this period worked to turn the word away from Jewish self-determination and toward permanent guilt. Soviet, Arab nationalist, Islamist, and Western language increasingly treated Zionism as racism, colonialism, Nazism, apartheid, or imperial power. UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 in 1975 gave that campaign its most famous sentence by declaring Zionism a form of racism. Its repeal in 1991 removed the resolution, not the slogan.

Origin / Lineage

Post-1948 Zionism grew directly out of the pre-state movement, but statehood changed its center of gravity. David Ben-Gurion and the early Labor-led governments emphasized immigration, defense, settlement, economic building, and international recognition. The 1948 war and the absorption of huge immigrant waves gave the state its first tasks.

The 1967 war marked the next break. Israel’s victory removed immediate threats from its borders but brought millions of Palestinians under Israeli rule. UN Security Council Resolution 242 tied withdrawal from territories captured in the war to the termination of belligerency and the right of every state in the area to live in peace within secure and recognized boundaries. That land-for-peace formula became the diplomatic language of the next generation.

The 1977 election brought Begin to power and moved Revisionist Zionism from opposition into government. Camp David and the Israel-Egypt peace treaty proved that a right-wing Zionist leader could return territory for peace. Israel withdrew from Sinai, including settlements, in exchange for peace with Egypt. That precedent matters because it shows that Zionism did not require permanent possession of every territory captured in 1967.

The Oslo process in the 1990s created a different hope: mutual recognition between Israel and the PLO, interim Palestinian self-government, and negotiations over final-status questions. The process failed to deliver final peace. The Second Intifada, with suicide bombings and attacks on Israeli civilians, shattered much of the Israeli public’s trust in the premise that withdrawal and negotiation would bring safety.

The 2005 disengagement from Gaza deepened that doubt. Israel dismantled all settlements in Gaza and withdrew its permanent ground presence. Hamas won Palestinian legislative elections in 2006, seized Gaza from Fatah in 2007, and built an armed enclave. Rocket fire and later wars made Gaza a warning case for many Israelis: leaving territory did not necessarily end the threat.

Function

Zionism functioned as the organizing commitment of Jewish sovereignty. It brought Jews home, defended them, gave them citizenship in a Jewish-majority society, and linked diaspora Jews to a state that could act when Jews were in danger. It also forced Jews to answer the burdens of power after centuries without it.

The movement’s practical success was extraordinary. Israel survived conventional wars, absorbed mass immigration, built a high-capacity economy, revived Hebrew as a daily language, signed peace treaties with two neighboring states, and maintained competitive elections through severe conflict. These facts do not settle every dispute over Palestinians, settlements, borders, religion, courts, or minority rights. They do show why Zionism cannot be reduced to slogans about domination.

For antizionists, this period created a problem. The state they opposed did not disappear. It endured, won wars, made treaties, absorbed Jews from persecution zones, and became central to Jewish life worldwide. As Israel became stronger, antizionist language became harsher. The claim moved from opposing particular policies to denying the legitimacy of the state itself.

Notes

The Law of Return is central to this period. It turned the Zionist promise of refuge into a legal right for Jews to immigrate to Israel.

The displacement of Jews from Arab and Muslim-majority countries belongs inside this history. It does not cancel Palestinian displacement. It does show that the post-1948 Middle East produced more than one refugee history.

The 1967 war is the hinge. Before it, Zionism meant state survival inside contested borders. After it, Zionism was judged increasingly through occupation, settlement, and Palestinian rule.

Resolution 242 should be read in full. It links withdrawal to peace, recognition, and secure boundaries. The slogan version usually keeps only the withdrawal part.

The 1979 Egypt treaty and the 2005 Gaza disengagement are hard facts for any claim that Zionism always means unlimited territorial expansion.

The failure of Oslo and the violence of the Second Intifada are central to Israeli public memory. Any account of Zionism from 1948 to October 6, 2023 that omits them cannot explain Israeli security thinking.

Responses / Criticisms

It did not end the Jewish question. It changed its terms.

Zionism did not make Jews universally safe. It gave Jews a state able to defend them, rescue them, and receive them. That is a different kind of safety from acceptance. It is less romantic and more durable.

It did not erase Palestinian claims.

Jewish self-determination and Palestinian self-determination collided in the same land. That collision produced real Palestinian suffering. A serious defense of Zionism does not need to deny that. It needs to argue that Jewish sovereignty remains legitimate even amid unresolved conflict.

It proved capable of compromise and still faced rejection.

Sinai was returned to Egypt. Oslo recognized the PLO. Gaza settlements were dismantled. These acts did not end the conflict. They do refute the claim that Zionism can only expand and never yield.

It remained necessary because Jewish vulnerability remained real.

Wars, terrorism, hostage-taking, global antisemitism, and the repeated denial of Jewish peoplehood kept the central Zionist premise alive. The argument was not that Israel would solve every danger. The argument was that Jews should not face danger without sovereign power.

Zionism (October 7, 2023–Present)

Definition

Zionism after October 7 means the same thing it meant before October 7: the Jewish people have the right to self-determination in their ancestral homeland, and the State of Israel has the right to exist, defend its citizens, and receive Jews who need refuge. The massacre did not change the definition. It changed the cost of forgetting it.

On October 7, 2023, Hamas and other armed Palestinians crossed from Gaza into southern Israel, murdered civilians in homes, towns, kibbutzim, a music festival, and military sites, and took 251 people hostage into Gaza. Israel's Ministry of Foreign Affairs lists more than 800 civilians murdered that day. AJC reported that the return of Master Sgt. Ran Gvili's remains in January 2026 completed the accounting for the 251 hostages taken by Hamas and other Palestinian groups on October 7.

For Zionists, the event renewed the oldest premise of the movement: Jews cannot make survival depend on outside sympathy. A Jewish state can fail, suffer surprise, make errors, and face bitter division. It still gives Jews an army, borders, rescue capacity, diplomacy, and a place of return. That is what Zionism supplies that emancipation, tolerance, and foreign protection never reliably supplied.

In antizionist usage after October 7, the word Zionism often means the opposite. It is used as a name for evil: racism, colonialism, genocide, apartheid, militarism, and Jewish power. The word no longer identifies only support for Jewish self-determination. It becomes a mark placed on people, institutions, artists, scholars, students, synagogues, and businesses. That is the post-October 7 turn: Zionism is treated less as a belief to debate than as a stain to attach.

Common Usage

After October 7, Zionism became one of the most contested words in Western public life. To many Jews, it meant grief, rescue, hostages, self-defense, and the need for Jewish power after Jewish civilians had been slaughtered.

The shift appeared quickly. National Students for Justice in Palestine's October 2023 Day of Resistance Toolkit called the attack a "historic win for the Palestinian resistance" and described the breach of Israel's border as proof that total return and liberation were near. It also urged students to normalize resistance. That document is useful because it shows the setting in which Zionism was being opposed: not after months of Israeli military action, but immediately after the murder and kidnapping of Israeli civilians.

The public climate also changed around Jews outside Israel. ADL reported 9,354 antisemitic incidents in the United States in 2024, the highest annual total in its audit's history. ADL said 58 percent contained elements related to Israel or Zionism. AJC's 2025 State of Antisemitism in America report found deep concern among American Jews about antisemitism after October 7. These figures do not prove that every anti-Zionist act is antisemitic. They do show that the word Zionist moved through a period of sharply increased Jewish exposure.

The word also became a test of belonging. Jews who rejected Zionism were often held up as acceptable Jews. Jews who defended Israel's existence were treated as suspect. In that setting, Zionism did not function as one position among many. It became the line that divided Jews who could remain inside progressive spaces from Jews who had to be corrected, shamed, or excluded.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning of antizionist use after October 7 is that Jewish national self-defense is uniquely illegitimate. Other peoples may speak of liberation, return, land, safety, and self-rule. Jews are told that the same language becomes supremacy when they use it.

That reversal matters. A person can oppose a prime minister, a coalition, a war aim, a settlement policy, or a military decision without opposing Zionism. Israelis do this every day. Antizionist speech after October 7 often skips that difference. It treats the existence of the Jewish state as the original wrong, then reads later events through that conclusion.

Hamas's own documents show why this matters. The 1988 Hamas Covenant spoke of a struggle against Jews and cited religious language about killing Jews. Hamas's 2017 document softened some wording, but still rejected any alternative to the full liberation of Palestine "from the river to the sea." That is not a dispute over one border adjustment. It is a rejection of Jewish sovereignty in the land.

For Jews, therefore, the post-October 7 debate is not abstract. When they say Zionism is the problem, many Jews hear a demand that the one Jewish state disappear. When those same activists call October 7 resistance, the meaning becomes sharper. The issue is not whether Israel should be criticized. It should be. The issue is whether Jewish self-determination remains a legitimate fact.

Origin / Lineage

This post-October 7 usage did not appear from nowhere. The earlier entries in this glossary trace the path: Soviet anti-Zionist writing, Arab nationalist rejection of Jewish sovereignty, Islamist war against Israel, postcolonial scholarship, Durban, BDS, campus activism, and social-media compression. By October 2023, the vocabulary was already in place. The massacre activated it.

The older Zionist line runs the other way. Herzl argued that legal equality without power left Jews exposed. The Basel Program sought a publicly and legally secured home in Palestine. The Balfour Declaration, the Mandate, the UN partition vote, and Israeli independence moved that claim from movement to state. After 1948, Israel absorbed Holocaust survivors, Jews expelled or pushed out of Arab and Muslim countries, Soviet Jews, Ethiopian Jews, and later Jews facing danger elsewhere. Zionism became not only a claim to land, but an answer to Jewish vulnerability.

October 7 pushed that answer back to the front. The attack took place inside the sovereign Jewish state, not in exile. That fact is sometimes used against Zionism: if Israel cannot prevent massacre, critics ask, what good is sovereignty? The answer is that sovereignty is not a promise of perfect safety. It is the ability to fight back, recover the kidnapped, bury the dead under one's own flag, and prevent others from deciding whether Jewish life is worth defending.

The antizionist line after October 7 tried to reverse that lesson. It treated the massacre as proof of Palestinian liberation, proof of Israeli weakness, proof of settler collapse, or proof that Zionism had no future. In doing so, it made the Zionist case again. A world willing to explain away the slaughter of Jews is exactly the world Zionism was built to survive.

Function

For Jews and Israel’s supporters after October 7, Zionism functions as a language of survival. It names the right of Jews to defend themselves collectively, to mourn without apology, to demand the return of hostages, to fight Hamas, and to insist that Jewish safety cannot be outsourced.

For antizionist movements, the word functions as a target. It lets speakers avoid saying Jews while still identifying many Jews as the problem. A synagogue can be called Zionist. A Hillel can be called Zionist. A Jewish student can be called Zionist. A cultural event, donor, professor, newspaper, or business can be called Zionist. The word can then carry blame without the speaker having to use older anti-Jewish language.

This is why the IHRA working definition matters. It says that denying the Jewish people their right to self-determination, including by claiming that Israel's existence is a racist endeavor, may be antisemitic depending on context. It also lists holding Jews collectively responsible for Israel's actions and comparing Israeli policy to Nazi policy. Those examples fit many post-October 7 uses of Zionist as a public mark of guilt.

The word also forces a choice. A person can be a Zionist and criticize Israeli governments. A person can be a Zionist and support Palestinian rights. A person can be a Zionist and oppose annexation, corruption, extremism, or failed policy. What a person cannot honestly do is define Zionism as Jewish evil and then claim to be having a normal policy dispute. Once Jewish self-determination itself becomes the offense, the dispute has moved to the existence of the Jewish state.

Notes

1. October 7 did not prove that every Israeli decision after October 7 was wise, lawful, or necessary. Zionism is not a blank check for any government. It is the claim that Jews have a right to national self-defense and statehood.

2. Antizionism after October 7 often says it opposes a state, not Jews. That distinction can be real in some cases. It collapses when Jews abroad are pressured, excluded, harassed, or asked to denounce Israel as the price of entry.

3. Hamas's 2017 document is sometimes cited as moderation because it accepts a Palestinian state on the 1967 lines as a formula of national consensus. The same document rejects recognition of Israel and rejects any alternative to full liberation from the river to the sea. Both parts must be read together.

4. The hostage record matters. The taking of civilians as hostages was not a metaphor. It was a crime against named human beings. Any account of Zionism after October 7 that omits hostages has already softened the event.

5. The post-October 7 Jewish fear is not paranoia. It rests on murders, kidnappings, threats, harassment, vandalism, and a documented rise in antisemitic incidents in several countries.

Responses / Criticisms

It keeps Jewish self-defense in view.

The central Zionist answer after October 7 is not triumphal. It is sober. Jews can be murdered even in a Jewish state. But without a Jewish state, Jews depend again on others to decide whether rescue is worth the cost. That dependence is the old danger.

It separates state criticism from state denial.

There is no contradiction between Zionism and criticism of Israeli governments. Zionism requires no loyalty to a given leader or party. It requires the legitimacy of Jewish statehood. That distinction is the line antizionist language often erases.

It exposes the public use of Zionist as a substitute slur.

When Zionist is used to mark Jews for exclusion, the word has stopped naming a belief. It has become a social weapon. A movement that claims to oppose only a state should not need to target Jewish students, synagogues, schools, restaurants, artists, or communal spaces.

It asks antizionists to name the future they want.

If the demand is two states, say two states. If the demand is equal rights in a negotiated one-state model, say that. If the demand is the end of Israel as a Jewish state, say that too. Zionism after October 7 forces the hidden question into the open: do Jews remain a sovereign people in the land, or not?
