Imperial State

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Imperial State” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Definition

“Imperial State” is an antizionist label used to portray Israel as a regional hegemon seeking dominion beyond its recognized borders through force, settlement, intelligence operations, and alliance with the United States. The term borrows the language of empire and applies it to a country of roughly ten million people in a hostile security environment. Its suggestion is simple: Israeli action is not defensive, retaliatory, or deterrent. It is rule.

The term can have a serious academic use when writers are analyzing settlement, sovereignty, occupation, or the legacy of European empires in the Middle East. It becomes antizionist shorthand when it treats the Jewish state itself as an imperial entity whose power is inherently illegitimate. In that use, Israel is not described as a state with disputed borders and severe security threats. It is described as a miniature empire whose survival depends on domination.

The label is often tied to the “Greater Israel” story: the claim that Israel secretly seeks territory from the Nile to the Euphrates. There are real maximalist currents on the Israeli far right, and there are real disputes over annexation, settlement, and sovereignty in the West Bank. Those facts should be named directly. They do not prove that the Nile-to-Euphrates version is the adopted program of the State of Israel. In hostile propaganda, “Greater Israel” often turns those disputes into a hidden blueprint for regional conquest.

Common Usage

Nur Masalha’s Imperial Israel and the Palestinians: The Politics of Expansion (2000), helped give the term a scholarly form by arguing that Israel’s post-1967 conduct toward Palestinians showed expansionist tendencies. Maxime Rodinson’s earlier Israel: A Colonial-Settler State? (1973) placed Zionism inside a broader history of European settlement and colonization. These works are part of the intellectual background for the phrase.

In current usage, however, “imperial Israel” often means more than a historical or legal thesis. It is used to describe targeted killings, strikes against Hezbollah, operations in Gaza, settlement growth, normalization agreements, intelligence reach, U.S. aid, and border enforcement as pieces of one project. In November 2025, New York Times reporting by Roger Cohen and David Guttenfelder used the headline “‘Imperial Israel’ in the New Middle East,” showing how the label had moved from activist and academic use into elite journalism.

The term is also paired with a contradiction common in anti-Israel rhetoric. Israel is described as an American outpost, yet also as the hidden force controlling American policy. It is said to be subordinate to empire and to command empire at the same time. The two claims cannot both be true in the same way. Their coexistence shows the term’s usefulness: it can make Israel appear both dependent and omnipotent, small and world-shaping, local and global.

Implied Meaning

Calling Israel imperial asks the reader to interpret nearly every Israeli use of power as domination. Security zones become colonies. Deterrence becomes expansion. Strikes on armed groups become regional rule. Peace treaties become tactical pauses. Once that premise is accepted, Israeli withdrawals no longer count as evidence against the thesis; they are recast as temporary maneuvers in a larger plan.

The historical record is less convenient. Israel returned the Sinai Peninsula to Egypt under the 1979 peace treaty and completed withdrawal in 1982. It withdrew from southern Lebanon in 2000, a withdrawal the UN Secretary-General reported as completed in accordance with Security Council Resolution 425. It dismantled all Israeli settlements in Gaza during the 2005 disengagement. None of this settles the West Bank, East Jerusalem, Gaza’s legal status after 2005, or the legality of later Israeli operations. It does show that the simple empire thesis cannot explain the whole record.

Critics point to settlement expansion, the West Bank, East Jerusalem, repeated operations in Gaza and Lebanon, and Israel’s reach across the region. Those are serious subjects. The problem is not that Israeli power is beyond criticism. The problem is that the empire label often decides the matter before the details are examined. It makes Israeli power illegitimate by category, while treating the power of Hamas, Hezbollah, Iran, Syria, or other actors as secondary reaction.

Origin / Lineage

The lineage of the term runs through several channels. The first is Cold War anti-Zionism. After Israel’s victory in the Six-Day War, Soviet writing portrayed Zionism as an agent of Western imperialism. Yuri Ivanov’s Caution: Zionism!, published in English by Progress Publishers in 1970, presented Zionism as linked to imperialist power, finance, propaganda, and anti-communism. UN General Assembly Resolution 3151 G in 1973 grouped “Zionism and Israeli imperialism” with South African racism and Portuguese colonialism. Resolution 3379 in 1975 gave the same campaign its most famous formula by declaring Zionism a form of racism.

The second channel is Arab nationalist and Palestinian liberation literature. Zionism was described as a European implant, Israel as a forward base of Western power, and the Palestinian struggle as part of a wider anti-colonial struggle. Rodinson’s 1967 essay and its later English publication supplied a Marxist version of this analysis. Masalha’s 2000 book extended the claim into the post-1967 settlement era.

The “Greater Israel” myth circulates especially well in this environment because it offers a total explanation for every Israeli move. A strike in Lebanon, a raid in Syria, settlement in the West Bank, and diplomatic normalization can all be folded into one story of hidden expansion.

Function

“Imperial State” functions as a status label. Once applied, it places Israel beside Britain in India, France in Algeria, or other empires remembered as domination. The term changes the debate before it starts. The question is no longer whether a specific Israeli action was lawful, necessary, excessive, or strategically wise. The question becomes why an empire should be tolerated.

The label also reduces surrounding actors to background. Hamas’s October 7 massacre, Hezbollah’s arsenal and cross-border fire, Iranian sponsorship, Houthi attacks, Syrian instability, hostage-taking, and prior Arab state wars become scenery around Israel’s alleged drive for rule. This is not an argument about Israeli policy. It is a story in which Israel acts and everyone else merely reacts.

Its deeper function is scale distortion. Israel is a small state with unusual military reach because it faces nearby enemies, narrow borders, and a dense regional threat network. Calling it imperial gives antizionism a grand villain. The word makes Jewish power sound larger, darker, and more coordinated than it is.

Notes

1. Imperialism usually refers to extending dominion through territorial acquisition or governing and economic control over other territories and peoples. That definition can illuminate parts of the Israel-Palestinian dispute, especially settlement and control in the West Bank. It does not automatically cover every cross-border strike, intelligence operation, or alliance.

2. Israel returned Sinai to Egypt, withdrew from southern Lebanon, and dismantled the Gaza settlements. These facts do not answer every criticism of Israel, but they are hard to reconcile with a theory of uninterrupted territorial expansion.

3. The West Bank remains the strongest terrain for critics of Israeli expansion. Settlement growth and annexation proposals should be argued on their own facts.

4. The Nile-to-Euphrates version of “Greater Israel” is not the adopted border policy of the State of Israel. Fringe maximalists and far-right rhetoric exist, but antizionist use often converts them into proof of a state-wide secret plan.

Responses / Criticisms

It stretches a real concept until it loses force.

Empire is a serious term. It describes dominion, hierarchy, extraction, and rule over subject peoples. If every Israeli use of force becomes imperial, the word stops explaining and starts performing. A state can use force wrongly without being an empire. A state can occupy territory without matching the full imperial model. Precision matters.

It hides Israel’s withdrawals.

The empire label has trouble with Sinai, southern Lebanon, and Gaza. Israel gave up territory larger than the state itself to make peace with Egypt. It left southern Lebanon in 2000. It removed every settlement from Gaza in 2005. These decisions did not end the conflict, and they did not solve the West Bank. But they do interrupt the claim that Israeli history is a straight line of expansion.

It turns Israeli security power into Jewish over-power.

The label makes Israel seem vast. That matters because antisemitic imagination has long exaggerated Jewish influence. When a small Jewish state is described as an imperial force directing the region or bending the United States to its will, the old pattern returns in updated language. The target is no longer “the Jews” in open terms. It is “imperial Israel.” The effect can be similar.
