Definition

“Blood on your hands” is a phrase and a gesture used in anti-Israel activism to pin responsibility for Palestinian deaths. Protesters paint their palms red, raise red-hand signs, or stamp the red-hand icon on pins, posters, banners, graffiti, and social-media graphics. The image needs no caption: red hands mean blood, guilt, complicity. That is why it works. In ordinary political speech, the charge can land on governments, armies, corporations, or officials. In antizionist settings its target widens. Israel, Zionists, Jewish organizations, synagogues, Jewish students, community centers, and ordinary Jews with some tie to Israel can all be treated as answerable for the same alleged bloodshed. That slippage is where the danger sits. A protest outside a ministry or an embassy is one thing. The same phrase shouted at Jewish students, painted on Jewish homes, or displayed outside a synagogue is another. There it no longer reads as criticism of Israeli policy. It tells Jews they carry blood guilt because of their presumed relationship to Israel. The red-hand image also carries a particular weight for Jews. It calls up the medieval blood libel, and it calls up the October 12, 2000 Ramallah lynching, when Aziz Salha was photographed at a Palestinian police-station window holding up his blood-covered hands after a mob murdered two Israeli reservists. Not every activist who paints a palm knows that history. The effect does not wait for their knowledge. The symbol lands in a Jewish memory already shaped by blood accusation and public Jewish vulnerability.

Common Usage

The phrase surged after the October 7, 2023 Hamas attacks and the war in Gaza that followed. Red hands appeared at protests, campus actions, public disruptions, graffiti, and celebrity activism. Its spread owed everything to its simplicity. No argument is required. A red hand delivers the verdict. The imagery turned up in vandalism and intimidation. In June 2024, the homes of Brooklyn Museum leaders, including Jewish director Anne Pasternak, were splashed with red paint and tagged with accusations including “Blood on your hands” and “White Supremacist Zionist.” That same spring, in May 2024, the Wall of the Righteous at the Paris Holocaust memorial was defaced with blood-red hands; French officials later investigated the act as possible foreign interference and antisemitic destabilization. Incident trackers caught the climate these symbols moved through. ADL recorded 9,354 antisemitic incidents in the United States in 2024, the highest annual total in the audit’s history, with 58 percent tied to Israel or Zionism. CST recorded 3,528 antisemitic incidents in the United Kingdom in 2024, its second-highest total ever. Those figures do not make every red-hand protest antisemitic. They show the environment in which “blood on your hands” crossed from protest language into pressure on Jewish life.

Implied Meaning

The phrase delivers guilt as already settled. It does not ask whether a given Israeli strike was lawful, whether civilian harm was avoidable, whether Hamas fought from within civilian buildings, whether hostages were nearby, or what choices a commander actually faced. It begins at the conclusion: there is blood, and it is on your hands. It also slides from the state to the group. A Jewish student who supports Israel’s existence, a synagogue praying for Israeli hostages, a Jewish cultural body with no link to Israeli policy—each becomes a fair target. In that slide, criticism of a government turns into an accusation against a people. The demand underneath is confession. The target is expected to admit guilt, denounce Israel, or wear the stain. That is why the phrase thrives in face-to-face confrontation. It does not invite a reply. It stages a shaming.

Origin / Lineage

The words “blood on your hands” are older than this conflict and turn up across every kind of politics. Red hands carry other histories too, including antiwar protest and campaigns against the use of child soldiers. The claim here is not that every red hand means one thing. In the Jewish and Israeli setting, though, the image cannot be separated from the Ramallah lynching of October 12, 2000. Two Israeli reservists, Yosef Avrahami and Vadim Nurzhitz, took a wrong turn into Ramallah and were brought to a Palestinian police station. A mob stormed the building, beat and stabbed them to death, and mutilated the bodies. Salha’s raised, blood-covered hands at the window became one of the defining images of the Second Intifada. He was arrested by Israel in 2001 and sentenced to life, released in the 2011 Gilad Shalit exchange, and deported to Gaza. Reuters reported in October 2024 that Hamas media and medics said he had been killed in an Israeli strike in Deir al-Balah; the report noted Israel did not immediately say whether he had been targeted. The blood-libel lineage gives the symbol its deeper charge. For centuries Jews were accused of using non-Jewish blood for ritual purposes, and those accusations fed trials, expulsions, pogroms, and murder. Modern “blood on your hands” activism rarely repeats the ritual-murder claim outright. It keeps the shape of it when aimed at Jews as Jews: bloodshed is laid at the feet of the group, and the group is told to answer.

Function

The phrase turns a hard war into an instant verdict of guilt. It compresses civilian casualties, the law of armed conflict, Hamas tactics, Israeli decision-making, hostage rescue, aid access, and ceasefire politics into one image, and draws its whole force from that compression. Inside antizionist circles, red hands also work as a badge. Displaying them shows you accept the charge against Israel; refusing them can be read as indifference or complicity. The gesture accuses outward and disciplines inward at the same time. Aimed at Jewish spaces, its function changes again. A synagogue, a Hillel, a Jewish school, a Jewish business is not the Israeli government. Most diaspora Jews command no soldiers and write no Israeli law. Red-hand imagery at those doors treats them as liable anyway. The result is not protest but the assignment of blood guilt—which is exactly why the phrase belongs in a glossary of antizionist language. It marks the point where a public political claim crosses into collective Jewish culpability.

Notes

  1. Magda Teter’s Blood Libel: On the Trail of an Antisemitic Myth (2020) traces the rise and persistence of the ritual-murder myth across European history. The relevance here is in the shape, not the letter. The modern slogan does not usually accuse Jews of ritual murder, but it can reproduce a pattern of blood guilt when Jews are targeted as a group.
  2. The Ramallah connection should be stated with care. Red hands have served other causes, and some users may intend only a generic antiwar guilt. The specific Jewish objection is that, after October 2000, raised blood-covered hands became an iconic image of murdered Israelis. Ignorance of that history lessens intent; it does not erase reception.
  3. The Artists4Ceasefire red-hand pin worn at the 2024 Academy Awards should not be cast as proven endorsement of the Ramallah lynching. The group’s stated aim was ceasefire, hostages, and humanitarian aid. The stronger criticism is visual and historical: a red-hand icon used during a war against Israel predictably calls up blood-guilt language and the Ramallah image for many Jews.
  4. The Brooklyn Museum and Paris memorial incidents are firm examples, better than vague claims about generic protest use. Both involved red paint or red hands applied to Jewish or Jewish-linked targets and were reported by major wire or mainstream outlets.

Responses / Criticisms

It converts war criticism into collective blood guilt. A serious protest can attack Israeli policy, military decisions, civilian harm, or government conduct. “Blood on your hands” often does something else. It moves responsibility from the people who make decisions to a wider class of people tied to Israel. When that class includes Jews as Jews, the phrase has crossed the line. It decides guilt before the evidence. The phrase does not build a legal case. It announces one. It draws no line between lawful and unlawful strikes, between military necessity and negligence, between a combatant’s responsibility and a civilian tragedy. It ignores how the symbol lands on Jews. Some activists insist the red hand is only an antiwar sign. That cannot end the matter. Symbols are not governed solely by the intentions of whoever raises them. In this setting, red hands summon the Ramallah lynching, the older blood-guilt charge, and the public marking of Jews as answerable for death. A movement that refuses to see that reception is not careless. It is choosing not to care.
