Glossary Entry

BLOOD ON YOUR HANDS

“Blood on your hands” is both a chant and a piece of theater. Protesters paint their palms red and raise them toward the cameras. The same red hand appears on pins and posters, in graffiti and online graphics. The image needs no caption because everyone reads it instantly: red hands mean blood, and blood means guilt. That immediacy is the source of its power. A slogan must be heard; a red hand only has to be seen. In ordinary political life the phrase has a long résumé. It has been hurled at governments and generals, at corporations and presidents. Used that way, it stays within the rough boundaries of democratic speech. In antizionist settings the target widens. Israel comes first, then Zionists, then Jewish organizations and students and congregations with no role in Israeli decision-making. Ordinary Jews with any connection to Israel can find themselves treated as answerable for the same alleged bloodshed. That widening is where the danger lives.

Where the red hand appears matters as much as what it says. A protest outside a ministry or an embassy is one thing. The same gesture shouted at Jewish students or painted on Jewish homes or displayed outside a synagogue is another. In those settings it no longer reads as criticism of Israeli policy. It informs Jews that they carry blood guilt because of who they are presumed to be. The audience has changed, and with it the meaning. For Jews the image arrives carrying old freight, echoing both the medieval blood libel and a specific photograph from the Second Intifada that this entry takes up below. Not every activist who raises red hands knows that history. The symbol lands anyway, in a Jewish memory shaped by blood accusation and public vulnerability.

The gesture surged after the Hamas attacks of October 7, 2023 and the war that followed. Red hands appeared at street protests and campus actions, in graffiti and celebrity activism. The spread owed much to simplicity. No argument is required and none is offered. A red hand supplies the conclusion and leaves the audience to assume the case was made somewhere else. In March 2024, red-hand pins appeared at the Academy Awards through the Artists4Ceasefire campaign. The group described the pin as a call for a ceasefire and humanitarian aid, along with the release of the hostages. Fairness requires saying plainly that the pin was never proven homage to the Ramallah lynching. Yet many Jews could not watch a red hand raised during a war against Israel and see anything else. A symbol can be intended one way and received another. When its shape collides with a traumatic communal photograph, intention alone does not settle the matter.

The imagery also turned up in cases of outright intimidation. In June 2024, activists hurled red paint at the homes of Brooklyn Museum leaders, among them the museum’s Jewish director Anne Pasternak, alongside accusations of “Blood on your hands” and “White Supremacist Zionist.” The month before, vandals defaced the Wall of the Righteous at the Paris Holocaust memorial with blood-red hands. That wall honors French citizens who rescued Jews during the Second World War. French officials later investigated the act as possible foreign interference meant to inflame antisemitic tension. These cases carry more weight than any generic protest, because here the red hand was applied directly to Jewish targets.

Incident trackers recorded the climate in which all of this circulated. The ADL counted 9,354 antisemitic incidents in the United States in 2024, the highest annual total in the history of its audit. Fifty-eight percent of those incidents contained elements related to Israel or Zionism.

Across the Atlantic, CST recorded 3,528 antisemitic incidents in the United Kingdom that year, its second-highest total on record. These figures do not prove that every red-hand protest is antisemitic. They do show the atmosphere in which “blood on your hands” traveled from protest language into pressure on Jewish communities.

The slogan treats guilt as settled business. It does not ask whether a given strike was lawful or whether the harm could have been avoided. It does not ask whether Hamas fought from civilian areas or whether hostages were nearby. It begins where an honest inquiry would end: there is blood, and it is on your hands. The questions that would complicate the charge are precisely the ones the gesture is built to skip.

The demand underneath the gesture is confession. The target is expected to admit guilt and denounce Israel, or else wear the stain. That is why the red hand thrives in public confrontation: it does not invite a reply, it stages a shaming.

The words themselves are far older than the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and appear across political life. Red hands carry other histories too, including anti-war campaigns and protests against the use of child soldiers. No one should claim the symbol has a single origin or a single meaning. For Jews and Israelis, though, the image cannot be separated from one day. On October 12, 2000, reservists Yosef Avrahami and Vadim Nurzhitz took a wrong turn into Ramallah and were brought to a Palestinian police station. A mob stormed the building, beat and stabbed the two men to death, and mutilated their bodies. Salha’s raised crimson palms, photographed at the station window, became an emblem of the era.

Israel arrested Salha in 2001 and a court sentenced him to life in prison. He walked free in the 2011 Gilad Shalit prisoner exchange and was deported to Gaza. In October 2024, Reuters reported that Hamas media and medics said he had been killed in an Israeli airstrike in Deir al-Balah. Israel did not immediately say whether he had been deliberately targeted. The man behind the most infamous red hands in Israeli memory thus reentered the news in the middle of the very war that revived the symbol.

The older lineage gives the image still another layer. For centuries Jews were accused of using non-Jewish blood for ritual purposes, an invention that fueled trials and expulsions and pogroms. Magda Teter’s Blood Libel: On the Trail of an Antisemitic Myth traces that myth’s astonishing endurance across European history. Modern red-hand activism does not repeat the ritual-murder charge, and the parallel should not be overdrawn. The relevance is the pattern: bloodshed gets attributed to Jews as a group, and the group is told to answer for it. Ignorance of that history may soften the intent of any individual protester. It does not change how the image is received.

What the gesture accomplishes is compression. Civilian casualties and military law, Hamas tactics and hostage rescue, aid access and ceasefire politics—all of it shrinks into a single red print. Within the movement, the hands also mark loyalty. Displaying them announces acceptance of the case against Israel; declining to display them can be read as indifference. The symbol accuses outward and disciplines inward at the same time. Few gestures perform both jobs so efficiently.

Aimed at Jewish spaces, the meaning shifts once more. A synagogue or a Hillel or a Jewish community center is not the Israeli government. Most diaspora Jews command no soldiers and write no Israeli laws. Red hands at those doorsteps hold them liable anyway. At that point the display stops being protested and becomes an assignment of blood guilt. That crossing, from public grievance to collective Jewish culpability—is exactly why the phrase belongs in a glossary of antizionist language.
