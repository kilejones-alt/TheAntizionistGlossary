By Any Means Necessary

Definition

“By any means necessary” is a formula for permission. In ordinary speech it signals determination, urgency, a refusal to wait. In antizionist usage—especially after October 7, 2023—it elevates Palestinian liberation above the ordinary limits on action and leaves the method deliberately unspecified. That ambiguity is the danger. The slogan does not say “kill civilians,” “take hostages,” or “fire rockets at towns.” It simply declines to draw the boundary. Applied to Palestinian armed action against Israel, the open boundary becomes a mechanism for excusing attacks on civilians.

The wording owes its American fame to Malcolm X. On June 28, 1964, at the founding rally of the Organization of Afro-American Unity in New York, he declared that the OAAU sought freedom, justice, and equality “by any means necessary.” He was speaking of Black self-defense against racist violence and state failure. The OAAU charter invoked the UN Charter, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the United States Constitution, and the Bill of Rights. That setting matters—the words were never a blank check for harm. The laws of war reject the premise that a just cause licenses every method. The principle of distinction requires parties to separate civilians from combatants. Hostage-taking is prohibited. Intentional attacks on civilians are war crimes. A movement may claim liberation, resistance, or self-defense—none of those claims erases civilian protection.

Common Usage

After October 7, the slogan traveled alongside “resistance in all its forms,” “From the River to the Sea,” and “Globalize the Intifada.” Together these expressions converted Palestinian violence into a liberation story while never naming the specific acts committed against Israeli civilians. The clearest campus example is National Students for Justice in Palestine’s October 2023 Day of Resistance Toolkit. The toolkit described the Hamas-led attack as a “historic win for the Palestinian resistance,” urged chapters to “normalize the resistance,” declared that confrontation was required “by any means necessary,” listed armed struggle among legitimate forms of resistance, and asserted that settlers are not civilians under international law. That final claim is not rhetorical excess—it is a direct assault on civilian status itself.

Within this usage, “by any means necessary” no longer operates as a general civil-rights slogan. It functions inside a vocabulary that treats October 7 as a breakthrough in liberation. That is why the borrowed Malcolm X language matters: the source setting involved self-defense under domestic racial terror, while the later use attached the same words to an armed assault that included civilian murders and hostage-taking. The Majdal Shams attack of July 27, 2024 exposes the problem with slogans that bless resistance before identifying the target. A rocket struck a football field in the Druze village of Majdal Shams and killed twelve children and teenagers. Israel and the United States blamed Hezbollah; Hezbollah denied responsibility. The victims were not combatants by any definition. A slogan that cannot clearly condemn such an attack has already abandoned the one rule it needs most.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that the goal governs the act. Civilian protection, hostage prohibitions, and the laws of war become secondary to liberation. The slogan need not approve a specific massacre in plain words—it creates the space for approval by refusing to name a limit. This is an abstraction doing the work. A speaker rarely says, “I support the murder of civilians at a music festival.” The speaker says “by any means necessary,” or “resistance in all its forms.” The formula keeps the speaker distant from the bodies while leaving the violence available for praise.

Fanon’s influence operates here. In The Wretched of the Earth, he assigned anti-colonial violence a psychological and redemptive meaning, not merely a tactical one. Transferred to Israel, violence can be recast as dignity, return, or liberation even when the victims are civilians—and the question of what actually happened becomes less important than the meaning assigned to it. Malcolm X spoke from a different position. He addressed self-defense where the government failed to protect Black Americans from racist violence. He did not speak of massacring concertgoers, kidnapping children, or treating entire civilian communities as military assets. The antizionist transfer keeps the urgency and discards the limiting facts.

Origin / Lineage

The American force of the wording comes from Malcolm X and the OAAU. His June 28, 1964 speech made it memorable: freedom, justice, and equality “by any means necessary.” The same speech tied self-defense to circumstances in which the government was unable or unwilling to protect Black life. A second line runs through anti-colonial theory. Fanon’s The Wretched of the Earth argued that violence could become part of the process by which colonized people recover agency, and Jean-Paul Sartre’s preface carried that argument into European intellectual life. Later campus writing absorbed this tradition in simplified form: decolonization became struggle, struggle became resistance, and resistance became difficult to judge by ordinary limits.

A third line is Palestinian militant rhetoric. Hamas’s 2017 Document of General Principles and Policies states that resisting occupation “with all means and methods” is legitimate and that armed resistance lies at its center. The same document rejects recognition of Israel’s legitimacy and calls for the full liberation of Palestine from the river to the sea. In that setting, “all means” is not an abstract flourish—it sits inside a program that preserves armed conflict against Israel. After October 7, these lines converged. Malcolm X supplied the English prestige. Fanon supplied the decolonial vocabulary. Hamas supplied militant doctrine. Campus groups translated the combination into toolkits, chants, posters, and teach-ins.

Function

The slogan pre-clears violence. It does not prove that a particular attack is lawful—it avoids that test entirely. It asks the audience to judge the act by the aim, not by the victim. It also works as a loyalty test. A person who refuses “resistance in all its forms” can be branded weak, colonial, or indifferent to Palestinian suffering. Jewish students are especially exposed to this pressure because they are routinely asked to condemn Israel while others are never asked to condemn Hamas.

Finally, the slogan reverses scrutiny. If someone raises Nova, Be’eri, hostage-taking, sexual violence, or rocket fire, the question itself can be recast as bad faith, and the demand for civilian protection becomes an obstacle to liberation. That is the deeper function: it trains listeners to excuse the next attack before they know what it is.

Notes

Malcolm X’s OAAU speech should not be stripped from its setting. The speech invoked freedom, justice, and equality by any means necessary, but the same address and charter language tied the claim to self-defense, constitutional rights, human rights, and government failure to protect Black Americans.

The NSJP Day of Resistance Toolkit is the strongest evidence of the need to rebuke the way this phrase gets used. It uses the phrase “by any means necessary,” urges students to normalize resistance, describes October 7 as a historic win, and claims settlers are not civilians under international law. That package matters more than the slogan alone.

Hamas’s 2017 document uses the phrase “all means and methods” and places armed resistance at the center of its program. The wording comes from Hamas’s own document.

Responses / Criticisms

It borrows self-defense language and drops the limiter.

Malcolm X spoke in the setting of Black self-defense against racist violence and government failure. Antizionist usage keeps the urgency while removing the boundary. That transfer changes the phrase into something its author never said.

It treats civilian status as negotiable.

The NSJP toolkit’s claim that settlers are not civilians under international law is not a minor flourish. It is the move that makes civilian protection easier to discard—and once that move is accepted, no community is safe from reclassification.

It evades the laws of war.

A liberation claim does not answer the legal questions. Who was targeted? Were civilians attacked directly? Were hostages taken? Were protected people harmed? “Any means” avoids those questions by making the goal do the work the evidence cannot.

It prepares people to excuse the next attack.

The slogan is dangerous because it operates in advance. It tells the audience that whatever comes next may already be justified, so long as it can be called resistance. By the time the bodies are counted, the verdict has already been issued.
