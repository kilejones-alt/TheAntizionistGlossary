By Any Means Necessary

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “By Any Means Necessary” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Definition

“By any means necessary” is a permission formula. In ordinary use, it can mean determination, urgency, or refusal to wait. In antizionist use, especially after October 7, 2023, it often does more. It places Palestinian liberation above the usual limits on action and leaves the method undefined.

That ambiguity is the danger. The phrase does not always say, "kill civilians," "take hostages," or "fire rockets at towns." It simply leaves the boundary open. When applied to Palestinian armed action against Israel, the open boundary can become a way to excuse attacks on civilians while keeping the speaker one step away from the details.

The phrase became famous in the United States through Malcolm X. On June 28, 1964, at the founding rally of the Organization of Afro-American Unity in New York, he said the OAAU wanted freedom, justice, and equality "by any means necessary." In that setting, he was speaking about Black self-defense against racist violence and state failure. The OAAU charter also invoked the UN Charter, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the U.S. Constitution, and the Bill of Rights. That context matters. It was not a blank check for harming uninvolved people.

The laws of war reject the idea that a just cause licenses every method. The principle of distinction requires parties to distinguish between civilians and combatants. Hostage-taking is prohibited. Intentional attacks on civilians are war crimes. A movement can claim liberation, resistance, or self-defense. None of those claims erases civilian protection.

Common Usage

After October 7, the phrase appeared alongside "resistance in all its forms," "From the River to the Sea," and "Globalize the Intifada." These expressions helped convert Palestinian violence into a liberation story while avoiding the specific acts committed against Israeli civilians.

The clearest campus example is National Students for Justice in Palestine's October 2023 Day of Resistance Toolkit. The toolkit described the Hamas-led attack as a "historic win for the Palestinian resistance." It urged chapters to "normalize the resistance," said confrontation was required "by any means necessary," listed armed struggle among legitimate forms of resistance, and claimed that settlers are not civilians in the sense of international law. That last point is not rhetorical excess. It is a direct attack on civilian status.

Within this usage, "by any means necessary" does not operate as a general civil-rights slogan. It becomes part of a vocabulary that treats October 7 as a breakthrough in liberation. That is why the borrowed Malcolm X language matters. The source context involved self-defense under domestic racial terror. The later use attached the phrase to an armed assault that included civilian murders and hostage-taking.

The Majdal Shams attack on July 27, 2024 shows the problem with slogans that treat resistance as legitimate before identifying the target. A rocket struck a football field in the Druze village of Majdal Shams, killing twelve children and teenagers. Israel and the United States blamed Hezbollah; Hezbollah denied responsibility. The victims were not combatants. A slogan that cannot clearly condemn such an attack has already abandoned the rule it needs most.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that the goal governs the act. Civilian protection, hostage prohibitions, and the laws of war become secondary to liberation. The phrase does not need to approve a specific massacre in plain words. It creates space for approval by refusing to name a limit.

This is how abstraction does the work. A speaker may not say, "I support the murder of civilians at a music festival." The speaker says, "by any means necessary," or "resistance in all its forms." The slogan keeps the speaker distant from the bodies while leaving the violence available for praise.

Fanon's influence matters here. In The Wretched of the Earth, he gave anti-colonial violence a psychological and redemptive meaning, not only a tactical one. When this thought is applied to Israel, violence can be recast as dignity, return, or liberation even when the victims are civilians. The question of what actually happened can then be treated as less important than the story assigned to it.

Malcolm X's use had a different setting. He spoke of self-defense where government failed to protect Black Americans from racist violence. He did not speak about massacring concertgoers, kidnapping children, or treating whole civilian communities as military assets. The antizionist transfer keeps the urgency and discards the limiting facts.

Origin / Lineage

The phrase's American force comes from Malcolm X and the OAAU. His June 28, 1964 speech made the wording memorable: freedom, justice, and equality "by any means necessary." The same speech tied self-defense to situations where government was unable or unwilling to protect Black life.

A second line runs through anti-colonial theory. Fanon's The Wretched of the Earth argued that violence could become part of the process by which colonized people recover agency. Jean-Paul Sartre's preface helped carry that argument into European intellectual life. Later campus writing often absorbed this tradition in simplified form: decolonization became struggle, struggle became resistance, and resistance became difficult to judge by ordinary limits.

A third line is Palestinian militant rhetoric. Hamas's 2017 Document of General Principles and Policies states that resisting occupation "with all means and methods" is legitimate and that armed resistance lies at its center. The same document rejects recognition of Israel's legitimacy and calls for the full liberation of Palestine from the river to the sea. In that context, "all means" is not an abstract phrase. It sits inside a program that preserves armed conflict against Israel.

After October 7, these lines converged. Malcolm X supplied the English prestige. Fanon supplied the decolonial vocabulary. Hamas supplied the militant doctrine. Campus groups translated the combination into toolkits, chants, posters, and teach-ins.

Function

The phrase pre-clears violence. It does not prove that a particular attack is lawful. It avoids that test. It asks the audience to judge the act by the aim, not by the victim.

It also works as a loyalty test. A person who refuses "resistance in all its forms" can be treated as weak, colonial, or indifferent to Palestinian suffering. Jewish students are especially exposed to this pressure because they are often asked to condemn Israel while others are not asked to condemn Hamas.

The phrase also reverses scrutiny. If someone asks about Nova, Be'eri, hostage-taking, sexual violence, or rocket fire, the question itself can be recast as bad faith. The demand for civilian protection becomes an obstacle to liberation. That is the slogan's deeper function: it trains listeners to excuse the next attack before they know what it is.

Notes

1. Malcolm X's OAAU speech should not be stripped from its setting. The speech invoked freedom, justice, and equality by any means necessary, but the same address and charter language tied the claim to self-defense, constitutional rights, human rights, and government failure to protect Black Americans.

2. The NSJP Day of Resistance Toolkit is the strongest direct source for this entry. It uses the phrase "by any means necessary," urges students to normalize resistance, describes October 7 as a historic win, and claims settlers are not civilians under international law. That package is more important than the slogan alone.

3. Hamas's 2017 document uses the phrase "all means and methods" and places armed resistance at the center of its program. The wording comes from Hamas's own document, preserved in English translation by Palquest and attributed there to hamas.ps.

4. The Majdal Shams facts should remain carefully phrased. Reuters and AP reported that the attack killed twelve children and teenagers, that Israel blamed Hezbollah, and that Hezbollah denied responsibility. Later U.S. statements also blamed Hezbollah. The entry should avoid stating more than the cited evidence supports.

5. The legal point is narrow. A cause may explain why a group fights. It does not legalize attacks on civilians or hostage-taking. ICRC Customary IHL Rules 1 and 96, Common Article 3, Geneva Convention IV Article 34, and Rome Statute Article 8 are the relevant anchors.

Responses / Criticisms

It borrows self-defense language and drops the limiter.

Malcolm X spoke in the setting of Black self-defense against racist violence and government failure. Antizionist usage often keeps the urgency while removing the boundary. That transfer changes the phrase.

It treats civilian status as negotiable.

The NSJP toolkit's claim that settlers are not civilians under international law is not a minor flourish. It is the move that makes civilian protection easier to discard.

It evades the laws of war.

A liberation claim does not answer the legal questions. Who was targeted? Were civilians attacked directly? Were hostages taken? Were protected people harmed? "Any means" avoids those questions by making the goal do the work.

It prepares people to excuse the next attack.

The phrase is dangerous because it operates in advance. It tells the audience that whatever comes next may already be justified, so long as it can be called resistance.
