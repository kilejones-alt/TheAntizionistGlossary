Globalize the Intifada

An entry tracing the history, usage, implied meaning, lineage, and function of “Globalize the Intifada” in modern antizionist vocabulary.

Definition

“Globalize the Intifada” is a protest slogan that calls for the intifada to spread beyond Israel and the Palestinian territories. The Arabic word intifada means shaking off or uprising. In Palestinian history, it refers especially to the First Intifada beginning in 1987 and the Second Intifada beginning in 2000. The second included suicide bombings, shootings, stabbings, lynchings, and mass-casualty attacks against Israeli civilians. That history travels with the word whether the speaker admits it or not.

That history matters. The First Intifada included strikes, demonstrations, stone-throwing, riots, and fatal attacks. The Second Intifada became far more deadly. It included suicide bombings and attacks on buses, restaurants, city streets, hotels, clubs, and other civilian sites. ADL summarizes the Second Intifada as a period in which Palestinian violence turned into deadly terrorism targeting Israeli civilians on buses, in restaurants, and in city streets; more than 1,000 Israelis were killed and thousands were severely injured.

The phrase is therefore not just a call to notice Palestinian suffering. It asks listeners to universalize a named campaign associated, in Jewish and Israeli memory, with bombed buses, cafes, and Passover seders. Not every person who repeats it knows that history or intends every implication. But the words do not become harmless because some users prefer the softer translation. A slogan that asks for intifada to be made global carries the memory of what intifada has meant in practice.

Common Usage

After October 7, 2023, the phrase became common at anti-Israel demonstrations in the United States, Britain, Australia, and Europe. It appeared in street protests, campus encampments, placards, social-media graphics, and chants outside Jewish or Israel-linked sites. Its public use became controversial because it does not only name Palestinian liberation; it names a method of confrontation already tied to civilian fear.

The phrase reached a major American hearing on December 5, 2023, when Representative Elise Stefanik questioned Harvard President Claudine Gay about campus calls for “intifada” and “intifada revolution.” Gay called the rhetoric personally abhorrent but did not give the categorical answer Stefanik sought about whether such language violated Harvard’s bullying and harassment rules. The exchange became one of the clearest examples of institutional hesitation around anti-Jewish intimidation after October 7.

The fear attached to the phrase is measurable. The American Jewish Committee’s State of Antisemitism in America 2025 Report found that 88 percent of U.S. Jews said seeing or hearing “Globalize the Intifada” would make them feel unsafe to some degree; only 12 percent said it would not make them feel unsafe at all. AJC describes the phrase as a call for aggressive resistance against Israel and those who support Israel, and notes that most American Jews understand it as encouraging violence against Jews, Israelis, and their supporters.

The phrase also acquired renewed law-enforcement significance after major antisemitic attacks abroad. In November 2024, Israeli Maccabi Tel Aviv soccer supporters were attacked in Amsterdam; Reuters reported that Mayor Femke Halsema described the attackers as “antisemitic hit-and-run squads,” while also noting anti-Arab conduct by some Maccabi fans before the match. On December 14, 2025, gunmen killed 15 people at a Hanukkah celebration at Bondi Beach in Sydney. AP reported that the alleged attackers opened fire with shotguns and a hunting rifle at hundreds of people celebrating Hanukkah. New South Wales later moved to ban “globalise the intifada,” with the state government calling the chant hate speech and encouragement of violence.

In Britain, the Metropolitan Police and Greater Manchester Police announced on December 17, 2025, that people using “globalise the intifada” at future protests or in a targeted way should expect police action and arrests. Their joint statement said the context had changed, that words have meaning and consequence, and that officers would use public-order powers, including around London synagogues during services.

Implied Meaning

The implied meaning is that the battlefield is everywhere. Once the intifada is globalized, the line between Israeli soldiers and Jews abroad becomes harder to maintain. A Jewish student, a synagogue, a Jewish community center, an Israeli soccer fan, or a company with Israeli ties can be treated as part of the same enemy network.

This is why maps and targeted protest materials matter. In November 2023, Within Our Lifetime temporarily posted maps of New York Jewish organizations, saying they had “blood on their hands”; the New York Jewish Week reported that the group calls for “intifada” and Israel’s destruction, and that the posts drew NYPD attention and public condemnation. The Boston Mapping Project had already supplied a similar model in 2022, naming hundreds of organizations and ending with the line that “every entity has an address, every network can be disrupted,” according to ADL.

The phrase also places Jewish fear outside the bounds of sympathy. If Jews say the slogan sounds like a threat, they are often told they misunderstand Arabic, fear Palestinian freedom, or confuse criticism of Israel with antisemitism. That answer evades the problem. The issue is not the dictionary meaning of a single Arabic word. The issue is the historical record attached to the word and the settings in which it is now directed at Jews.

Origin / Lineage

The phrase depends on the prestige of revolutionary language and the ambiguity of translation. Intifada can mean uprising. In Palestinian use it also names specific wars of confrontation with Israel. The First Intifada began in 1987. The Second Intifada began in 2000 and became associated internationally with suicide bombings and mass-casualty attacks against Israeli civilians.

The contemporary slogan takes that local history and turns it outward. Earlier protest language focused on Palestine itself: “Free Palestine,” “End the Occupation,” or “From the River to the Sea.” “Globalize the Intifada” does something more aggressive. It does not only demand a change in Israeli conduct. It asks supporters to export the struggle.

The line also sits inside a longer antizionist habit of treating Jewish institutions abroad as proxies for Israel. Soviet anti-Zionist propaganda portrayed Zionism as a worldwide system. Arab nationalist and Islamist literature often described Zionism as a global enemy. “Globalize the Intifada” puts those habits into one command: make the struggle local, wherever Jews and Israel-linked institutions can be found.

Function

Jewish institutions, Israeli-linked events, Zionist speakers, synagogues, campus Hillels, businesses, and cultural organizations can then be treated as local fronts in a worldwide struggle.

It also supplies deniability. When challenged, supporters can say intifada only means uprising, resistance, or liberation. When shouted in a crowd after October 7, or displayed near Jewish spaces, it carries a harsher meaning. The ambiguity is useful. It lets the slogan sound poetic to outsiders while sounding threatening to Jews who remember what the Second Intifada did.

Governments and police agencies have increasingly treated that ambiguity as a safety issue. New South Wales moved to prohibit the chant after Bondi. The Metropolitan Police and Greater Manchester Police said they would arrest people who used it in targeted protest settings. Those decisions are contested on free-speech grounds, especially in jurisdictions with broader protest protections. But the legal debate itself confirms the core point: the phrase no longer operates as a neutral expression of solidarity. It is now widely understood as language capable of intimidation.

Notes

1. The phrase should not be analyzed only through literal translation. “Intifada” has a dictionary meaning, but it also has a conflict history.

2. Not every speaker intends suicide bombings or attacks on Jews. That does not settle the matter. Public slogans are judged by history, context, and foreseeable effect, not only by private intent.

3. The strongest objection is not that the phrase criticizes Israel. The strongest objection is that it carries the memory of civilian targeting into Jewish life outside Israel.

4. AJC’s 2025 polling shows why the phrase cannot be treated as harmless: 88 percent of U.S. Jews said hearing or seeing it would make them feel unsafe to some degree.

5. The Amsterdam and Bondi examples should be used carefully. They do not prove that every chant causes an attack. They show why Jewish communities and public authorities began treating calls to globalize intifada as a concrete safety concern.

6. The phrase often appears alongside other maximalist slogans. In that setting, “globalize” does not sound like peace activism. It sounds like expansion of the conflict into civilian space.

Responses / Criticisms

It is not only a metaphor once it names a campaign of worldwide confrontation.

The phrase does not simply ask people to care about Palestinians. It asks them to spread the logic of intifada into cities, campuses, transit hubs, synagogues, and Jewish neighborhoods far from Israel. The Second Intifada is not an abstract symbol for Jews and Israelis. It is remembered as a period of mass civilian fear.

It turns Jewish public life into an extension of the battlefield.

The slogan blurs Israel and Jews outside Israel. A Jewish student, a Jewish community center, a synagogue, an Israeli soccer fan, or a company with Israeli ties can all become a site of mobilization. That is why maps, chants, and protest actions matter. They do not only express anger at a government. They identify targets and move pressure into ordinary Jewish life.

It uses the language of resistance to excuse intimidation.

The word intifada carries a history of revolt, but it also carries a history of civilian targeting. Antizionist often ask listeners to accept the first meaning while ignoring the second. That double meaning is the point. The slogan can be defended as poetic resistance when challenged, then heard as permission for escalation when shouted in a crowd.
