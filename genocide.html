Genocide / Genocidal

Definition

In today’s antizionism, genocide has become the main charge against Israel, and the word has been cut loose from what it means in law. Genocide is a specific crime. The 1948 Genocide Convention defines it as acts done with the intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group because of what it is. Intent is the whole case. A high death toll is not genocide, heavy civilian losses are not genocide, and even disproportionate force, where it is proven, is not genocide. The drafters built the word that way on purpose, and the World Court has held the line: in Bosnia v. Serbia (2007) it ruled that when intent is read from a pattern of conduct, genocide must be the only reasonable explanation the conduct allows. If there is another plausible reason for the fighting—beating an armed enemy, urban warfare, counterinsurgency—the charge fails. That is not a technicality. It is the difference between a war, even a terrible one, and the crime of crimes.

Applied to Gaza, genocide is not a description of what is happening. It is a verdict handed down before the trial. It places Israel next to the Holocaust, the Holodomor, and Rwanda while no court has examined whether the intent was ever there. A person may condemn a single strike, a siege, or a war cabinet without claiming Israel set out to destroy the Palestinian people. The genocide charge skips that step. It announces the conclusion first and goes looking for the evidence afterward.

Common Usage

The word now gets used as though the matter were already settled. On campuses it rides the slogan it is not complicated, it is genocide, a line built to shut down the argument it pretends to make, and it drives divestment votes, faculty letters, and campaigns to keep Israeli scholars out of conferences and journals. It has also entered the formal system, where it carries a weight few stop to question. South Africa took the charge to the World Court in December 2023. The United Nations Special Rapporteur Francesca Albanese titled her March 2024 report Anatomy of a Genocide. Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch each said in December 2024 that Israel was committing genocide, and in September 2025 the International Association of Genocide Scholars passed a resolution saying the same. Repetition is doing the work that proof is supposed to do.

Implied Meaning

The charge does its heaviest damage around October 7, 2023. If Israel is the party committing genocide, then the murder of roughly twelve hundred people that day, the rapes, and the seizure of more than two hundred and forty hostages can be recast as resistance, and the worst single slaughter of Jews since the Holocaust quietly becomes something a person can defend. David Hirsh has described the way the argument seals itself shut: every Israeli act of war is folded back in as more proof, so nothing is ever allowed to count against the charge. A claim that cannot be tested is not a finding. It is an article of faith.

Origin / Lineage

The genocide charge against Israel did not start with this war. It has a long track record, and that record shows the same accusation arriving again and again, ahead of the facts that are supposed to back it. The people who wrote the 1948 Convention saw the misuse coming. Secretary-General Trygve Lie warned that stretching the word too wide would wreck the treaty, and the drafting record states plainly that heavy civilian losses in ordinary war do not, as a rule, amount to genocide. When Syria moved to write forced displacement into the definition—aimed squarely at the new state of Israel—the Legal Committee voted it down twenty-nine to five, and even Egypt, then at war with Israel, called it a reach beyond anything genocide had ever meant.

The charge came back in 1982. United Nations General Assembly Resolution 37/123 called the Sabra and Shatila massacre an act of genocide and passed without a single no vote, even though the killing was carried out by Lebanese Phalangist militia. Antonio Cassese pointed out that the delegation behind it never argued the actual elements of the crime, and William Schabas concluded the word had been chosen to embarrass Israel, not to fit what happened. From the Durban conference in 2001 the language worked its way deep into international law, and in 2023 it reached the World Court. Six decades sit between Syria’s amendment and South Africa’s case. What stays the same is never the evidence. It is the defendant.

Function

Outside the courtroom, the word works as a loyalty test. Jewish students and professionals who keep any tie to Israel meet it as a demand to denounce, and for most Jews outside Israel that is no small thing, because Israel is family and faith, not a foreign abstraction. Norman Goda and Jeffrey Herf argue that the label is not only wrong on the facts; in practice it hands cover to the openly stated goal of Hamas to kill Jews. The charge does far more political work than it could ever prove.

There is a quieter move inside the scholarship itself. The genocide theorist Martin Shaw has proposed swapping the question what is genocide for what should genocide mean, and has called for dropping mass killing and intent from the list of required elements. That is a confession dressed up as a proposal. It admits that under the law as written, the defenders of Israel can show the test is not met, so the test gets rewritten until the verdict fits. When you have to change the definition to win, you have already lost the argument on the facts.

Notes

  1. The legal definition matters because it blocks the word from becoming a synonym for horror. Genocide was written to name the destruction of a protected group as such, not every war with massive civilian suffering. Once intent is removed, the word stops describing a crime and starts describing an emotional reaction.
  2. The Gaza accusation depends on collapsing Hamas and Palestinians into one body, then accusing Israel of targeting that body as a people. But Israel’s stated war aim, from the beginning, was the defeat of Hamas after October 7. That does not settle every legal question in Israel’s favor, but it does supply the competing explanation that genocide law requires accusers to exclude.
  3. The historical pattern should make the charge suspect before it is accepted. Israel was being pulled toward the genocide category in 1948, in 1982, at Durban in 2001, and again after October 7. The facts change, the wars change, the legal posture changes, but the conclusion arrives almost unchanged: Israel is named first, and the definition is adjusted afterward.

Responses / Criticisms

Genocide is a crime of intent. A body count is not intent.

This is the whole case, and it is law, not feeling. A war can be ugly, and break the rules in places, without being genocide, because genocide requires the specific aim of destroying a group as such. Israel said from the first day that it was fighting Hamas in answer to October 7, not the Palestinian people, and that stated aim is itself the competing explanation Bosnia v. Serbia says must be ruled out before intent can be found. You can judge the war harshly and still see that the bar for the worst crime there is has not been cleared.

A state out to destroy a people does not fight the way Israel fought.

Israel telephoned residents before strikes, dropped leaflets, sent recorded warnings and text messages in Arabic, and published gridded evacuation maps telling people which blocks to leave and where to go. It paused its own war over three rounds in September 2024 so the World Health Organization could vaccinate more than six hundred thousand Gazan children against polio. Military analysts such as John Spencer argue that Israel sent ground troops into close urban combat, at heavy cost to its own soldiers, because that path kills fewer civilians than leveling buildings from the air. It has opened investigations against its own troops and brought charges in some cases. None of this means the war was clean, and parts of it are disputed. Taken together, it is the conduct of an army trying to defeat Hamas, not of a regime trying to erase a people—because a regime bent on extermination warns no one, vaccinates no one, and prosecutes no one.

The courts that have ruled found other crimes—and that difference decides everything.

The World Court found plausible rights and ordered protective measures; it did not find genocide. The International Criminal Court found grounds for war crimes and crimes against humanity, and on its own record could not even reach extermination, which sits a step below genocide. A serious argument refuses to puff these rulings up into a genocide verdict, and refuses just as flatly to spin them into a clean record. Stand on what the courts actually said. It is more than enough.

The charge comes before the evidence and outlasts it.

The line from 1948 through 1982 to 2023 shows an accusation that returns no matter the facts of the particular war. It nearly tipped into farce in September 2025, when the International Association of Genocide Scholars passed its resolution. Of about five hundred members, only one hundred twenty-nine voted, one hundred eight of them in favor, in a body that admits non-experts to its rolls, while dissenters said the debate had been shut down. A rival letter, Scholars for Truth About Genocide, drew about one hundred seventy-eight signatures—more than the resolution itself—including former Nazi war-crimes prosecutors and major Holocaust historians, and demanded the measure be pulled. Their point was plain: cheapening the word genocide to land a political hit is its own kind of harm.

The humanitarian record is the hardest ground, and honesty wins it, not bluster.

Israel points to large amounts of food and supplies let in across the war, much of it tracked by its own agency COGAT, and to a Gaza population that grew under blockade instead of vanishing. Set against that is a hard fact this entry will not hide: from early March to mid-May 2025 Israel sealed Gaza off completely for about eleven weeks, the longest total blockade of the war, and that August an international panel declared famine in part of the strip. The starvation charge in the ICC warrants grows straight out of this. So the argument has to stay narrow and exact: a country can run a punishing siege, and answer for it as a war crime, without that siege proving an intent to destroy a people as such. Push the aid claim past that line and it snaps. Keep it there and it holds.

The intent the definition was written to catch is not hidden.

Here is where the whole record lands. The intent to wipe out a people is not hiding in this conflict, waiting to be guessed at from a map of airstrikes. It is written down, in plain language, in the Hamas charter of 1988. To convict Israel of genocide, you have to rewrite the definition of the crime. To find the intent the definition was written to catch, you only have to read what Hamas already published.
