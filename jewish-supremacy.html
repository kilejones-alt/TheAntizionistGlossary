Jewish Supremacy

The phrase translates Jewish national survival into domination.

DEFINITION

“Jewish supremacy” is a label used in anti-Israel and antizionist writing to describe Israel as a system organized to privilege Jews over Palestinians. It is not an invented phrase. B’Tselem used it in 2021 in the title of its paper, “A regime of Jewish supremacy from the Jordan River to the Mediterranean Sea: This is apartheid.” Adalah, the Legal Center for Arab Minority Rights in Israel, uses related language in legal and advocacy work. The phrase’s force comes from its resemblance to white supremacy. That resemblance is the point.

The phrase has a narrower and a broader use. In its narrower use, it is a legal claim about Israeli rule, the West Bank, settlements, East Jerusalem, Gaza, the Nation-State Law, land policy, and unequal treatment of Palestinians. Those subjects can be argued on their own evidence. In its broader antizionist use, the phrase does something else. It treats Jewish national self-determination itself as domination. The problem is no longer one law, one border, one settlement policy, or one government. The problem becomes the Jewish state because it is Jewish.

That is the key move. A serious critique can say Israel has civic inequalities, unresolved territorial disputes, settlement abuses, discriminatory land practices, or a flawed Nation-State Law. "Jewish supremacy" often converts those disputes into a status verdict: Jewish sovereignty is racism by definition. That shift is why the phrase belongs in this glossary.

COMMON USAGE

The phrase entered high-profile NGO usage before the Gaza war that began in October 2023. B'Tselem's 2021 position paper argued that the entire area between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean Sea is organized around one principle: advancing Jewish supremacy over Palestinians. Adalah's public materials on the Nation-State Law say the law enshrines Jewish supremacy over Palestinian citizens of Israel.

After October 7, the phrase spread more widely through campus protest, BDS material, social media, teach-ins, faculty letters, and activist graphics. It often appears near the words apartheid, settler colonialism, ethnostate, and Zionist racism. In that setting, the phrase is rarely confined to one disputed law or one part of the West Bank. It becomes a shorthand for Zionism itself.

The phrase also gives anti-Israel activism a familiar American grammar. White supremacy is one of the clearest civic evils in American public life. Transferring that grammar to Zionism gives the anti-Israel claim immediate force. The listener is not asked to decide whether a specific Israeli policy is lawful or unlawful. The listener is asked to decide whether to oppose supremacy.

IMPLIED MEANING

The phrase implies that Jewish collective rights are different in kind from the collective rights of other peoples. National symbols, a national language, Jewish immigration preference, Hebrew calendar references, Jewish holidays, and diaspora protection are treated not as features of a nation-state, but as proof of racial hierarchy.

Israel's 2018 Nation-State Basic Law is the strongest source for critics. It says the State of Israel is the nation-state of the Jewish people, that the right to national self-determination in Israel is exclusive to the Jewish people, that Hebrew is the language of the state, that Arabic has special status, that the state is open to Jewish immigration, and that Jewish settlement is a national value. Critics can reasonably argue that the law should have included an explicit equality clause and that its settlement language is dangerous when applied across the Green Line.

But the leap from those criticisms to supremacy is not automatic. Israel also has Basic Law: Human Dignity and Liberty, which protects human dignity and liberty and states that its purpose is to anchor the values of Israel as a Jewish and democratic state. Basic Law: The Knesset provides that every Israeli citizen eighteen or older is eligible to vote unless a court has deprived that person of the right under law. Arab citizens vote, run parties, sit in the Knesset, serve in government coalitions, argue before courts, and serve on courts. Justice Khaled Kabub became the first Muslim member of Israel's Supreme Court in 2022. Ra'am joined the Bennett-Lapid coalition in 2021, the first time an Arab party was a formal member of an Israeli governing coalition.

Those facts do not erase inequality. They do limit the claim. A country with Jewish national institutions and Arab civic participation is not usefully described by the same vocabulary used for regimes that deny the minority vote, exclude it from courts, or build law around permanent civic caste. The sharper criticism is that Israel contains a tension between Jewish national rights and minority equality. "Jewish supremacy" often collapses that tension into one word.

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

The phrase draws power from the American civil-rights vocabulary of white supremacy and from the South African anti-apartheid vocabulary of racial domination. In Israel-related use, it also grows out of the older "Zionism is racism" tradition. Soviet and Arab-state campaigns after 1967 treated Zionism as racial domination and helped push that wording into UN forums, especially Resolution 3379 in 1975. The resolution was repealed in 1991, but the vocabulary survived.

A second line runs through Israeli and Palestinian human-rights organizations. Yesh Din, B'Tselem, Adalah, and other organizations increasingly adopted apartheid and supremacy language in the years before and after 2020. Their strongest evidence usually concerns the West Bank, settlement rule, movement restrictions, differential legal systems, land use, and the constitutional symbolism of the Nation-State Law.

Once the phrase leaves a legal report, it loses qualifications. The distinction between Israel proper, East Jerusalem, the West Bank, Gaza, refugees, Arab citizens, noncitizen Palestinians, and Jewish Israelis often disappears. What remains is a slogan: Zionism equals supremacy.

FUNCTION

The phrase functions as a disqualification device. It tells the audience that Zionism does not need to be debated because it has already been placed inside the civic category reserved for racial domination. Once that placement is accepted, ordinary contact with Israel can be redescribed as complicity with supremacy.

It also shifts the burden onto Jews. A Jewish student, scholar, artist, donor, synagogue, Hillel chapter, or Israeli speaker can be asked to prove distance from Zionism before being treated as acceptable. The question becomes not "what do you think about this policy?" but "will you reject Jewish supremacy?" That wording makes support for Jewish national self-determination sound like support for racial hierarchy. The phrase is especially useful because it can move between softer and harder meanings. When challenged, the speaker can say it refers only to unequal Israeli policy. In movement use, it often means that a Jewish state is inherently illegitimate. The same words carry both meanings, which is why they travel so easily.

NOTES

1. B'Tselem and Adalah are the strongest direct sources for the phrase, and they earn it the way the slogan never bothers to—with evidence. B'Tselem's January 2021 position paper puts the entire territory between the Jordan River and the Mediterranean under one organizing principle, then names that principle apartheid. Adalah's materials on the 2018 Basic Law argue the statute raises Jews above Palestinian citizens of Israel as a constitutional matter. Both tie the claim to named laws, land policy, and movement rules a reader can go check. The campus version keeps the conclusion and quietly drops the proof.

2. The label survives only as long as nobody counts the Arab Israelis already inside the system. Israel's Supreme Court seated Khaled Kabub—a Muslim judge who'd spent decades on the Tel Aviv District bench—as its first permanent Muslim member on May 9, 2022, filling a retiring justice's seat through the regular appointments committee. Ra'am, an Islamist party led by Mansour Abbas, became the first independent Arab faction to sit in a coalition on June 2, 2021, trading its votes in the Knesset for billions of shekels aimed at Arab towns. A system built on permanent caste doesn't hand the people it keeps at the bottom a ballot, a robe, and a chair at the cabinet table. None of this clears a single Israeli policy. It just makes "supremacy" the wrong word for the complaint.

3. This charge isn't homegrown—it descends from a Cold War propaganda effort that predates every settlement and checkpoint now invoked to justify it. The UN General Assembly branded Zionism a form of racial discrimination through Resolution 3379 on November 10, 1975, a verdict Soviet and Arab-state delegations had spent years pushing for. Israel's ambassador Chaim Herzog tore the text apart at the podium. Sixteen years later that same body struck the language down through Resolution 46/86 on December 16, 1991, the price of getting Israel to the Madrid peace talks. The wording outlived its own repeal and came back word for word in the chants heard at today's protests.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

It borrows the language of white supremacy and assumes the resemblance proves the case.

White supremacy described a ruling caste that shut the other group out of the vote, the courts, and the schools. Israeli practice runs the other way: Arab citizens are roughly a fifth of the population, they elect their own parties, and in 2021 Ra'am became the first independent Arab faction to sit in a coalition. A year later Khaled Kabub took a permanent seat on the Supreme Court, the first Muslim to hold one. Those facts don't erase the real gaps in how Arab citizens are treated, but a system designed to keep Jews on top would not seat the minority in parliament or on its highest court. The label carries the weight of a different history without showing that the same conditions exist here.

It's a verdict standing in for a description.

The phrase tends to arrive before the evidence rather than after it. Once you assume a Jewish state is wrong because it's Jewish, ordinary features start reading as proof—the Law of Return, Hebrew on official forms, the symbols on the flag. Read the same country without that assumption and you find a vote extended to every citizen over eighteen and Arab judges ruling against government ministers. A serious complaint can name the statute and the harm: settlement rule, the missing equality clause in the 2018 Nation-State Law, unequal land allocation. "Supremacy" skips that specific work and reaches the conclusion first.

It isn't a finding—it's a recycled Soviet slogan.

The claim that Zionism equals racism didn't begin in a courtroom or on a campus; the UN General Assembly adopted it as Resolution 3379 on November 10, 1975, after years of Soviet and Arab-state lobbying. The same body found it indefensible enough to repeal on December 16, 1991. The wording faded and then came back almost word for word after October 2023 gave it new use. No other nation's self-rule draws the same label—not Greek, not Japanese, not Irish—which points to an objection aimed at this state's existence rather than any policy it follows.
