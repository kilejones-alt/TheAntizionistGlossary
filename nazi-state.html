Nazi analogy / historical inversion / demonization

Nazi State

The phrase uses the Holocaust’s weight against the Jewish state.

DEFINITION

“Nazi State” is an antizionist label that presents Israel as the successor to Hitler’s Germany. It does not say only that a law, minister, military action, or coalition is wrong. It turns the country itself into the present form of Nazism.

That comparison depends on a severe historical distortion. Nazi Germany was a one-party racial dictatorship built on state terror, conquest, antisemitic law, and the systematic murder of six million Jews. The United States Holocaust Memorial Museum describes the Holocaust as the systematic, state-sponsored persecution and murder of six million European Jews by Nazi Germany, its allies, and collaborators. The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 stripped Jews of citizenship and banned marriage and sexual relations between Jews and Germans defined as non-Jewish under Nazi race law.

Israel is a Jewish-majority country with grave conflicts, contested laws, military rule in disputed territory, internal inequality, and fierce public debate. It is not a Nazi state. It has elections, opposition parties, courts, Arab citizens, non-Jewish judges, public dissent, and military conduct subject to legal review. None of those facts makes Israel immune from criticism. They do make the Nazi label historically false.

The point of the label is not accuracy. Its point is reversal. The state created after the destruction of European Jewry is recast as the heir to the regime that tried to annihilate the Jews. The phrase therefore performs Holocaust inversion: it takes the central trauma of modern Jewish history and turns it against Jewish sovereignty.

COMMON USAGE

The label appears in protest signs, online graphics, graffiti, cartoons, placards, and boycott materials. It often comes with swastikas drawn onto Israeli flags, Hitler mustaches placed on Israeli leaders, SS imagery, camp imagery, or slogans describing Gaza as Auschwitz. These images are not explanatory. They are designed to shock, shame, and isolate.

The International Holocaust Remembrance Alliance working definition gives this pattern a precise place in contemporary antisemitism: examples may include comparing contemporary Israeli policy to Nazi policy, depending on context. The European Institute for the Study of Contemporary Antisemitism's 2009 report on the "Nazi card" similarly defined the problem as the use of Nazi terms or symbols in reference to Jews, Israel, Zionism, or aspects of Jewish experience.

The phrase became especially visible again after October 7, 2023. The Hamas-led massacre was followed by a wave of claims that Israel had become Nazi-like because of its war in Gaza. In that setting, the label did two things at once. It erased Hamas's crimes by shifting attention to Israel as the supposed new Third Reich, and it made Jewish grief after October 7 appear hypocritical or fraudulent.

The term also appears in official speech. Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan repeatedly used Hitler comparisons against Israeli leaders during the Gaza war. Those comparisons were reported by Reuters in 2023 and 2024. When a head of state uses Nazi analogy against Israel, the language moves beyond street protest into formal public conflict.

IMPLIED MEANING

Calling Israel a Nazi state implies that Israel's existence is not only disputed but monstrous. It asks the listener to place Israel outside ordinary relations. Trade, scholarship, travel, diplomacy, culture, and institutional exchange can then be treated as contact with fascism.

The label also implies that Jewish national power is uniquely corrupting. Jews were once victims of Nazi race law, ghettos, deportations, and extermination centers. The Nazi-state slogan says that, once armed and sovereign, Jews reproduced the evil that targeted them. This is why the phrase wounds beyond ordinary insult. It does not only condemn Israel. It tells Jews that their survival project became the thing that murdered their families.

It also collapses categories that must remain separate. Civilian deaths in war are not death camps. Military occupation is not the Nuremberg Laws. Border control is not industrial extermination. Harsh or unlawful conduct by a state is not proof that the state is Nazi Germany. A serious case must identify the act, the rule, the evidence, and the responsible actor. The Nazi-state slogan avoids that work by spending the stored horror of the Holocaust.

The result is a permission system. If Israel is Nazi Germany, then those who support Israel's existence can be treated as fascist sympathizers. Jewish students, synagogues, Hillels, artists, scholars, donors, and community organizations can then be pressured, excluded, or threatened under the claim that they are aligned with Nazism. The label does not stay in the Middle East. It follows Jews into civic life.

ORIGIN / LINEAGE

Nazi comparison in anti-Israel speech did not begin after October 7. It became a central feature of Cold War anti-Zionist language after Israel's 1967 victory over Soviet-backed Arab states. Soviet propaganda needed to discredit Zionism among postcolonial and Western left audiences. One method was to equate Zionism with racism, fascism, imperialism, and Nazism.

Yuri Ivanov's Caution: Zionism!, published in English by Progress Publishers in 1970, presented Zionism as a reactionary global force and linked it to fascism. Soviet and allied propaganda cast Israel as racist, colonial, and Nazi-like while avoiding open religious language against Jews. UN General Assembly Resolution 3379 in 1975 then gave one part of this campaign global prestige by declaring Zionism a form of racism and racial discrimination. The resolution was revoked in 1991, but the vocabulary survived.

Izabella Tabarovsky has traced how Soviet anti-Zionism borrowed from tsarist and Nazi antisemitic material, adapted it to Marxist-Leninist language, and exported it through state publishing and international networks. In that lineage, the Nazi comparison did not operate as careful history. It operated as role reversal. Jews became Nazis. Zionism became racism. Israel became the new embodiment of the evil from which Jews had barely survived.

Israeli flags fused with swastikas, Gaza compared to concentration camps, and Jewish institutions described as fascist all turn a specific conflict into a symbolic theater. The details change. The reversal stays.

FUNCTION

“Nazi State” functions as a disqualification label. Once accepted, Israel no longer has to be debated as a real country facing real enemies, real failures, real duties, and real security threats. It is placed in the category of ultimate evil. That placement does the work.

The phrase also makes nuance sound obscene. If Israel is Nazi Germany, then asking about Hamas tunnels, hostage-taking, rocket fire, human shields, civilian evacuation, proportionality, or peace proposals can be treated as evasion. The label trains audiences to resent context.

It also helps boycott and exclusion campaigns. Cooperation with Israelis can be redescribed as normalization with fascism. Jewish institutions can be treated as suspect if they defend Israel's existence. Israeli scholars, artists, athletes, and speakers can be made untouchable. The label creates the atmosphere in which social punishment feels deserved.

The phrase's deepest function is humiliation. It uses Jewish memory against Jews. It takes the name of the regime that destroyed European Jewry and attaches it to the Jewish state. The effect is not only to criticize Israel, but to degrade the meaning of the Holocaust and to make Jewish grief appear fraudulent.

NOTES

1. The IHRA working definition does not say that all criticism of Israel is antisemitic. It gives examples that may be antisemitic depending on context, including comparisons of contemporary Israeli policy to Nazi policy.

2. The EISCA "Nazi card" report is useful because it treats Nazi analogy as a pattern of words, images, and symbols used against Jews, Israel, Zionism, or Jewish experience. It also recognizes that criticism of Israeli policy can be expressed strongly without Nazi comparison.

3. Nazi Germany was not simply a harsh state. It was a racial dictatorship that destroyed democracy, stripped Jews of citizenship, waged aggressive war, and carried out genocide. Any comparison to it has to meet an extraordinary burden.

4. Israel can be criticized without Nazi analogy. One can criticize settlement, ministers, military decisions, coalition politics, judicial disputes, religious coercion, police conduct, or treatment of Palestinians without calling the country Nazi Germany.

5. The strongest objection to the label is not that Israel is beyond criticism. The strongest objection is that the label falsifies Nazi history, wounds Jewish memory, and makes relations with Israel appear equivalent to collaboration with fascism.

RESPONSES / CRITICISMS

It uses the word Nazi as a substitute for proof.

Nazi Germany was a racial dictatorship committed to conquest and genocide. Israel is a flawed, embattled, democratic country with elections, courts, opposition parties, Arab citizens, public dissent, and severe security threats. Criticism of Israel can be harsh and still remain serious. Calling it a Nazi state avoids the burden of making a case.

It punishes Jews with their own catastrophe.

The phrase takes the Holocaust and turns it against the people most marked by it. It tells Jews that the state built in the shadow of their near-destruction has become the heir to the destroyer. That is why the comparison is not only offensive. It is a reversal of historical memory.

It makes normal contact with Israel seem forbidden.

If Israel is Nazi Germany, then trade, diplomacy, study abroad, academic partnership, cultural exchange, and public defense of Israel become collaboration with fascism. That is not a path to better policy. It is a path to quarantine.

It erases the actual Nazis.

The Holocaust was a specific event carried out by a specific group of people with specific laws, institutions, camps, armies, collaborators, and victims. Turning every hated state into Nazi Germany empties the word of meaning. In the Israel case, it does something worse: it uses the crime against the Jews to delegitimize the Jews' own state.
